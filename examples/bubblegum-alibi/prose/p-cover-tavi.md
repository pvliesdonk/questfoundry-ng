"Here's what happened," I say again, and I hear the number I never counted come out of my mouth like it's the truth I've been holding all night. "Lights went at eleven-forty, right when the finale hit. Twenty-two seconds. That's the window." I say it the way I'd caption a homecoming photo, easy and certain, everybody smile. It is not eleven-forty. It was earlier and I know it was earlier because I was counting through the dark and the count started before the beat dropped, before the finale that Tavi cued, and the gap between the number I say and the number I felt is the whole crime folded small and slid under my tongue.

Tavi is beside me. I don't look but I feel the laptop bag shift, the strap she never lets go of, and then her hand finds mine down where the circle can't see, and she grips. Hard. A whole conversation in one squeeze. *Thank you. Don't. I know. Me too.* I grip back before I can decide not to, and that's the moment it welds, whatever we are now, the two of us stitched into the same lie with no seam anybody can find. We made a yearbook together. We kept everyone's secrets in it, alphabetized. This is just one more, except this one has a body under it and I'm the one who filed it wrong on purpose.

The circle exhales. They believe me. Of course they believe me. I'm the girl who keeps the record straight, and I just handed them a straight one, ruled and clean, and it's a forgery.

But here's what a lie does that the truth doesn't. The truth is a door. My lie is a fog, and everybody in this ragged pastel ring is already wading into it. I watch them do it. I watch Devon narrow his eyes at the window where the sky's gone gray at the edges, doing his own math, deciding those twenty-two seconds are big enough to hold whatever he needs them to hold. I watch a sophomore whose name I'd have to check my own pages for edge half a step toward Mona, then away, testing. The dark I invented is real now, an open plot everyone can plant a body in, and every single one of them is reaching for a shovel.

That's the toll. Not the lie itself. The way a fogged minute is a room with no walls, and anyone frightened enough will build the walls wherever it saves them, and the person they wall in doesn't get a vote.

Tavi's thumb moves once against my knuckles. Steady. Safe. I keep my bright face on, the group-shot face, and I feel the fear underneath it like a second heartbeat, because I bought her the dark and I don't yet know what it's going to cost the next person somebody decides to leave standing in it, alone, while the doors stay chained and the glitter keeps ticking down.
