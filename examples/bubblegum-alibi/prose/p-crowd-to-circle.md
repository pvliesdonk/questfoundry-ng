I turn to face it, and the room does the thing a room does when it has decided you are in charge of the ending. The chatter tightens all at once, a drawstring pulled, and then it's just breath and the disco ball ticking its soft glitter over everyone's shoulders. The circle finds its shape around me. Not a neat one. A ragged one, pastel and uneven, elbows and borrowed ties and one boy still holding a punch cup he's forgotten about. Streamers sag low overhead, pink and gold gone tired, drooping like they've been up as long as we have.

And the eyes. All of them, on me now, not on Mona. That's the part that catches in my throat. I built the circle to point at her and instead it swiveled a quarter-turn and landed on the girl who knows things, the girl with the lanyards in her pockets and the reputation for keeping the record straight. They want an answer. They think I have one because I always seem to, because I caption every disaster and make it sit still in a frame. Marilee dead, doors chained, dawn coming, and here's Juno Park, who cataloged this whole school like it was a specimen, please tell us who to hate before the sun does it for us.

So I count. Old habit, the one I fall into during any dark. One. Two. The seconds are the only steady thing in the gym and I let them keep me upright. Three. Somebody coughs. Four. My dead phone presses its cold nothing against my palm and I let it go.

Here is what I know and can't unknow: the twenty-two seconds have a shape inside them, and the shape sits on a timeline, and the timeline is only as clean as the moment the lights went out. That's the loose thread in the whole sweater. When did the dark start. Who made it start. Because Mona saw hands at the sash, yes, but the *when* of it decides everything, decides whether her picture is the true one or just the first one somebody handed the room.

And I know when the lights went out. I know it in my counting bones, because I was counting then too, and the number didn't match the number I let everyone believe. Somebody cut them early. Somebody I would burn the whole gym down to protect.

"Okay," I say, and my voice comes out bright, God help me, the same peppy lilt I use for group shots. "Here's what happened." The circle leans. This is the moment. I can hold the timeline shut, keep it clean and cruel and pointed exactly where I aimed it, or I can pry it open and let the early cut fall out where everyone can see whose hand was on the switch. Either way I'm the one talking, and either way somebody I love pays the toll for the sentence I'm about to finish.
