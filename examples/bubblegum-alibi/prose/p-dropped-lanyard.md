The lanyard's still swinging when I crouch, and I catch it before it can settle into being just floor litter. Punch has glued it to the tile, that neon-red fruit stuff Beau spiked before the doors chained, and it comes up tacky, leaving a bright kiss-print behind. I turn it over in the graying light like I'm cataloging it for a spread nobody will ever want in a yearbook. Faculty issue. The kind Principal Okafor makes chaperones wear so we know who's allowed to tell us no.

Only this one's snapped. Not worn-thin, not chewed loose at the clip the way mine gets by June. Snapped, the plastic clean-broken and pale at the fracture, the way something gives when a hand closes on it and forgets to open again. Somebody wore this around their neck and then, in the dark, gripped it hard enough to break it off themselves. I press my thumb to the split edge and it bites back, still sharp. Fresh.

I want it to mean nothing. I want it to be a decoration that lost a fight with gravity. But I count the seconds again the way I counted them under the blackout, and I lay the little facts down flat: the lanyard is here, near the sailor's table, where the heel-marks point, where the sash-knot lives in only three sets of hands. Someone stood in this exact glitter-strewn spot, close enough to touch, and needed both fists so badly for something else that they crushed the one thing telling the room they belonged.

Who takes a lanyard off in a panic. Who forgets they're wearing it. I turn it and turn it, hunting for a name written on the back in Sharpie the way we all label our stuff so it comes home, and there's a smear where a name should be, punch-blurred into a pink ghost, and I hate that my first feeling is relief, because relief means I was afraid of whose name it would be.

The brightness of it is the worst part. Cotton-candy lanyard, cotton-candy sash, a whole night color-coordinated for a girl who wanted the room to look up and got exactly that. I keep my smile bolted on out of pure habit, cataloging, captioning, the way I've captioned every ruin I've ever been trusted with, and the caption my brain writes is the last thing I have any business writing: someone was right here, and they were terrified, and terror this close to the body is not a thing I can flip past.

I slide the broken lanyard into my pocket beside the ledger, one more secret pressed against my ribs, and the two of them touch like they know each other. The windows keep graying. The doors keep not opening. And I crouch there in the punch-sticky dark holding proof of somebody's shaking hands, understanding that whatever this snapped clip remembers, I'm the one who has to make it talk before the room decides it already knows the answer.
