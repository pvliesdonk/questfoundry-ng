"The lights went out at the wrong time," I say, and I say it to the whole ragged pastel circle, not to her, because if I say it to her I'll lose my nerve. "The finale cut was supposed to hit at midnight sharp. That's the number we all remember, that's the number we've been building on. But it didn't. It hit early. Eight seconds early, maybe nine." My voice stays bright. That's the terrible part. I've captioned so many disasters my mouth doesn't know how to stop being cheerful. "I know because I was counting. I always count in the dark. It's a thing I do. And the number in my bones doesn't match the number in the story."

The room does the math slower than I do, but it does it. I watch the loose thread pull taut. If the dark started early, then the hands Mona saw at the sash were already working before anyone thinks they were, and the muddled window everyone's been squinting through snaps shut, clean, and the timeline stops sliding around under our feet and just sits there. Fixed. Provable. A shape you can point at.

And everyone's face turns, slow as the disco ball, one quarter-turn away from the middle of the room and toward the DJ table. Toward the girl with the headphones around her neck and the laptop bag she won't set down.

"Tavi ran the lights," I say, because they'd get there anyway now, and better my voice than the mob's. "Tavi cut them early."

I don't look at her while I do it. I can't. I feel her instead. I feel the exact second the warmth at my elbow goes to nothing, the way heat leaves a room when a door opens onto morning. She doesn't gasp. She doesn't argue. She just clamps that laptop bag to her side like a life vest, the way she does when she's cornered, when the blackout comes up and she flinches, and I've seen her do it all night and told myself it meant nothing, told myself it meant the same thing my own folded arms mean, which is *I'm fine, are you fine, aren't we all so brave.*

Then she turns around. Not fast. Not a scene. She just gives me her back, the glow tape on her sneakers flaring once as she pivots, and she faces the wall of streamers gone tired and pink, and she stops being on my side of the room.

The timeline holds. That's the thing I wanted and now I have it, whole and firm and cruel, the crucial minutes locked into place where nobody can bend them. I built a frame and made the disaster sit still inside it. Congratulations to me, the girl who keeps the record straight.

But the record straightened right through the middle of the one person who came to this gym as mine. My co-conspirator. The other half of every inside joke in the yearbook we'll never finish. I traded her for the truth and the truth doesn't hug back, doesn't laugh too high near the punch table, doesn't hold my hand while the sky grins gray.

She won't look at me again tonight. I already know it, in my counting bones. So I stand in the cold she left behind, and I count.
