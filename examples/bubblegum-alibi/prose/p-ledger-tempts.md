"Show us," Priya says again, and her palm doesn't waver, because Priya's palms never waver. She's spent all night running the room the way she runs everything, headset dead around her neck, clipboard tucked under one arm like a shield, and the terrible competence of her is that she's *right*. It would be fair. A ledger against a rumor. Names against a lean. She's asking me to make the morning tidy, and I want to hate her for it, except I can't, because tidy is exactly what I built and forgot to want.

My free hand is already inside my cardigan, thumb finding the little wheel of the lighter I lifted off Devon three hours and one body ago. The ledger sits against my ribs where it always sits, warm from me, all those pages of everyone's worst mornings pressed to my heartbeat like it belongs there. Best Smile bit her nails to blood over a thing on page nine. Mona's whole quiet ruin lives on page eleven. And somewhere past that, in my own handwriting, cheerful and neat, is a name I'd swallow the whole notebook to keep unsaid.

That's the math Priya doesn't have. She thinks it's one truth for one killer. It's dozens. It's every kid in this circle handed to the principal gift-wrapped in ink, their belonging revoked at dawn because I couldn't keep my captions to myself. The truth in here can prove the thing. It just detonates forty other things on the way to proving it.

Tavi's fingers tighten in mine. She doesn't know what I'm holding. She thinks she does. Steady, steady, that lie we keep making at each other.

I thumb the lighter open under my cardigan where nobody can see, and the little wheel bites my skin, and I think: how bright. How quick. A utility sink and a flame and all these secrets gone up sweet, and nobody walks out ruined, and nobody walks out proven, and the morning becomes a thing of *who do you believe* instead of *here it is in her own hand.* Mercy, or a mess. I can't tell which is which anymore and that's the joke that won't stop costing.

Then the doors go. Not open. Just tried. A key scrapes the lock outside the chain, metal on metal, once, twice, and the whole circle flinches toward the sound like moths, and I know that scrape. I've heard it every morning of my life. Keys on a lanyard, principal's ring, the fat brass one for the gym.

Minutes. Maybe fewer. Then the chain comes off and the dawn comes in and somebody has to have a name in their mouth by the time it does, and it's my mouth, it's always been my mouth, keeper of everyone, and the number on the wall clock is the last one I get to make up.

Priya's hand is still out. Patient. Waiting for the passed note. "Juno," she says, soft now, almost kind. "Whoever it is. Let it be fair."

The wheel is under my thumb. The flame is a flick away. So is the truth. I only get one.
