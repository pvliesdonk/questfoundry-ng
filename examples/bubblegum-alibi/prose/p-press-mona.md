"They already know something's wrong with you," I tell her, and I watch it land. "You came in overdressed to a room that dropped you. They can smell the wanting on you, Mona. The only question is what I say next, and how loud."

It is the cruelest sentence I have ever arranged, and I arrange it beautifully, the way I caption a photo of someone crying so it reads as *candid* instead of *destroyed*. I keep my voice bright. I keep it kind, the tone I use for the kids who need coaxing to smile. Underneath, my dead phone is a stone in my pocket and I keep reaching for it anyway, thumb to a black screen that gives me nothing back.

She breaks the way a dropped ornament breaks, all at once, silver everywhere. "Fine. *Fine.* I saw someone." Her hands go to her face and smear the black rivers wider. "In the dark. Right after the lights went, before the music came back, there was somebody at the sash. Standing over it. Hands moving." She mimes it without meaning to, fingers looping air, the muscle memory of a hundred rehearsals crooking her wrists into the exact shape of the knot. "I couldn't see a face. I swear on anything, I couldn't. But somebody was *there*, Juno, somebody who knew where to stand."

And there it is. The gap nobody could fill, filled. Twenty-two seconds of dark with a body in them, and now a figure inside the dark, hands working the pink cotton while the room held its breath for a spotlight that was already too late. It is a hard fact. It is the first hard thing I have touched all night, and I want to be glad, and I am, and it curdles instantly, because I bought it with her.

The demotion is out. I said it too loud, or the room has ears the way rooms do, or Bree three feet away has been leaning into our little conference like it's the last dance. It doesn't matter which. It spreads. *She got cut this morning. Marilee cut her. She had a reason. She tied the knot in rehearsals, didn't she, she'd know how.* The words move through them like a current finding water, and the current is relief, ugly and grateful, because a scared crowd wants a shape to point at more than it wants the truth.

Mona feels it turn. I watch her feel it. She looks at me first, not at them, because she still thinks I keep everyone safe, because I let her think that this morning when I logged her ruin in my ledger and said nothing kind. The faces swing toward her, one by one, pastel and hungry under the disco ball, and she shrinks into the drinks table like she could fold small enough to disappear.

"Juno," she says, and it isn't a question anymore. It's the ledger closing on her fingers. "You said you always know what to do with people."

I do. That's the terrible part. I always do.
