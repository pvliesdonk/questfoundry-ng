The neon over the trophy case buzzes like it's chewing on something. It's been doing that all night, this low electric worry, the *P* in CHAMPIONS half-dead and stuttering, and I keep catching myself listening to it instead of to the people, because the people are all saying the same thing now, and the neon at least is honest about being broken.

Twenty-two seconds. My gift to the room. I watch it work.

Here's the thing nobody tells you about a gap. It doesn't sit still. It's not a hole in the floor everyone steps around politely. It's a soft dark spot, and the frightened lean toward it the way you'd lean toward a warm vent in January, and I can see them doing it, every one of them, tilting their bodies at the place where the minutes went missing. Devon leans. The sophomore leans. Even Mona, who has more reason than anyone to want the dark left alone, leans, because when nobody can pin a thing down, everybody gets to be right about it for a little while, and being right feels like being safe.

It is not safe. I know exactly how not-safe it is because I built the room they're all standing in.

And the not-knowing is the worst part, worse than a scream, because a scream ends. This just holds. The whole circle is one held breath, pastel and sequined and gone gray under the fluorescents, waiting to find out which of us the dark decides to keep. I catalog them the way I'd catalog a page. Best Smile, chewing her thumbnail bloody. Most Likely to Succeed, already rehearsing an alibi behind his teeth. I know their faces so well I could draw them from memory, and right now every single face is doing the same small thing, the lean, the tilt, the reach toward the gap I opened with a number I made up, and I want to yell at them to stop, don't, that's not a room, there's no floor, but I'm the one who told them it was solid.

Tavi's fingers are still laced through mine down where the light can't reach. Steady. She thinks steady is the same as safe. I let her think it.

The neon stutters again. Somebody laughs, too high, and it dies fast. The sky at the window is the color of an old bruise going yellow at the edges, which means dawn, which means doors, which means somebody has to walk out of here first and I don't yet know who, and the not-knowing sits on my chest like a whole yearbook stacked there, every page a secret I swore I'd never spend.

Then Priya turns. She's been quiet in the corner this whole time, watchful, and when she speaks it's not to the circle. It's to me. "Juno." Just my name, and then her hand comes out, palm up, patient as a teacher waiting for a passed note. "You've got it. You've always got it. Show us."

She means the ledger. Of course she means the ledger.
