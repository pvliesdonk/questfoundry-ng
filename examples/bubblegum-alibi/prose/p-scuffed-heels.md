The scuffs are the prettiest thing in the room, which is the worst possible thing for them to be. They shimmer. Somebody dragged a heel through the sugar-dust of a thousand spilled cupcakes and the fallen glitter, and the marks catch the gray window-light in little arcs, one after another, a dotted line drawn by a hand in a hurry. I crouch. I keep my camera pressed to my chest so it won't swing and tap and announce me. Click, in my head only, because I don't dare take the picture that would make this real for anyone but me.

First scuff. It points away from the dance floor, away from the slow-turning center where nobody's cheering now, where the crowd has folded into knots of held breath and one girl sobbing in hiccups near the punch table. Second scuff, a longer skid, like the heel caught and the ankle rolled. Third. They're getting deeper. Whoever made these was moving fast and off-balance and not caring who saw, or maybe caring exactly, betting that in a room this loud and this drunk on tradition nobody counts the seconds the way I do.

I count them anyway. Four. Five. The trail bends past Beau's abandoned letterman jacket, past a fallen streamer curled like a comma, and I follow it the way I've followed everything my whole polite career, one favor at a time, telling myself I'm just documenting, just keeping the record neat, just being everyone's soft place, and the lie is so worn and comfortable I could almost sleep in it.

Six. The scuffs drag toward the costume corner, where the racks stand crammed with sequined nothings and the sailor's table waits with its little brass anchor buttons scattered like teeth. That snapped lanyard is still swaying. It has no business swaying this long. Somebody touched it a minute ago, maybe two, maybe in the exact dark I was busy counting through, and my stomach does the thing it's been doing since the lights came back, that cold peppy freefall, glitter over the drop.

The marks point home. Of course they point home. They always were going to point at the corner I've been trying not to look at, at the girl who came off that stage a half-beat late with silver in her hair, and I hate the trail for being so bright and so tidy, so easy to read, so much like a caption I'd write myself. Here she is. Best Smile. Most Likely to Break My Heart Doing Something I Can't Cover For.

Dawn is filling the windows like water rising in a sink. My knees ache from crouching over the shine. I could smudge these with one sneaker and no one would know. I could. My foot even lifts.

Instead I press two fingers to the last scuff, feel the grit of it, and let it pull me the rest of the way in, because if I don't read the blackout myself, someone else will read it wrong, and wrong might be worse. Or wrong might be the only mercy left. I trace the glitter toward the tangle where the dark went, and I go.
