Here's the thing about leverage. It only works if you're willing to spend it, and I've spent my whole life saving.

Mona's grip on my sleeve is not a threat. It's a prayer with fingernails. She's asking me to be the person she thinks I am, the one who knows what to do with people, and the terrible joke is that I do know. I know exactly what her demotion is worth. Cut from the circle at dawn, near the body in the dark, hands that could tie that knot in her sleep. I could hold all of it up to the light and watch the room turn toward her like sunflowers toward slaughter. They want a target so badly the walls are practically leaning in.

I look at her ruined mascara, at the two black rivers she keeps making worse, and I think: she's already been executed once today. Quiet and clean. And I decide I won't be the second blade.

"Nobody's going to hear it from me," I say. "The cut. The circle. None of it. As far as this room knows, you were always exactly where you're supposed to be."

Relief hits her like weather. Her whole body unclenches, and for one bright second I've done a kind thing, a good thing, the sort of caption I'd write proud. *Mona Vale, safe.* Then I feel the cost arrive right behind it, on schedule, the way costs always do. Because she believes me now. She believes I'll keep her, and so she'll never tell me what she saw, and I'll never ask, because asking would mean pulling the thread I just promised to leave alone.

Her hands drift up to fix her face, and I watch them, and I catch it: the crook of her fingers, the loop and pull, that small rehearsed shape muscle memory makes when the mind's gone somewhere darker. A hundred rehearsals live in those hands. Whatever she watched happen near that pink cotton knot in the twenty-two seconds nobody counted, it's folded up inside her now, and I've just handed her the key and told her to swallow it.

"Thank you," she whispers, and it's wet and real and worse than any accusation. "I knew you'd know what to do."

She stands. She smooths the overdressed dress. And she fades back into the crowd the way she's always wanted to, unremarkable, belonging, one more pastel body under the disco ball, and the last person who knew the sash intimately goes quiet right in front of me and takes the truth with her.

The sky in the high windows is getting ideas. Grey creeping in at the edges of the black. I've got a body, a knot, a blackout that came early, and now a witness I chose to bury, and my reconstruction just lost a corner it can't get back.

I step back into the hush I paid for. It's very quiet in here. I start counting anyway, because I always count, and the number I land on is the number of people I can still lose.
