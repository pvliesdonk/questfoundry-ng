Audit-lanterns hiss along the damp stilts. Their blue fire beads on chain and nail. The curfew ribbon rides my pulse and pricks at each swing of my hand. Sanctioned ink fumes the wet and finds my neck, close as a whisper taught in the counting-halls. The street I fixed holds thin and white between the pilings. Bells breathe. Oars mutter and backwater gleams. People turn where the auditors point, eyes lowered, burdened, patient the way a tide is patient until it breaks.

A watcher falls in at my shoulder, coat wet to the knees. “Keep them to the rails.” His voice is paper-dry. I nod. The map warms under my palm. The clerk’s red stitch lies snug down the vellum’s spine and shows where the night will shear at dawn. Keep left, the map says, faint and salt-bitten. One file. Do not call. I walk the countersigned edge and the lanterns follow my line, hissing approval, measuring me as they measure fog. The smell of ink thickens at the turn where a guttersill gives to a door barred with rope and copper teeth.

Inside, lamplight presses hard and flat. A bowl waits on a low plank, brine and black folded together, the mark riding the surface like breath on glass. A scribe eases back to the wall. “For tally,” he says. “If you would.” He does not look at me when he speaks. I hear the canal in the wood. I hear my mother’s name the way you hear rope strain in a storm, all fiber and ache. The curfew writ bites again at my wrist, impatient, a small law asking for a larger one. Name, the map says, austere as a tide-gate. Hold tight.

Duty crowds the threshold. Inheritance stands beside it, salt-eyed, asking the same price in a different tone. I set the map flat, my thumb damp with canal. The bowl’s rim is cold and pebbled from old chips. Ink clocks against the wood with each breath I take. I lift my hand over the surface where the mark waits, bright as a fish under thin ice.
