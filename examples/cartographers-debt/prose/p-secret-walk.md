I wrap the vellum in oilcloth and slip it under my coat. The flood-lantern rides high on the strap with its shutter pinched near shut. A tap on a lintel. A word through a slat. Lanterns bloom one by one, weak as minnows. A knot gathers. Rope-worker. Mudlark. Widow with her hem pinned. No writ. No clerk.

Keep left, the map whispers. One file. Do not call.

We take the lock-steps down where the stilts rake the sky. I knock my knuckles twice on the first piling and breathe us into it. One. Two. Walk. Kelp ropes lift in the wash and brush our knees. Pitch gleams like eyes. I count under my breath and they rock to it, lanterns tucked to ribs so the light doesn’t skate. Three. Hold. Slide.

A boy’s boot slips. My palm finds his collarbone clean as script. Weight settles. He nods once and fixes his toe in the silt seam I point. We move again. The cut narrows. Water kisses stone. I ease the lantern’s throat and reach to a piling where I chalked a salt-mark before the bell. A plain cross where the step dips. The woman with the pinned hem sees it flare pale, checks, and lifts. Her skirt hisses instead of drowning. We pass.

Tight. Quiet, the map insists, its voice like wet paper.

The wards breathe with us, slats whispering, chain complaining. We come up under a bridge-back where the cut shears off and turns to ordinary alley. I let the shutter open a finger. Faces soften. Fingers unhook from belts. They file to doorways and vanish like tide in reed.

By cockle dawn a runner goes dry-foot along the quay, trading a rumor like bait. Unwrit chain over the Bracken cut. Watchers step to the rail with their salt-blue lamps and thumb the margins of their books. They look for my beat between shutters, the pace of my counts, the way my light leans at a sink. On the black water my last glow thins to a reed of amber, and I pinch it dark before they finish their sums.
