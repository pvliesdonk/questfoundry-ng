I lift the bowl and shoulder the door. Canal air breaks cold across my face. The mark in the brine brightens, thin as a gill-flash. I tip. The black goes over and takes it. A hiss like surf against stone. “Denied,” I say to the water, and the word holds.

Back inside, I set the vellum flat and hook my nail under the red stitch the clerk laid down the spine. It parts with a small, felt sound. The map shivers. Ink beads, retreats, runs. The Ledger’s pale rules unmoor, their blue pricks thinning to wet ghosts and then gone. New lines plait from gutter to plank to rail, a braid that will not take a comb. The compass rose blacks deeper and turns once, slow as a tide-clock. Unbound, the map whispers, paper-dry. Keep waterwise.

Outside the sanctioned street loosens. Lamps at the posts hold steady, but only where the countersign still lives. Beyond, the Bracken goes to its old habits. A cart-sill slips and scrapes a shin raw. A rush pushes across a blind bridge-back and takes a hat. A boy cries once and is hauled up by a rope-woman with tar on her hands, cursing the curfew that left that stretch dark. Watchers stack at corners with blue fire steady at their hips, faces damp and exact. They look for a wayfinder without a writ. They look for me.

I slide the lantern’s shutter to a blade and keep it low. One. Two. Three. I breathe the last number into the rail and move when the wind claps chain against chain. I know the slat that bellies under a heavy step. I know the stink-eddy that means a surge will slap the pilings. The map’s wet logic hums under my palm, private as grief. No countersign, it says, austere as a gate. Keep to memory.

So I do. I take who I can under the narrow light and the old runs my mother taught me and the ones I cut myself when the city turned its face. The posted lanes fall away like a tide pulling silk from nails. The blue lamps keep their rule. My little lantern works against the dark, small and stubborn, a held ember walking the edge of the water.
