The cousin gestured to the men who had waited in the passage beyond the east arch. They entered with tools wrapped in cloth that bore the stains of previous removals. Their boots tracked meltwater across the parquet in straight lines that pointed toward the walls.

Helena stepped back from the settle. The seventeen heirs dispersed toward the perimeter with the automatic coordination of a troupe executing a poorly rehearsed exit. Marion Ashworth replaced her pince-nez and moved toward the west window, her kid gloves leaving no mark on the frost-coated glass. Teddy Bryant remained near the escritoire, his hands held before him at the precise angle of a clerk awaiting signature. The frayed damask of his borrowed coat caught the light from the empty grate.

The men approached the mahogany paneling. They worked with the efficiency of coroners stripping a body of valuables, prying with iron bars that left gouges in the plaster. The first section of wood groaned and separated from the wall with a sound like a ledger tearing. They lowered it toward the sleds waiting in the snow beyond the service entrance, the sleds that had carried the cousin and now waited to carry away the house itself.

Helena watched the paneling descend. The empty fireplace held only ash now, the carbonized remains of Bernard's alphabetical divisions that might have purchased delay, might have constructed defenses from exposure, had they not been fed to the flames. The ash lay undisturbed in the grate, gray and fine as archival dust, testament to secrets now sealed with the deceased beneath the frozen ground. She understood that the time for leverage had ended with the burning.

The men returned for the next section. The heirs stood against the walls as the room's architecture was dismantled around them. The oxidized silver of the ruined chandelier caught the light from the falling snow, and the mahogany groaned toward the waiting sleds. The estate's order dissolved into the systematic plunder of fixtures, each piece inventoried and valued for liquidation. Helena stood beside the empty grate and watched the ash settle.
