Helena's hand remained at her throat. The tarnished silver of her bracelet held the dim light. She lowered the limb with the deliberation of a clerk closing a ledger that had balanced against her.

The seventeen heirs had clustered toward the room's center. A gentleman knelt to retrieve the shattered pocket watch; his fingers cut themselves on the crystal fragments scattered across the marble. No one spoke. The silence held the weight of obsolete account books stacked in a cellar vault.

Helena moved toward the escritoire. The emerald velvet of her glove brushed the portfolio where Marion's fingers had left their mark. She opened the leather case. Inside, cramped handwriting covered fourteen months of assignation dates on creased foolscap. She withdrew the bundle and placed it upon the escritoire's surface, spreading the pages with the methodical gesture of a mortician arranging limbs for viewing. The foolscap crackled against the marble.

"Read these," Helena said.

Her voice carried the resonance that Marion's review had declared obsolete. The syllables did not waver. Marion stood beside the escritoire, her pince-nez settled against the bridge of her nose, her posture maintaining the lines of administrative precision. Her fingers twitched toward the bell cord and arrested there. The wire frames tightened against her skin.

One of the heirs—a lady in watered silk—stepped forward and took the topmost letter. Her eyes tracked the solicitor's script. Her mouth opened, then closed. She passed the page to the gentleman beside her, whose cuffs bore the stains of cellar grease. He read, and his hands trembled.

Marion's breath emerged as a sharp intake, the sound Helena recognized from a hundred opening nights when the critic prepared to deliver devastation. This time, the gesture preceded no judgment. Marion's fingers rose to her pince-nez and removed them. She placed the spectacles upon the escritoire beside her review and the love letters. The lenses caught the light and held two frozen reflections of the room's dissolution.

The heirs stepped back from the escritoire as if from a corpse newly discovered. The documentation lay spread among them like a contagion, the ink recording assignations in the solicitor's precise hand rendering Marion's marriage and Helena's performance equally obsolete. The assembly had lost its structure of restraint.
