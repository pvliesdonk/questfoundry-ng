The roads cleared by noon. Constables entered through the vestibule; their boots tracked grey residue across the marble tiles. They carried irons that had tarnished to a dull oxide from disuse. Dr. Mott maintained her posture with her arms secured behind her, her fingers numb against her spine. The lead constable consulted a sheet from his breast pocket and recited the charge regarding the patriarch's death three days prior. His breath rose visible in the chilled air, dissipating against the cracked plaster.

She did not move. The constable approached with the irons. The metal clicked shut around her wrists, colder than the brass fixtures of her medical bag had ever been. The weight pulled her shoulders forward and constrained her gesture. She observed that Teddy Bryant had stepped away from the mantelpiece. His grease-marked cuffs hung at his sides, and his shoulders had lost their rigid squareness toward the assembly.

The constables escorted her toward the vestibule. The heirs parted without speaking, their faces turned toward her with the rigid propriety of those who had demanded legal accountability. As the door sealed against the grey daylight, she heard behind her the strike of metal against metal as the investigator removed Teddy's shackles. The sound of a pencil scratching against ledger paper carried through the silence, correcting the record. She stepped over the threshold, her medical authority revoked by the irons, leaving the account of her violence closed and the archival dust undisturbed upon the settle beside Bernard's covered form.
