Marion brushed the ash from her cuffs with gloved precision. The kid leather had darkened since morning, stained first by the escritoire's tarnished braid and now by the carbon residue that had settled upon every surface in the drawing room, infiltrating the weave with a granular permanence. She brought her fingers to her face. The scent permeated the velvet curtains, a dry, alkaline intrusion that no amount of laundering would remove from the fibers, lingering in the cloth like a chemical accusation. The violence of the previous evening had found its permanent record in the weave, indelible as ink on foolscap.

She surveyed the adjacent Great Hall through the archway. Seventeen heirs had resumed their positions around the perimeter, their postures corrected to administrative neutrality, but their eyes tracked the smudge marks on her darkened sleeves. The oxidized silver armature of the fallen chandelier twisted across the Persian rug, contorted like a collapsed ledger spine. The fire had consumed Bernard's archive; the alphabetized divisions had oxidized into smoke, and the evidence of seventeen failures had risen through the flue. The secrets that poisoned the house were buried now with the butler's hands beneath the frozen garden's crust.

Marion adjusted her pince-nez. The household attempted to restore its polite order, straightening surfaces and righting inkwells, yet the combustion residue clung to the damask and the frost-locked windows. The cost remained visible in the frayed hems of gloves and the tremor in one's fingers when reaching for a bell cord. She smoothed her wool coat. The record stood, irreversible and absolute, marking the precise point at which the archive had surrendered to flame and the house accepted its new, unwritable chapter.
