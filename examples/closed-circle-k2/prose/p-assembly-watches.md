Helena Vance maintained her position beside the settle. The portfolio's leather corners pressed rigid lines against her ribs. She counted seventeen heirs assembled in the Great Hall, distributed between the dying fire and the frost-locked windows. The schism had assumed physical geography. Teddy Bryant occupied the room's center, his borrowed tweed still settling from his previous gesture. Marion Ashworth remained near the trestle, her fingers leaving ink transfers on the ledger's smudged columns. Dr. Mott hovered at the mantelpiece, her empty glass creating faint rings on the oak in three-second intervals.

Above them, the chandelier dripped. Each drop fell three meters from the frozen key's tip, striking the parquet with a sound like a stamp on obsolete account paper. The faction demanding action had positioned themselves beneath the drip, tilting their faces upward as if receiving benediction. The faction insisting on propriety had retreated to the damask walls, their gloved hands folded against frayed velvet seams. A man Helena recognized as the estate's distant cousin stepped forward. He stated that the ice required immediate fracture. A woman in oxidized silver jewelry countered that the will demanded natural thaw. No one moved to mediate.

The cost of their order manifested in small ruptures. The gentleman's pocket watch consulted seventeen times in as many minutes. The lady's teacup shattered against the hearthstone; the fragments remained unattended. The ice water pooling beneath the chandelier spread across the floor, creating a boundary neither faction would cross first. Helena shifted the portfolio. The documentation within maintained a weight independent of its physical mass. The assembly had ceased to be a gathering and had become a ledger page where survival and manners balanced in unequal columns.
