The constable closed his notebook. He stated that the entry in the cellar ledger placed Bernard elsewhere at the hour of death. He stated that the disturbed dust confirmed an alibi for the homicide. He stated that the matter of the accounts remained outside his jurisdiction. He looked toward the door.

Helena Vance stepped forward. Her emerald hem brushed the ash that had settled on the Persian weave. She stated that the estate could not retain a servant who had traded discretion for larceny. Her voice carried the particular crack of crystal under pressure. She extended her hand. She requested the livery.

Bernard removed the coat. The braid had tarnished from silver to grey at the cuffs. He folded it along the seams. He placed it on the escritoire where the ink had dried in dark pools. Seventeen heirs watched from the perimeter. No one spoke.

The wine thefts became known. A gentleman referenced the absent bottles between the dated racks. A lady noted the reduced levels in the port. Their voices remained low but their eyes moved to where Bernard stood in his shirtsleeves, mapping the inventory of his fourteen-year appropriation. His decades of service ended not with a settlement of accounts but with this catalog of missing stock recited in the freezing vault.

Bernard retrieved the portfolio from beneath the settle. The brass corners had collected frost from the marble. He arranged the strap across his shoulder and felt the weight of the alphabetized pages settle against his hip. He moved toward the door.

The storm had broken against the façade. Snow entered the vestibule in horizontal lines. Bernard stepped onto the drive. The wind removed the heat from his skin in seconds. He walked toward the gateposts that had marked the boundary since his employment began thirty-seven years prior. The archive knocked against his side with each step. The snow weighted his footfalls deeper into the drift, leaving tracks that the storm would erase before the thaw.
