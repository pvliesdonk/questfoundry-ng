The ledger in Bernard's hand recorded an eleven o'clock inspection. The patriarch had died in the cellars at eleven. Marion aligned the two facts in her mind. The butler had descended to the wine racks; the patriarch had died between them. Time placed Bernard at the scene of the murder, making him the primary suspect despite his own death.

They carried him through the corridor with the gravity of pallbearers. His shoes dragged across the threshold, leaving twin trails in the frost that filmed the floorboards. Marion followed Helena, whose gloves hung from her wrists like abandoned formalities. No one spoke. The procession served only to move the evidence from one chamber to the next, preserving the chain of custody for a court that would never convene.

The Great Hall enclosed them in its frozen stillness. The chandelier hung sheathed in ice, its crystal arms heavy with frozen water. The block containing the will stood upon the table, sweating in the fire's warmth, the document within still trapped behind opaque ice. They placed Bernard's body upon the settle with the same care they had shown the ice block, arranging both objects with precision. The heirs settled into postures of observance around the room, their faces turned toward the corpse and the obstruction with identical expressions of civilized dread. The ice had supplanted the magistrate; the frozen block had replaced the bar. Marion watched the assembly sink into the grotesque stasis, and understood that justice would remain suspended until the thaw, or until the killer among them chose to speak.
