Helena stood beside the escritoire. The emerald velvet of her sleeve had absorbed damp from the marble; the fabric clung to her wrist like a ledger page warped by cellar moisture. She did not open the portfolio. The seventeen heirs had ceased their circulation and now occupied fixed positions around the room's perimeter, each maintaining the distance of creditors who had called in incompatible debts. No one stood near Marion.

Marion remained at the settle's edge. Her left hand hung at her side, the third finger pale where a ring had rested until the previous hour. The skin retained the groove of the metal, a tan line precise as an entry in a bankruptcy ledger. She did not cover it. The heirs had seen the gesture when she removed the band and placed it upon the escritoire's corner, where it now sat beside the foolscap containing dates and solicitor's fees from Pimlico.

Helena adjusted her glove. The frayed hem of her gown brushed against the Persian rug's ash trail, disturbing Bernard's last path toward the hearth. She had not moved from the documents since the whispers completed their circuit. The review lay exposed now, no longer unpublished, its ink fixed upon the page with the permanence of a copperplate engraving. The heirs had read the critique of her final performance; their postures confirmed the assessment's accuracy. She observed their averted gazes and recognized the calculation of diminished assets.

Marion turned toward the window. The frost had thickened since morning, sealing the glass with the opacity of filed accounts. She touched her empty finger and withdrew her hand. The gesture conveyed the termination of her marriage with the bureaucratic finality of a discharged lease. The heirs understood; their silence acknowledged the collateral damage of revealed truth. Neither woman possessed leverage sufficient to compel the other's discretion now. The portfolios remained open, their contents mutually assured destruction rendered obsolete by prior detonation.

The room settled into isolation. Helena remained beside the escritoire. Marion remained at the settle. Seventeen heirs occupied the interval between them, preserving the distance of parties to a dissolved partnership. The chandelier's oxidized silver armature glinted from the rug where it had fallen, a structure of tarnished metal supporting nothing. The cold had ceased to be an atmospheric condition and had become the house's permanent administrative policy. Both women stood in the frozen archive of their reputations, inventorying the ruins of candor.
