Helena Vance did not move from the settle. The portfolio's leather corners maintained their pressure against her ribs. Seventeen heirs stood in the vault of the Great Hall, arranged between the grate's final embers and the windows sealed with interior ice. Each exhalation rose in discrete columns, visible and white, merging above the parquet in a single cloud that fractured and reformed with the rhythm of their breathing. The mingled vapor climbed toward the rafters, dissipating into the archival dust that coated the upper beams.

No one spoke. The proposal to scale the façade had suspended all dialogue, leaving only this communal respiration as evidence of their panic. Marion Ashworth's fingers remained fixed to the ledger's smudged columns; Dr. Mott's empty glass had ceased its migration across the oak mantelpiece. Teddy Bryant stood with his hands unclasped at his sides. The breath-clouds continued their ascent, mingling where private anxieties should have remained distinct, contaminating the communal atmosphere with shared desperation.

The performance of drawing-room manners had reached its terminal calculation. A gentleman near the fireplace lowered his pocket watch without consulting the face. A lady withdrew her hand from a frayed damask seam and let it fall against her oxidized silver necklace. The visible cost of maintaining these appearances manifested in these small ruptures: seventeen separate respirations merging into one visible sigh of anxiety, rising, fragmenting into individual streams, and vanishing into the shadowed vault above.
