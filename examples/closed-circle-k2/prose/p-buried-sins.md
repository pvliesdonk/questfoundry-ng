Helena stood apart from the seventeen heirs. The silence between them had achieved a specific gravity, pressing against the eardrums with more weight than the ice sheeting the slate above the plaster cornices. No constable remained to record statements; the law had departed with its notebooks incomplete, leaving the assembly to manage their own culpability. She observed the rigid postures around the perimeter, each spine aligned to a military exactness that betrayed the looseness of their moral accounting. The guilt resided in the set of shoulders and the refusal to meet gazes, unprosecuted but catalogued in the muscle memory of each participant.

Bernard's murder would remain filed in the ledger of incomplete entries. The motive had literally reduced to carbon, drifting in the air as particulate matter too fine for any court to subpoena. Helena calculated the impossibility of justice in a room where the evidence had undergone combustion. The killer walked among the heirs, indistinguishable from the innocent by any marker that legal process could isolate. The assembly breathed the same smoke that had contained the proof of malice, exhaling guilt into the shared atmosphere without alibi or accusation.

The grate held the final consumption. Helena watched the last sheets of Bernard's portfolio curl and blacken, the alphabetized divisions of seventeen lives oxidizing into the draught. The Persian rug beneath the andiron remained immaculate, the ash settling only in the hidden weave where vacuum and broom could not extract it. The murder lay buried there, technically unsolved, beneath strata of carbon and the formal structure of a crime without evidence. Above, in the chamber where the oxidized silver chandelier had collapsed, the ice block containing the patriarch's testament rested against the wall, its thaw proceeding at a rate visible to the patient observer, droplets collecting on the floorboards with the regularity of a dripping clock.
