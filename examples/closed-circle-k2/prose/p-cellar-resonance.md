Helena descended the final step. The cellar air pressed against her face, a cold weight that carried no scent of wine or oak, only the mineral sharpness of frozen stone and the administrative silence of storage. She had counted seventeen risers from the pantry; now the staircase sealed itself behind her, the draft extinguishing the match she carried before she could register the flame's color. Darkness resettled, complete and archival.

She stood motionless. The vault extended before her in rows of oxidized hoops and ice-crusted staves, the barrels registering as obsolete account books in the gloom, their contents stopped in time like entries in a ledger no one consulted. Her breath emerged in discrete quanta, visible and countable, each exhalation a small cloud of testimony. She advanced three paces, her right hand trailing the limestone wall, fingers finding the frayed damask of cobwebs that replaced wallpaper here below the frost line, the silk dust adhering to her emerald velvet glove.

The drip occurred at the moment her left foot found the floor's central channel. A single drop detached from the ceiling's black ice and struck the stone with a sound like tarnished silver striking slate. The impact expanded through the vault with the volume of a heavy ledger dropped in an empty archive, reverberating off the ice-crusted racks until the walls seemed to hold it, a note sustained beyond acoustic possibility, multiplying in the freezing air. The echo exposed the chamber's true dimensions: a quarter acre of suffocation trapped beneath the house's public floors, the isolation absolute and without exit.

She removed her hand from the wall. The silence that rushed to fill the sound's absence was heavier than the cold, a palpable weight that settled on her shoulders like a frozen coat. She stood in it, calculating the distance to the wine racks where Bernard had died, understanding that the vault preserved only what was placed within it, and that she had descended now into a space where even her breath would be logged and kept against thaw. The drip had marked her entry in the only record that remained down here. She did not move again for twelve seconds.
