The irons encircled his wrists with the finality of a ledger entry closing an account. The metal weighed against his borrowed tweed, pulling his shoulders into the rigid stance of a clerk who awaited a signature that would never arrive. He stood near the escritoire where he had held his hands before, but the position now served a different registry.

Helena gathered her emerald velvet from the settle. She lifted the fabric with a gesture held too long, the frayed hem brushing the damask that covered Bernard's form, and turned toward the service entrance. She did not look at Teddy. Through the frost-coated glass beyond the empty grate, industrial demolition markers punctuated the snow in precise orange intervals, calculating the estate's erasure in meters and hours. The fireplace held only ash, fine and grey as archival dust, where the proof of Bernard's killers had carbonized into the grate's iron teeth.

Helena paused at the arch and turned. She watched as the constable secured the final chain on Teddy's wrists, the click of the mechanism echoing against the mahogany. Behind them, the fireplace held only grey ash where proof of motives once sat, the carbonized remains of seventeen failures now interred with the deceased. The cousin's men approached the wainscoting with metal tapes that clicked out obsolete account numbers, marking each section for the sleds beyond the service entrance. They measured the walls with the same calculation applied to thawing ice, assessing removal costs against the storm's approach. Justice stood satisfied and empty, complete in its hollow arrival, the ledger closed with the house's dissolution.
