She stated the time of death without inflection. Rigor mortis had fixed the limbs approximately four hours prior to her binding, placing the fatal incident in the east wing before the cellar examination commenced. The blood on her cuff had oxidized to the precise shade of rust that appeared after twenty minutes of air exposure, confirming the scalpel in her bag had entered living tissue prior to the official incision. Teddy’s gaze traveled from the mark to her face. He did not speak, but his hand moved away from the chloroform bottle.

Helena Vance entered the vault. Her emerald velvet gown hem dragged across the limestone floor, frayed threads collecting dust. She carried no light, but her posture indicated she had observed the binding through the cellar grille. Dr. Mott’s medical bag sat open on the settle, and Helena’s gaze fixed upon the interior pocket where the remaining flasks nested against the peppermint oil bottles.

"The examination requires completion," Helena said. Her voice retained the cracked crystal quality, but the register lowered. "The findings must be reliable."

Dr. Mott’s hands remained strapped to the table. The irons above the silk provided an anchor she had not previously allowed herself. She had concealed three metal flasks behind the anatomical manuals, wrapped in damask napkins to prevent clinking. The withdrawal had already begun its arithmetic in her nerves, calculating the hours since her last sip against the tremor she had suppressed.

"In the bag," Dr. Mott said. "The side compartment."

Helena crossed the space. She reached into the bag with her ungloved hand, the tan line on her finger visible against the oxidized silver clasp. She withdrew the flasks without comment, holding them by their necks. The glass contained the remaining gin, approximately eight ounces, preserved against the emergency she had anticipated.

"You will not substitute vinegar for antiseptic again," Helena said.

Dr. Mott shook her head. The motion sent a fresh wave of nausea through her shoulders, but the straps held her wrists immobile. Helena walked to the stone basin where meltwater gathered from the ceiling ice. She uncapped the first flask and poured the gin into the drain. The liquid vanished without sound into the porous limestone. She emptied the second, then the third. The vault filled with the volatile edge of wasted spirits, sharp enough to sting the eyes.

Dr. Mott watched the alcohol disappear. The clarity remained, brutal and unbuffered, the ledger of her addiction closing with a final entry that permitted no emendation. Her joints ached with the specific gravity of obsolete account books, archival dust settling into the spaces where gin had previously circulated.
