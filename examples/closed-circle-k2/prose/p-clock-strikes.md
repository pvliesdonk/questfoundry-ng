The clock struck twelve. Each chime fell against the frozen chandelier like a hammer against glass, twelve blows that dismantled the architecture of courtesy. Marion stood with her bare hand upon the portfolio on the settle, feeling the paper edges shift beneath the leather as the vibrations traveled through the floorboards.

The heirs had arranged themselves in a semicircle around Bernard's corpse, maintaining a distance that suggested both respect and suspicion. Dr. Mott remained stationed by the mantelpiece, her fingers tracing the circle her glass had frozen into the marble. Teddy Bryant stood apart from the others near the hearth where ash marked the rug, his cuffs dark with cellar grease, his hands open at his sides in a posture of arrested gesture. Helena Vance held her removed glove in her left fist, the fabric crumpled like discarded correspondence.

On the twelfth stroke, the frozen key in the chandelier cracked loose and plummeted until the remaining ice arrested its fall. The assembly flinched as one. A gentleman dropped his pocket watch; the crystal shattered against the floorboards with a sound like snapping bone. A lady stepped backward into the escritoire, her hip striking the edge hard enough to rattle the inkwell. The seventeen heirs maintained no composure now. Their breath fogged the air in irregular bursts, and the precision of their spacing collapsed into random clusters.

The last echo died against the sealed windows. The room lay exposed, a vault without exterior, its inhabitants stripped of the shelter that etiquette provides. Marion felt the portfolio grow damp beneath her palm. The cold had ceased to be an atmosphere and had become a verdict.
