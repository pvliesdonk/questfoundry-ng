Marion Ashworth brushed snow from her coat. The gesture felt final, funereal, a closing of accounts with the exterior world. She stood in the vestibule where Bernard had stood not ten minutes prior. The wool retained streaks of dampness. She brushed again. The residue fell to the marble and ceased to be distinct.

She examined her gloves. The kid leather had darkened at the fingertips where moisture seeped. She removed them. Her ink-stained fingers emerged, stiff from the carriage ride. She flexed them once. The stains had settled into the creases like ink into a ledger page.

Beyond the arch, seventeen heirs maintained their rigid postures. The assembly had not moved since the constable closed his notebook. The escritoire held the dried pools and the tarnished braid of the removed livery. She adjusted her pince-nez. The damp wool brushed against her wrist with the weight of evidence.

She smoothed the sleeve. The motion required her to straighten her spine, to arrange her features into the administrative neutrality that passage demanded. She could not bring the storm inside. The snow on her shoulders dissolved into the fabric, becoming her secret, evidence of the cold exterior she had traversed to arrive here. She stepped forward. The marble accepted her footfall without echo. The gesture of brushing had concluded her transition from the outer darkness to this sealed vault of inheritances and threatened reputations. She let her hands fall to her sides. The damp wool settled against her hips. She turned from the window where ice had formed on the interior frame.
