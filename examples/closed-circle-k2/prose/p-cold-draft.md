Marion held the ledger against her chest. The fire had consumed the last visible log three hours prior, leaving a bed of ash that coated the andirons like sediment upon a closed ledger. A draft descended the chimney flue with the sound of pages turning in an empty room. It scattered the ash across the hearth in a pattern that resembled spilled ink caught in the act of dispersal. The final embers guttered and died, and the room's last warmth vanished into the flue.

The temperature plummeted. She felt the drop in the metal of her pince-nez where it touched her nose bridge, and in the ink stains on her fingers that stiffened as the moisture froze. The light had fled with the flames, leaving the silver photograph frame on the mantelpiece dull and unreflective. Dr. Mott's glass ceased its tremor against the brass and sat silent. Teddy stood by the settle, his hands hanging at his sides, the grease visible against the tweed of his cuffs. He had not moved since offering his confession, his posture rigid as theatrical scenery stored in winter.

The draft breached the room's integrity. Ash settled on the parquet in a fine layer, dusting the hem of Helena's velvet gown where it brushed the floor. The ice in the chandelier ceased its dripping. The frozen key above them stabilized, suspended in the stillness that followed the fire's extinction. Marion counted seven seconds of silence. The house exhaled through the chimney, and the barrier between the upper rooms and the frozen cellars dissolved. She wiped ash from her sleeve and turned toward Helena.
