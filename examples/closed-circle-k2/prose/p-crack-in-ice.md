Marion approached the table where the patriarch's frozen testament stood. The ice block rested upon the frayed damask cloth, sweating, oxidized silver candlesticks flanking it like obsolete account books awaiting audit. She had catalogued its dimensions earlier: seventy-four centimeters in height, thirty-two in width, the specific gravity of natural lake ice. Now she observed a deviation that arrested her breath. A hairline fissure ran vertically along the southeast face, originating not from the base where thermal stress would accumulate in natural freezing, but from a point precisely three centimeters below the apex.

She removed her right glove. Her fingertip, ink-stained from earlier notations, traced the seam. The crack presented clean edges, lacking the dendritic branching characteristic of organic fracture. It terminated in a precise horizontal junction, forming a right angle invisible to casual inspection. Marion withdrew her hand. The fissure suggested metal tooling, not weather.

She looked toward the chandelier. The frozen key remained suspended above the assembly, encased in the same milky ice that sheathed the central fixture. The vertical crack aligned with the block's center of mass, a structural compromise that would accelerate thawing at a calculated rate. Someone had prepared this obstruction with archival precision.

Marion replaced her glove. The assembly maintained its rigid postures around the settle, attending to Bernard's corpse with the same administrative patience they had shown the document. No one had observed her examination. She understood that the ice served not as preservation, but as mechanism. The will was not trapped; it was timed.
