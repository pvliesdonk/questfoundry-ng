Helena Vance withdrew her hand from the escritoire's marble edge. The portfolio had rested near the heat since Marion placed it beside the settle, its oiled leather gathering tarnish while the alphabetical tabs absorbed moisture from the frozen air. She grasped the binding. The brass corners felt slick against her emerald velvet gloves, which had frayed at the hem since morning. She lifted the archive without ceremony. Seventeen heirs observed the motion from their positions around the perimeter, their postures shifted as she moved toward the grate where the chandelier lay ruined upon the Persian rug.

She hurled the portfolio into the dying fire. The leather struck the ash bed and curled inward immediately, brass corners darkened as the heat reached the glue beneath the binding. Pages blackened at the edges and folded like dried leaves. The alphabetical arrangement of seventeen failures, debts, and assignations oxidized into smoke. She watched the foolscap that contained Marion's Pimlico encounters carbonize and vanish upward. The fire consumed the evidence with a sound like fingers drumming against a ledger.

The guests stood in silence. Their faces turned toward the chimney as the smoke ascended. Some pressed hands to their stomachs. Others breathed deeply for the first time since the clock struck twelve. The relief appeared in their shoulders first, then in the slackening of their jaws. Nausea followed immediately; several heirs turned away from the spectacle of their own salvation. The evidence of their lives had vanished up the flue, leaving only the smell of burning hair and sealing wax.

By evening they had carried Bernard to the frozen garden. The diggers broke earth that rang like pottery against the spade. They lowered the butler into the trench without epitaph or explanation for his death. His knowledge remained sealed beneath the frost alongside his ivory holder and his liver-spotted hands.
