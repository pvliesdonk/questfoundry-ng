Helena inhaled when the drop struck silver. She held the breath for seventeen seconds, then released it as the next bead of water detached from the chandelier's frozen armature. The interval had stabilized. Around her, the seventeen heirs had ceased their card play; they too marked the cadence, their postures adjusting in micro-alignments with each impact. A gentleman at the escritoire closed his eyes at the exact moment of impact, his lips moving in silent enumeration. Marion Ashworth's fingers stopped their folding and unfolding of kid gloves, suspended now in the interval between drips. Dr. Mott sat beside her medical bag, her shoulders rising and falling with the same measured rhythm, the brass clasps on her bag catching light at precise intervals.

The regularity imposed itself upon the assembly. No pipe clanked, no draft stirred the frayed damask. The house had not surrendered to entropy; it had assumed a pulse. Helena observed the heirs as they recognized this truth in the set of their jaws, in the way their hands deserted their pockets to hang at their sides, awaiting permission to move. The temperature continued its fractional ascent, but the warmth brought no comfort. The metronome dripped again. Seventeen heirs synchronized their breathing to its account, and the cost was visible in their stillness: they had abandoned the pretense that the house proceeded by accident rather than design.
