Helena stood at the escritoire. The morning editions had arrived with the post, and the newspapers lay open on the marble where the constables had abandoned them. The ink was still damp, smearing the alphabetical entries that named seventeen indiscretions with the precision of a ledger. She read her own name beside a date and a sum, the notation as dry as an entry for spirits or coal. Her emerald velvet sleeve brushed the escritoire’s edge, the frayed hem catching on the corner. Marion stood at the window, her pince-nez reflecting the frost that sealed the glass from within. On the settle where Bernard’s body had lain, the medical bag still reeked of peppermint, though Dr. Mott had been removed in irons hours before. The paper recorded her prescription fraud with the same neutral font it used for Marion’s assignations and Helena’s debts. No one spoke. The scandal had achieved the permanence of print, and the cold lived in the paper itself, which seemed to stiffen as it dried, becoming archival and obsolete.

Years passed. The estate transferred to the National Trust with a deed signed by creditors. The heirs dispersed to lodgings in Pimlico and Bath, leaving the furniture under dust sheets. The architecture remained intact, the frost on the windows preserved behind locked glass, awaiting the curiosity of strangers.

The house became a museum of scandal. Tourists queued in the vestibule where Bernard had once brushed snow from his livery, and they photographed the room where the portfolio had rested, their flashes illuminating the oxidized silver of the chandelier armature where the key had frozen. The guide recited the inventory of exposed secrets as if reading prices from a menu, and the visitors listened with the solemn attention of heirs at a will-reading, though they inherited only the spectacle. The blackmail room was roped off with velvet frayed at the hem, and the display case held a reproduction of the ledger, its pages open to the alphabetical entries that had destroyed three lives. The tourists adjusted their cameras and stepped back, converting private shame into public consumption, while the cold remained in the objects—the tarnished inkwell, the damask faded to the color of old ice—and never in the air they breathed.
