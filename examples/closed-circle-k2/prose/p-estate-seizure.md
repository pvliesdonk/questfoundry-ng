The first axe bit into the wainscoting at half past the hour. Helena felt the vibration through the parquet beneath her boots. Seventeen heirs pressed against the east wall, their formations collapsed into a single mass where the cold lived in their posture—shoulders drawn tight, hands clasped behind backs as if bound by invisible irons. Their eyes drifted toward the study door, where Bernard's portfolio remained sealed behind hardware that had tarnished black, the alphabetized record of debts and assignations that had outlasted the patriarch himself.

Another axe struck. Splinters of oak scattered across the Persian rug, joining the ash that marked the path toward the escritoire. The heirs did not speak. Their silence had shifted from performance to demand; they stood united in their willingness to see the scandal catalogued, their own indiscretions weighed against the greater crime of Bernard's death. Helena remained seated. The emerald velvet of her glove caught on a splinter of wood as she adjusted her position, and she calculated the depreciating value of the house against the rising worth of testimony.

From the vestibule, the cousin's agent whistled a tune that belonged to countinghouses. He moved along the trestle tables where heirlooms awaited transport, touching each item with gloved fingers that left no trace. Crystal, silver, the obsolete account books from the east wing—he marked each in a leather-bound docket, assigning numbers that would become entries in police evidence logs. His breath did not mist in the air; the cold had settled into his routine, a bureaucratic precision that acknowledged no distinction between the living and the archived.

The heirs watched him work. Their gazes shifted between the agent's inventory and the study door, acknowledging that the evidence would soon speak to strangers. The agent paused at a tarnished candelabra. He whistled the next measure, and seventeen pairs of eyes fixed upon the docket, recognizing that the heirlooms would become exhibits, the house itself a ledger of frozen water and oxidized silver that would be read by courts.
