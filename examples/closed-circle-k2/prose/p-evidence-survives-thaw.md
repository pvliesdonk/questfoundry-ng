Teddy knelt on the Persian rug where ash still marked the path toward the escritoire. He held his shoulders rigid beneath the borrowed tweed and kept his hands visible, flat against the frozen fibers. The constable stood two paces off, his presence sufficient guard without raised voice or drawn baton. Helena held the eviction notice at her side, the cousin's seal broken against her emerald velvet glove. The document trembled once, then steadied.

The constable turned the combination on the wall safe behind the frayed damask. He placed Bernard's leather portfolio between obsolete account books that had outlasted their owners. The click of the locking mechanism measured the distance between evidence and entropy. He withdrew his pocket watch and held it near the frost-rimed window, comparing the timepiece against the ice's retreat. The thaw would release the roads by morning, transporting the archive to courts that would outlast the house itself.

Helena stepped beside Teddy. Her shadow fell across his grease-stained fingers, which remained pressed against the rug. The constable reviewed the safe's contents through the open door, confirming the alphabetized arrangement of debts and assignations. The cousin's agents moved through the great hall with their dockets, cataloguing the chandeliers' oxidized silver armatures and tarnished chains as if recording the anatomy of a dismantled corpse. The estate would not survive the season; its walls would collapse into flooded vaults, its heirlooms dispersed to countinghouses. The blackmail archive would remain preserved in the safe, waiting to testify against Bernard's killers long after the last chandelier fell.
