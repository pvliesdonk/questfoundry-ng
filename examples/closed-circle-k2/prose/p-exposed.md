Marion Ashworth stood at the center of the drawing room. The settle's edge had left a line across her palm where the wood pressed against the key-bow welt; she opened her fingers and observed the mark without covering it. The gesture felt administrative, yet her hand trembled in a manner that betrayed the protocols of ledger-keeping. She had filed her devastating review, witnessed the foolscap containing her assignations circulate among seventeen heirs, and discharged her marriage with the same bureaucratic finality she had once reserved for correcting theatrical blocking. Now the correction remained visible upon her hand, and upon her bare finger where the ring had rested until the previous hour, leaving a groove precise as an entry in a bankruptcy account.

She adjusted her pince-nez. The motion, which had previously signaled the prelude to judgment, now revealed the shake in her thumb. Seventeen heirs observed the tremor from their positions around the room's perimeter, maintaining the distance of parties to a dissolved contract. She saw their postures shift, noting the exact angle at which each inventory-taker adjusted their stance to assess her diminished assets. Helena remained at the escritoire, her emerald velvet sleeve absorbing damp from the marble, her gaze averted from the documents that had failed to purchase silence. The ink on Marion's fingers had dried to the color of oxidized silver, staining the skin where the kid gloves had protected her until recently. She had set the gloves down beside the wreckage of the chandelier, which rested upon the Persian rug with its metal armature twisted against the weave, and her hands remained exposed to the room's administrative chill.

The architecture of her performance had collapsed. Every gesture she made—the sharp inhalation before speaking, the precise straightening of her spine, the assumption of critical authority—now broadcast the underlying structure of bone and fear beneath the costume of composure. She stood in the indexed ruins of her own reputation, cataloguing the cost of her discretion, aware that the chill had settled into the mortar and would not be dislodged by season's end.
