Helena Vance lifted the teapot. The oxidized silver had dulled to the color of old account books, and the stream she poured fell in a thin, precise column. It filled Marion Ashworth's cup without deviation.

Marion sat across from her. The pince-nez caught the light from the dying fire, but the wire did not mark her skin in any way Helena could observe. Marion's hands, sheathed now in kid leather that had darkened past cleaning, rested on the table's edge. The gloves concealed the ink stains and the welt shaped like a key bow that Helena knew inhabited the palm.

"You maintain a steady hand," Marion said.

Helena set the pot upon the trivet. She withdrew her own hands to her lap, where the emerald velvet of her gown settled, the frayed hem lying against her slipper. The tan line on her palm remained visible, a pale circumference that no longer carried the threat of exposure but retained its clarity as evidence.

"The Darjeeling has kept its flavor," Helena said. Her voice produced the sound of cracked crystal, precise and brittle.

Marion lifted her cup. She inhaled, a sharp intake of breath that preceded her judgment, and sipped. The china clicked against her teeth.

"Administrative competence," Marion said. "One notes the attention to protocol."

Their eyes met above the table. Marion's gaze held the specific weight of carbonized paper, of a review turned to ash. Helena returned the look with the accumulated data of assignation dates, now likewise dissolved. Neither spoke. The knowledge passed between them in silent transaction, archived and cross-referenced in memory alone.

Helena did not reach for the bell cord. Marion did not adjust her position to stand. The frost on the interior windows had advanced since the clock last struck, sealing the room against the outside. The ash on the Persian rug marked the path toward the escritoire, a trail now impassable without mutual consent. The ruined chandelier lay across the parquet floor where it had fallen, its oxidized silver armature twisted and its chain tarnished, a frozen waterfall of metal and ice.

The silence accumulated. It weighed like account books gummed shut by frozen water and the residue of liquor. Helena calculated the distance to the door and rejected the movement. Marion's hand remained stationary above her cup; the stillness suggested a similar accounting of risk against exposure.

They remained seated. The tea cooled in cups that neither finished. The cold transmitted itself through the oxidized silver service into their fingers, preserved them in formaldehyde and frost. The arrangement held them suspended, incapable of separation without the fear of mutual dissolution.
