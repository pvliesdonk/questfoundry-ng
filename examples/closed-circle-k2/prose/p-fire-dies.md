Marion Ashworth watched the final ember subside into grey ash. The grate held nothing now but the residue of burnt leather and foolscap, cooling rapidly against the iron. Water dripped from the ceiling onto the dead fire and hissed into silence. The drawing room dimmed as the last glow failed, shifting from amber to charcoal, then to the shade of obsolete account books. 

Seventeen heirs stood against the walls. Their breath emerged in discrete clouds, suspended before their faces like archival dust disturbed from a ledger, then vanishing. No one commented. A gentleman adjusted his collar with movements slowed by the temperature, his fingers stiff above the mother-of-pearl studs.

Marion stood without her gloves, which she had left upon the bench. Her fingers ached. She folded them against her palms and felt the resistance of the joints. The assembly appeared frozen in their stations. Each heir remained in the position they had assumed, though their shoulders rose and fell with increased rapidity as the temperature dropped. Marion observed the vaporous evidence of their respiration, each exhalation a small betrayal of the body's internal machinery. A lady near the window pressed her handkerchief to her lips. The linen stiffened against the fabric.

The cold lived in the objects now: the inkwell held a meniscus of ice; the fittings contracted with audible clicks; the floor covering stiffened beneath their feet. Marion straightened her spine. She would not be the first to seek warmth. Seventeen heirs stood in the grey half-light, breathing visible ghosts into the space between them, and no one moved to stoke the grate.
