Teddy set the iron against the grate. The ice block had fractured along its fault lines, and the document inside rested exposed against the andiron, ink bleeding at the margins but the patronymic legible despite the water damage. He straightened his borrowed tweed and felt the lining catch against his wrist. The grease beneath his fingernails had congealed since he had handled the cellar rack, and he kept his hands visible as he stepped back from the heat.

Seventeen heirs stood in a semicircle around the Persian rug. Their gazes lifted from the parchment toward the ceiling beams, tracking the location of the study where Marion had retired. They understood that she held the safe key against her welted skin, and that the archive waited behind oxidized silver, inaccessible until she returned. Their chins dipped in grudging acknowledgment that the text designated him heir, tempered by the mutual knowledge that final verification remained locked upstairs.

Water pooled on the rug, spreading from the thawing will toward the escritoire. Helena Vance stood beside the desk, her emerald velvet glove resting near the inkwell. Her eyes fixed upon his right hand, then lifted toward the ceiling, indicating her awareness that the birth record in the files above would unmake his claim if examined. Teddy understood that she calculated the risk of exposure against the storm that sealed the roads.

He dipped the nib. The scratch of the pen against paper echoed in the silence, louder than the drip of melting ice. His hand trembled. The forged signature wavered, the loops distorting under the pressure of her observation and their shared knowledge of what sat locked above. He completed the final stroke and set the pen down. The safe ticked above them, measuring the time until the key might turn.
