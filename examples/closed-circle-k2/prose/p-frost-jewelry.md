Marion adjusted her pince-nez and regarded Bernard as he stood in the doorway to the Great Hall. Ice crystals had formed upon his eyelashes, catching the lamplight like some grotesque bridal jewelry. The sight summoned an unbidden memory of winter weddings she had witnessed in her youth, ceremonies where the bride’s silver thread had tarnished against the frost, where the ornamental became indistinguishable from the injured.

He had come up from the cellars; she could smell the damp archive of wine and stone clinging to his livery, the scent of obsolete account books left to molder in subzero vaults. His fingers, liver-spotted and clutching the inventory ledger, betrayed no tremor, yet the frozen water on his lashes confirmed what the dropping temperature had only suggested. The frost had penetrated the civilized interior. The barrier between the house’s public face and its frozen underbelly had dissolved.

She noted how the crystals refracted the light, transforming his unblinking pale eyes into something both decorative and damaged. This was no mere atmospheric effect. The cold had migrated upward through the stairs, through the very protocols of service and scrutiny, until it adorned him like oxidized silver, until ornament and injury collapsed into a single, glittering fact. He stood there, bearing his ledger, his breath visible in short notations that hung and vanished, a living ledger of the house’s decay.
