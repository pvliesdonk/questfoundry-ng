Helena stood at the window. The frost that had sealed the glass from within began to surrender its geometry, retreating from the center in jagged tributaries that carried the dust of the room suspended in water. She watched the gray emerge behind the dissolving crystals—not the white of continued storm, but the flat, municipal gray of an ordinary winter morning in the world beyond the estate. The process was irreversible; each droplet ran a vertical course down the pane, cutting a clear channel through the accumulated rime.

The meltwater gathered in the sill's groove, pooling against the frame until the seal broke and a drop descended the wall. It left a dark trail through the archival dust that had settled on the wainscoting, marking the plaster with a stain that would not lift. The barrier had been imperfect all along, merely frozen, and now the thaw announced that the isolation that had preserved their crisis was ending. The protective opacity failed by inches, and the gray light admitted that the world had continued its indifferent administration beyond the glass.

She stepped back from the transparency. The light entered unfiltered, falling across the Persian rug where the chandelier's wreckage still sprawled, across the escritoire where damp newspapers recorded seventeen indiscretions with permanent ink. The comfortable ignorance of the sealed room—the pretense that they existed outside of time and consequence—dissolved with the ice. Outside, a crow shifted on a bare branch, visible now, and it regarded the house with the detached curiosity of the uninvolved. The truth outside was that the world continued, and would receive them, and would judge. Helena turned toward the stairs before the water pooling at the sill could breach the floorboards.
