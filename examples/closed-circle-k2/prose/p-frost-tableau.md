Helena Vance turned toward the window. The ice on the interior surface had thickened into a lens that silvered the room behind her into a flat tableau. She saw seventeen figures arrested mid-gesture: Marion’s ink-stained hand hovered above the settle where she had placed the portfolio, Dr. Mott’s fingers gripped the rim of her empty glass, and Teddy stood with his grease-stained palms open in a posture of surrender. The chandelier appeared as fractured glass above them, its frozen key suspended like punctuation in a sentence no one dared complete.

The reflection preserved the exact angle of accusation. Marion’s shoulder aligned with the escritoire’s tarnished brass; Bernard’s corpse maintained its rigid diagonal on the settle, the broken cigarette holder catching the dim light like a splinter of bone. No breath fogged the glass from within; the cold had consumed even that evidence of life. The heirs remained fixed in their positions, their shadows motionless against the damask; civility calcified into a posture that permitted no movement forward or retreat.

Helena observed her own gloved hand, emerald velvet darkened to black in the reflection, clutching the portfolio that contained the ruin of Marion’s reputation. She did not lower it. They held their places, trading the possibility of justice for the certainty of frozen order, until the thaw or the ceiling fell.
