Marion Ashworth lifted the ledger from the settle. The leather binding had darkened at the edges where moisture had wicked into the grain. She opened it to the marked page with her thumb and forefinger, bracing the spine against her palm.

The paper resisted. She applied steady pressure and the sheet separated from its neighbor with a sound like tearing silk. The fibers stood in relief against the lamplight, ridged and corrugated in parallel lines that ran the length of the margin. She ran her nail across the surface, feeling the raised striations catch against the quick.

"Water damage," she said.

Helena stood at the window, her back to the room. "Bernard kept the cellar inventory dry."

"Not damp," Marion said. "Frozen. Submerged and thawed."

She held the page to the light. The warping followed a pattern she had seen once in a library flood during a Cambridge winter—paper that had frozen solid, its cellulose structure expanded by ice crystals, then softened again into this corrugated topography. The ink had survived, but the substrate bore the topology of its imprisonment.

Dr. Mott looked up from the body. "The cellars flooded?"

"The lake," Marion said. She closed the ledger with a snap that made Teddy flinch. He had returned from the upper corridor and stood now by the mantelpiece, his hands clasped behind his back in an attitude of parade rest. "Something was taken from the ice. Or dropped into it."

The ridges in the paper held their shape despite the warmth of her fingers. Cold lived in the archive, preserving the record of its own sin.
