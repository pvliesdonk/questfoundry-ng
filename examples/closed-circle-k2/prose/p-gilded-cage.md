Marion Ashworth smiled. The expression tightened her cheekbones and produced a copper taste upon her tongue. She held the smile as she held her cup, with calibrated pressure that risked neither spillage nor commitment.

Helena Vance sat opposite. The emerald velvet of her gown had settled into the chair's damask, the frayed hem recording the day's movements in loosened threads. Her hands rested on the table's edge, the tan lines on her palms facing upward like empty ledger columns. She maintained her posture without shifting. Marion observed the angle of her spine against the chair back, the rigid geometry of a woman braced against collapse.

The silence between them had thickened since the papers turned to ash. It occupied the space where negotiation had occurred, where threats had been catalogued and exchanged for equivalent silence. Now it pressed against the windows, which had sealed with frost since morning, and against the door, which stayed shut against the corridor. The cold moved through the china into Marion's fingers. She rotated her wedding band. The metal had survived the incineration; the record of her assignation had not.

Marion swallowed. The metallic taste remained. She calculated the distance to the escritoire and found it impassable. The path crossed through the wreckage of the chandelier, its tarnished metal armature twisted across the floorboards, and through the zone where their mutual destruction had been averted by simultaneous surrender. They had preserved each other with matches and mutual cowardice. The courtesy they maintained operated as bars, gilded and ornate, trapping them in postures of administrative civility while the storm sealed the estate beyond the ice-rimed panes.
