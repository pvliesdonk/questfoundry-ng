She ascended the twenty-three steps with the calipers in her left hand and the scalpel in her right, the instruments trembling against each other in a minor key. The iron on her wrists had warmed to her body temperature, but the metal still chafed the skin with each upward motion. She emerged from the cellar door into the Great Hall where seventeen heirs stood in a semicircle around Bernard’s settle. The fire had sunk to embers; the room held the gray light of the storm.

Her medical bag remained below, beside the body, but she had carried the flask upstairs in her coat pocket. It was silver, or had been silver before the tarnish set in, and it caught the dying light from the chandelier as she moved toward the center of the room. The glass surface showed the level of clear liquid remaining: precisely half of what it had contained when she had filled it below. She felt the weight of seventeen gazes shift from Bernard’s covered form to the oxidized curve protruding from her wool pocket.

Helena Vance stepped forward with her emerald gloves clasped before her. Marion Ashworth turned from the escritoire, her fingers still stained with ink. Teddy Bryant stood by the andiron, his borrowed tweed jacket sagging at the shoulders. No one spoke. Dr. Mott’s hand moved toward the pocket to conceal the flask, but the motion arrested when she recognized the futility. The tremor in her fingers resumed, broadcasting the precise chemical composition of her blood to the assembly. She lowered her hands to her sides. The pretense of medical authority detached from her posture like ice calving from a glacier, and she stood exposed in the center of the rug with only the half-empty vessel gleaming against her hip.
