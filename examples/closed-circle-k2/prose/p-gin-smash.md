Teddy stood in the vault where the bulb swung above the black ice. His breath hung visible in the draft. He held the gin bottles he had found concealed by frayed damask between the 1874 and the 1876 ports. He gripped the iron poker in his other hand.

Dr. Mott stood beside Bernard's form on the settle. Her wrists wore irons that caught the swinging light. Her medical bag sat open beside her, reeking of peppermint oil that failed to mask the sharper absence beneath it. She watched Teddy's hands.

He raised the bottles and struck them against the limestone wall. The impact sang through the vault like a ledger snapping shut. Glass shattered. Liquid sheeted down the stone and pooled on the floor, mixing with the ice crust. The alcohol steamed briefly and then stilled.

Dr. Mott stepped forward. Her hands trembled violently, the irons clinking. She reached toward the shards as if calculation might reassemble them.

"That was evidence," she said. Her voice cracked.

"It was your anesthesia," Teddy said.

She straightened. The tremor traveled up her arms to her shoulders. Sweat gathered on her temple and froze there, a crystal node that the irons prevented her from brushing away. A drop of water fell from the ceiling's black ice and struck the broken glass. She looked at the ruin, then at the bag containing her scalpel, then at Bernard's form.

"I require," she said, and stopped. She swallowed. "I require clarity for the examination."

"You shall have it," Teddy said.

The withdrawal bent her forward. She gripped the settle's edge with white knuckles and straightened again, a motion broken mid-gesture. The performance of medical authority she had once wielded cracked like the ice above them. She faced him without the buffer of liquor, her gaze raw.

"Then witness what sobriety retrieves," she said.
