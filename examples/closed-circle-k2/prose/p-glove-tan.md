Marion withdrew her right hand from the glove. The lining parted from her ink-stained fingers, uncovering a band of skin three tones lighter than the exposed knuckles, a stripe of pigment that chronicled hours absent from her careful records. The tan line ringed her finger at the exact junction where the kid leather terminated, a boundary as distinct as a ruled line in an account book.

She extended the hand at waist height, turning it to catch the gray light. The differential between covered and uncovered flesh represented a collapse of coverage, a fracture in the armor of propriety she had maintained since the storm sealed the valley. For fourteen months she had enforced the distinction between public skin and private compromise, yet the boundary had blurred under winter sun she could not place in her memory. The error appeared in the ledger of her body as clearly as a smudged entry.

Helena remained beside the escritoire, her attention directed toward the ice block resting upon the andiron. Dr. Mott had turned from Bernard's body, her gaze settling upon Marion's hand. Seventeen heirs maintained their positions in the freezing hall, their breath forming faint clouds that vanished before rising. Marion observed their attention shift to the exposed skin, noted in the silence.

Marion understood that the glove had not shielded her; it had merely postponed the exposure. The tan line attested to hours unrecorded, to assignations that had imprinted despite all concealment. She let the glove dangle from her left hand like a discarded skin, the two-toned finger extended toward the assembly as evidence she could not withdraw.
