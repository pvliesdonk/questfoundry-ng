Dr. Mott took the portfolio from Marion's unresisting hands. The leather had stiffened in the dry cold, corners brass-bound and oxidized to a dull green that matched the tarnish on Helena's neglected silver. She opened it. The pages were arranged with the precision of a medical chart, each entry dated and initialed in Bernard's cramped clerk's hand.

She found her own name beneath the tab marked M. The notation recorded three deliveries from the distillery courier, quantified in bottles and dates, with marginal calculations of her credit against the household accounts. The handwriting did not accuse; it merely noted the peppermint she used to mask the liquor on her breath, the tremor in her hands during the patriarch's examination. The facts lay inert on the page, awaiting activation.

Helena stood beside her. The emerald velvet of her sleeve brushed the oiled leather as she turned to her own entry. The hostess said nothing, but her finger traced the entry for the warehouse theatre receipts, the curriculum vitae that proved Teddy's origins. Her hand withdrew and gripped the portfolio's edge.

"We burn it," Helena said. Her voice retained its cracked crystal pitch, but the words emerged staccato, interrupting the room's suspended breath. "Now. Tonight."

Dr. Mott closed the archive. The pages resisted, reluctant to return to darkness. She understood that the preservation of their lives now required the destruction of this record. The obsolete account book had become a ledger of execution, and only ash could void its entries.
