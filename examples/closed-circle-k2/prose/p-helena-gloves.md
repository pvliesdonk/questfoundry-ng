Helena Vance drew the left glove taut across her palm. The leather creaked like dry bones. She aligned the right cuff, smoothing emerald velvet until frayed threads vanished beneath the seam. The motion consumed twelve seconds. It established a cadence the frozen room required, a measured interval that held the chaos of the recognition at bay while the cold deepened its claim on the furniture.

Marion Ashworth kept the certificates suspended at waist height, the papers trembling slightly in the dim light. Dr. Mott stood by the sideboard, her empty glass positioned at the precise center of a tarnished silver coaster. Teddy Bryant remained near the mantelpiece, his shoulders rigid, his fingers lying still against his trouser seam in the posture of a man who had abandoned a gesture midway. The cold had calcified the upholstery, stiffening the damask until the chairs seemed to calculate distances between occupants with archival precision.

Bernard Croft appeared in the doorway. Ice crystals glinted on his eyelashes, and he carried the cellar inventory ledger against his chest with both hands, the leather binding stiff with frost. His boots left faint marks on the frozen floorboards. Helena watched him enter. She did not rush the inspection of her gloves, though the tendons in her jaw had tightened beneath the powdered surface, betraying the violence she contained beneath the polished etiquette.

She looked up. The gesture held too long, a performer's pause calculated to project across the footlights. Her voice carried the brittleness of frozen crystal. "Mr. Croft," she said. The syllables landed flat between them, stripped of warmth, a bureaucratic exactitude that permitted no acknowledgment of what he had certainly found below.
