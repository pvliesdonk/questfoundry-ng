Helena Vance adjusted the emerald velvet at her wrist where the frayed threads caught against her glove. The drawing room had contracted around the assembly, the air between the guests grown thick with the particular silence that followed documentation. Marion Ashworth stood with papers in her hand, certificates whose discrepancies Helena did not need to inspect to verify. The cold had settled into the damask furniture, stiffening the upholstery until the chairs seemed to hold their occupants at a measurable distance.

She looked at Teddy Bryant. He stood near the center of the room, his borrowed tweed hanging wrong across the shoulders, grease still dark beneath his fingernails despite the attempt at transformation. His hand moved toward his pocket watch, then arrested the gesture. It was the stillness of the hand that reached her first, the particular arrested motion of a performer caught mid-count before an entrance.

Twenty years prior, in a warehouse theatre south of the river, she had trained a boy to still his hands before the curtain. The same disciplined arrest, the same calculation of breath before the lie of the stage. She had taught him to project from the diaphragm, to shape vowels into weapons, to become whatever the role required.

"You played the second murderer in *The Duchess of Malfi*," she said. Her voice carried the crack of long-unused registers, crystal strained beyond its tolerance. "You forgot your cue in the fourth act and wept backstage for an hour. I gave you a handkerchief."

Teddy's head turned. The posh accent he had maintained through dinner slipped entirely, revealing the harder consonants of Whitechapel. "Mrs. Vance," he said. The syllables came flat, stripped of pretense.

"The forgery was competent," Helena continued. She did not raise her voice. "But the performance betrayed you. You were acting the heir as you once acted the duke's villain. The training survived when the documents failed."

The room's civility remained intact in its forms—cups positioned on saucers, spines straight against chair backs—but the frost had entered the gesture. Marion looked from the certificates to Helena, understanding threading through her expression. Dr. Mott's hand tightened on her empty glass. The heir they had assembled to mourn was a fabrication built upon Helena's own obsolete curriculum, a deception rendered professional by her tutelage.

Teddy said nothing. His fingers twitched once, then lay still against his thigh, the composure of a student discovered in an error he had rehearsed to perfection.
