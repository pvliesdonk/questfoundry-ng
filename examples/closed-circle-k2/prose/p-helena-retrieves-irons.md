Helena crossed the parquet to the fireplace. The grate held a bed of carbonized matter, the last of Bernard's alphabetical divisions collapsed into grey particulate that still held the rectangular memory of foolscap. No draught disturbed the ash; it settled into the iron weave of the andiron with the patience of archival dust accumulating in a closed repository.

She stood before the velvet-lined stand where the fire irons rested in their prescribed order. The poker and tongs lay parallel, their brass handles tarnished to a brownish patina that matched the oxidized silver of the fallen chandelier across the room. She extended her hand, noting the emerald velvet of her glove where it frayed at the wrist, the threads separating like split hairs in an obsolete account book.

Her fingers closed around the poker's shaft. The metal retained the chill of the room, transmitting it through the fabric to her palm. She lifted the iron from its rest, feeling the weight of the instrument shift from decorative object to functional weapon. The tongs remained in place, insufficient for the task she calculated. She gripped the poker in her right hand, the hem of her gown brushing the Persian rug where the ash trail had scattered earlier, now obscured by the debris of the collapse.

The seventeen heirs maintained their perimeter around the settle, their postures rigid as the frozen water sheeting the windows. She held the iron at her side, its heft a ledger entry in her muscles, and turned toward the assembly. The poker's point described a small arc in the air, innocent and deliberate, as she assessed the distance to Teddy Bryant's position near the escritoire.
