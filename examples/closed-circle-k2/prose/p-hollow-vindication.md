Marion stood at the settle's edge. Her fingers found the manuscript in her pocket, tracing the folded edge where the fibers had stiffened against the damp. The paper felt like a filed receipt for services rendered, brittle and exact. She had published the review; the heirs had read the inventory of Helena's theatrical failures. The exposure of her own assignations now served as collateral documentation in a closed account, stamped and filed beyond retrieval.

She withdrew the page slightly, enough to feel the ink's raised surface through the kid leather. The sensation produced a satisfaction sharp and breakable as frozen glass. She had executed her judgment with administrative precision, and the theater had closed its doors on Helena's career. This was the texture of martyrdom: the precise alignment of principle and destruction, documented in copperplate and distributed to the assembly with certified copies.

Her righteousness burned with the same cold intensity that sealed the windowpanes. It provided no warmth, only a blue-white clarity that illuminated the ledger of damages without forgiving them. The manuscript in her pocket represented a completed transaction—her marriage discharged, her reputation itemized among the losses, her critique entered into the permanent record with binding force.

The vindication tasted of cellar ice and oxidized silver. She stood in the archive of her own making, surrounded by the heirs who had witnessed both disclosures. The manuscript's edges cut against her palm with the hollowness of a perfect entry that balanced no books, corrected no errors, restored no capital to the empty account.
