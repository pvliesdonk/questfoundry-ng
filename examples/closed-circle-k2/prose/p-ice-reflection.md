Marion stepped toward the block. The ice had begun to sweat under the fire's insistence, its surface glossed with a sheen that mirrored the room in curved distortion. She adjusted her pince-nez and leaned closer. Her reflection swam upward in the frozen water, elongated and grotesque, the proportions of her face pulled beyond their proper boundaries by the ice's merciless curvature. The ink stains on her fingers appeared as black lesions against the pallid blur of her hands.

She drew breath to steady herself. The inhalation caught in her throat, sharp and insufficient, the same breath she took before recording a particularly damning notice in her notebook. Here was the caricature she had spent decades avoiding: the sharp beak of nose exaggerated, the severe line of mouth pulled into a rictus, the eyes bulging behind the spectacles' magnification. The ice rendered her as she rendered others—grotesque, malformed, stripped of the civility that posture and tailoring suggested.

The reflection trembled as the block settled. Marion straightened, her gloves brushing the frayed damask of her sleeve. The distortion remained fixed in her mind's eye, a ledger entry she could not redact. She understood that the polished manners she maintained were merely a thin varnish over this truth, that the critic who dissected the failures of others was herself a specimen of grotesquerie, preserved here in frozen water until the thaw should release her ugliness into the common air.
