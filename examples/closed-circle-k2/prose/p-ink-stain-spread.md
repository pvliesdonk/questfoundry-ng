Marion Ashworth placed her palm flat against the escritoire's edge. The inkwell had tipped. A dark pool formed on the linen, spreading outward in slow increments. The stain advanced across the damask weave like a bruise rising beneath skin.

Seventeen heirs stood in the freezing vault of the Great Hall. Their posture had held rigid through Teddy's testimony, through the revelation of Bernard's fourteen-year embezzlement, through the admission that they had traded silence for discretion and purchased only shame. Now their composure cracked along with the plaster.

A gentleman lowered his pocket watch. The chain tangled in his buttonhole. He tugged; the button held; his elbow struck the mantelpiece. Dr. Mott's glass trembled on the marble, leaving a frozen ring that expanded and contracted with the temperature. No one moved to right the inkwell.

The stain reached the ledger Marion had left open. It seeped into page eleven, blurring Bernard's cellar entry, dissolving the record of his alibi into a dark smear. The ink touched her fingertip. She did not withdraw her hand.

Helena Vance stepped back. Her heel caught the Persian rug's ash trail. She staggered, caught herself on the settle where Bernard's body lay, and righted her posture with a gasp that showed her teeth. The emerald velvet of her glove brushed the tarnished silver of the inkwell's cap. The assembly watched the stain spread without comment.

The pretense of control had dissolved. The evening's performance ended not with the thaw of ice but with this slow seep of liquid into cloth, marking the escritoire's surface with evidence that could not be burned, frozen, or denied.
