Helena checked her watch. Three hours had passed since Bernard's body was moved to the settle, and the temperature in the hall had risen two degrees by the thermometer near the door. The ice block containing the testament now showed a sheen of meltwater on its surface, and the crack Marion had observed earlier had widened to a visible fissure. The mathematics of thaw had shifted from preservation to dissolution.

She observed the others without turning her head. Marion had unfolded her certificates and refolded them three times, the paper softening with handling. Dr. Mott's bag remained on the floor where she had placed it, but she had moved the empty glass from the coaster to the mantelpiece, a displacement of two meters that suggested restlessness constrained by protocol. Teddy had unclasped his hands and now held them at his sides, fingers curled as if awaiting the weight of a servant's tray that would not arrive. None had spoken in seventeen minutes.

The killer occupied one of these postures. Helena calculated the remaining hours before the valley became passable. At the current rate of warming, the thaw would break the seal in forty-eight hours, perhaps less if the wind shifted. Each hour of maintained silence allowed the murderer to contemplate the destruction of evidence or the preparation of flight. The etiquette that preserved their positions around the dead man also preserved the killer's anonymity, a transaction of civility that traded justice for the temporary comfort of not accusing.

She brushed another crystal from her hem. The gesture no longer maintained appearance; it marked time.
