Marion lifted the portfolio toward her face. The leather had retained the damp of the lower level, a cold residue that seemed to deepen as she brought it closer. She inhaled. The scent was not merely aged hide and polishing oils, but something beneath that—sweet and particular, the ferment of organic matter breaking down in darkness. It was the smell of ledgers left too long in vaults, of paper rotting from within, of secrets undergoing their slow chemical decomposition. The odor possessed a texture she could almost taste, the granular persistence of archival dust suspended in corrupted air.

She held the binding closer. The smell intensified against her cheeks. The odor clung to the brass corners and to the tabs that marked alphabetical entries with a specificity that suggested prolonged proximity to spoilage. It rose from the pages she had already examined, a miasma rising from the documented affairs and embezzlements. The corruption was not metaphorical; it lived in the fibers, a physical spoilage that no amount of bureaucratic precision could arrest. The portfolio presented itself as a respectable instrument of filing—oiled, cornered, ordered—and concealed within its spine the stench of mortality.

Marion lowered the archive. The smell remained in her nostrils, a cold intrusion that would not dissipate with breath. She understood that the household's preserved civility was similarly compromised: a surface maintained while the contents liquefied. The leather had trapped Bernard's final environment, the cellars where he had catalogued their disgrace. She stood in the servant's cell, holding the evidence of their shared decay, and felt the weight of obsolete account books settle into her arms with the gravity of ice forming on still water.
