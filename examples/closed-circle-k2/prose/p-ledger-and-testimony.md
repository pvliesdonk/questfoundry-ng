Teddy cleared his throat. The sound cracked in the vault like ice shearing from a blackened barrel hoop. He stood with his hands unclasped. The grease beneath his fingernails caught the light of the single exposed bulb that swung in drafts above the settle. Marion held the ledger open against her lap. The pages documented Bernard's embezzlement in cramped columns: vintage years, quantities removed, prices realized in cash. The records spanned fourteen months of systematic theft, the paper bearing water marks from freeze-thaw cycles but the ink remaining legible as a physician's prescription. Each entry placed the butler in the cellars during hours he claimed to serve upstairs, inventorying spirits he had already sold to a Pimlico dealer.

Teddy understood what testimony would cost. He had found the evidence himself three hours prior: a glass of port with Bernard's lip prints, a corkscrew abandoned between the 1874 and 1876 vintages, the dust disturbed in a pattern that suggested hasty retreat. If he spoke now of these observations, he would place Bernard below stairs at the precise hour the patriarch's skull split against the iron bracket. The alibi would clear Teddy of the murder he had falsely confessed to shield another, but the ledger would ensure Bernard's ruin. The butler would face the courts for larceny rather than the gallows for homicide, his blackmail archive rendered valueless by the exposure of his accounts.

He removed his borrowed tweed jacket and folded it over the settle beside Bernard's body. The fabric smelled of archival dust and obsolete mothballs. Twenty years prior in a Whitechapel warehouse he had forgotten his cue as the second murderer, standing silent while the Duchess died offstage. Now he prepared to speak with precision, knowing his words would sever his own noose and snap another man's livelihood like the ivory cigarette holder snapped beside the pantry corpse.
