Helena smoothed her lapels. The sergeant had closed his ledger; the irons remained fixed on the wrists of the arrested. She stepped toward the escritoire where the alphabetized pages lay in sorted piles. Her bootheel caught the edge of the fallen pocket watch, the mechanism the gentleman had shattered earlier, and she corrected her stride.

A sheet detached from the uppermost folder. It drifted downward, rotating once, a square of vellum that caught the lamplight like tarnished silver exposed after long concealment. It struck the Persian rug and settled with its crease split open, the ink still legible despite the damp that had seeped into the room from melting ice.

The assembled heirs shifted their gazes from the constables to the fallen document. Helena observed the hand that had released it—Marion's fingers remained extended, trembling, the gesture frozen mid-retraction. The letter bore no formal address, only a line of dedication that named an attachment Helena had not catalogued among the arithmetic of embezzlement and prescription fraud. The handwriting matched samples she had seen in the cellar ledger.

The assembly stood in arrested motion. A gentleman adjusted his stance; the motion caused his shadow to fall across the page, obscuring the signature but not the substance. The performance of administrative neutrality that had governed their postures since the constable's arrival cracked along its central seam. Marion did not retrieve the letter. No one offered to burn it. The room became a sealed vault of mutual knowledge, and the cold lived in the objects—the frayed damask, the oxidized silver of the ruined chandelier—rather than the air.
