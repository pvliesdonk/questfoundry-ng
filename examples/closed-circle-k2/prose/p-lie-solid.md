Helena withdrew her hand from the tweed sleeve. The signature remained on the document, a permanent mark that had dried darker than the water stains blurring the ledger's earlier entries. She had steadied the forgery without speaking, and the gesture had completed itself as gestures did in long-rehearsed productions, becoming indistinguishable from the authentic movement it mimicked. The performance had repeated itself through sufficient iterations to congeal into administrative fact; the distinction between assumed identity and recorded name had dissolved into the paper's fiber.

She turned toward the glass above the escritoire. Her reflection met her with the precise posture she had maintained through seventeen curtain calls, the emerald velvet holding its line despite the frayed hem. The woman in the glass held documentation of a succession she had witnessed through grease-stained complicity. The lie had acquired the specific gravity of archived matter, requiring now the physical maintenance that all solid records demanded. The mirror reflected the complicity until it appeared as a bureaucratic inevitability, a truth beyond the reach of exposure or recantation.

She retrieved the estate keys from the escritoire drawer. They weighed against her palm with the oxidized silver dullness of keys that had opened cellar locks for thirty-seven years. The decanter stood on the sideboard, half-filled with the 1874 vintage that Bernard had embezzled and she had elected not to inventory. She lifted it in her ungloved hand, feeling the tan line pale against her wrist where the glove had rested, and moved toward the door that led downward. The descent could no longer be postponed; the record required verification against the stored bottles, and her silence had become a physical weight that demanded placement among the racks. The cellars waited with the patience of frozen water.
