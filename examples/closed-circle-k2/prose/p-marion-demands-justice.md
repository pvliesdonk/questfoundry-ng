Marion Ashworth reset her pince-nez with her thumb and forefinger, the ink stains on her cuticles darkening the brass hinge. The ledger in her left hand had begun to stiffen, the freeze-thaw cycle of the cellar air crystallizing the margins where Bernard had last written. She stepped forward, placing herself between the escritoire and the hearth where Helena stood with the portfolio extended toward the dying flames.

"The documents remain," Marion said. Her voice occupied the lower register she reserved for theater reviews that closed provincial careers. She did not raise it. "They are evidence in a homicide."

Helena's glove tightened on the leather corner. The emerald velvet had frayed at the seam, threads catching on the brass corner of Bernard's archive. "The police will find nothing but scandal," Helena said. "Bernard's death is the closing of an account. This—" she gestured with the portfolio toward Marion, toward the assembled heirs "—is the ledger of living disgrace."

Marion removed her right glove. The tan line across her third finger showed paler skin, unrecorded in any Bath registry. She extended the bare hand toward the escritoire where the oiled leather portfolio lay open, its gelatin prints catching the gray light from the ice-sealed windows.

"Burn that," Marion said, "and you burn the only record of who stood in these cellars at eleven o'clock. You burn the murderer's motive. You burn justice for a servant you treated as furniture."

Dr. Mott's empty glass left a frozen ring on the mantelpiece where she had set it down. Teddy Bryant's grease-stained hands hung open at his sides. The seventeen heirs had ceased their movement, the pocket watches lowered, the teacups arrested mid-air.

Marion picked up the portfolio. The weight of the paper surprised her; forty years of observation accumulated into a mass no heavier than a theater program. She turned it toward the assembly, the alphabetical tabs visible, the oxidized silver of her own nameplate dulled by the handling of decades.

"I accept the exposure," she said. Her thumb traced the tab marked VANCE, then ASHWORTH. "My assignations. Your embezzlement. His forged prescriptions. The police will inventory every page."

She placed the portfolio on the settle beside Bernard's corpse, aligning the brass corner with the ivory cigarette holder snapped at the stem. The frozen key in the chandelier above them cracked, descending three millimeters until the ice held it suspended, a witness arrested in glass.
