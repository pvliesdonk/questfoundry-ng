Helena adjusted the silk cord behind her left ear. The mask's edge was silver, oxidized to a dark tarnish that matched the andiron's coating. She drew the knot tight against her temple, and the metal lip sliced through the foundation's grease into the skin beneath her eye. She did not flinch. A thin line of blood appeared at her cheekbone, beaded once, and froze into a red crystal before it could track down toward the frayed velvet of her gown. The cold had entered the room so gradually that none had marked its jurisdiction, yet it now performed its administrative function with perfect efficiency, arresting the evidence of her injury before it could stain the emerald fabric.

She touched the frozen bead with her gloved fingertip. The blood detached intact, a small red pill of ice, and she deposited it into the empty port glass on the escritoire. The gesture held too long, as her gestures did, a theatrical pause that had lost its audience but retained its mechanics. Seventeen heirs stood at the room's perimeter, their postures rigid with the awareness that the succession had been fixed by a forged signature and a damaged testament. They did not look at her face.

Helena turned toward the cellar stairs. The mask's edge cut deeper when she smiled, so she did not smile. She descended with her hand trailing the balustrade, the frozen blood melting on her glove's kid leather, leaving a tackiness that would stiffen in the lower temperatures. The first laceration of the evening marked her as the wearer, and the cold entombed the evidence as red ice before she reached the vault door.
