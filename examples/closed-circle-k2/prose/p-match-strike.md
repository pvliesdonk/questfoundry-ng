Helena struck the match. The sulfur head flared and released its distinct odor, sharp against the cold air that carried only the scent of ash and damp wool. She held the flame steady and advanced toward the ice block where it rested three feet from the grate.

The light caught seventeen faces turned toward her, their features exposed in the sudden yellow glow. A gentleman dropped his pocket watch; the crystal shattered against marble. A lady's hand froze at her oxidized silver necklace, the chain tangled in frayed damask. Teddy Bryant stood with his greasy cuffs visible in the illumination, his posture rigid. Marion Ashworth's pince-nez slipped down her nose and she did not adjust them. Dr. Mott's fingers trembled against her medical bag, the brass clasp catching the light.

The heirs had maintained their formation around the dripping chandelier for six hours, preserving the fiction that they were administering an estate rather than awaiting dissolution. The flame revealed what the dying fire had concealed: trembling hands, ink-stained fingers clutching obsolete ledgers, breath coming in shallow intervals. Their composure, which had served as legal scaffolding for their panic, cracked like river ice in a thaw. Someone knocked over a glass. The sound echoed against the frozen windows. No one moved to retrieve the fragments. The assembly stood revealed beneath the chandelier, their assignations and embezzlements illuminated by a sulfur flame that burned toward Helena's fingers with steady chemical precision.
