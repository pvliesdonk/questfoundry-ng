Dr. Elspeth Mott removed her hand from the ledger. The iron on her wrist slid against the escritoire's edge, but the pressure registered as a dull sensation, distant and administrative. She blinked. The gaslight overhead shifted from harsh white to a forgiving gold that pooled upon the mahogany like spilled syrup. The contradiction in the ledger—arsenic versus trauma, chemical stain versus physical fracture—no longer demanded resolution. The uncertainty wrapped itself around her shoulders with the weight of a hospital blanket, soft and institutional.

She became aware that her mouth was dry. The flask in her pocket pressed against her hip, a solid fact warmer than the room. Helena Vance's emerald gloves hung in her peripheral vision, motionless as surgical drapes. Marion Ashworth's stained fingers gripped the wood, but the gesture seemed performed for another room, another epoch. The seventeen heirs had become obsolete account books standing upright in the gray light, their pages filled with entries she no longer needed to audit.

Dr. Mott took a breath. The air tasted of peppermint and dust. She felt the tremor in her hand, but the observation arrived wrapped in cotton, a medical curiosity rather than a professional failure. The calipers lay upon the escritoire, their oxidized silver catching the golden light. She did not reach for them. The fracture in the temporal bone and the gastric discoloration could remain adjacent entries in the ledger of her negligence, reconciled by the simple fact that she no longer wished to reconcile them. The cold had entered her posture, not her skin, and she found she could hold it indefinitely.
