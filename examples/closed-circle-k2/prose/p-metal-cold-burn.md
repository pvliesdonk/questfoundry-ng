The key fell. Marion extended her hand and caught it before the assembly could react, closing her fingers around the frozen metal. The cold struck immediately, not as chill but as burn, searing the palm beneath her glove with the intensity of oxidized silver held to a flame. She did not drop it. She held it at the length of her arm, feeling the heat drain from her blood into the key's core, the temperature transmitting through the kid leather with perfect fidelity.

She peeled the glove back. A welt rose in the shape of the bow, red against the white flesh, blistering at the edges where the metal had pressed. The mark clarified slowly, like ink spreading through water-damaged paper. She turned her hand to display the injury to the seventeen heirs, not as appeal but as evidence. They watched the welt form, their breath held in collective suspension, their postures rigid as archival ledgers.

She understood then that the cold was not absence but presence, a hostile entity that lived in the objects they had handled. The key was its envoy. She transferred it to her left hand, though the welt throbbed, and felt the chill search for her bones through the new glove. The locked room demanded collateral. It would not yield to those who had not paid in flesh.
