Dr. Elspeth Mott placed her medical bag upon the settle beside the corpse. The leather had stiffened in the cold, and the buckle resisted her thumb. She smelled peppermint rising from the canvas lining, a medicinal sharpness that failed to mask the other scent beneath it. Her hands trembled as she unclasped the hasp.

"You are the only physician present," Helena Vance said. The statement carried no inflection; it was a ledger entry, a column of figures that demanded balancing. Marion Ashworth stood behind her, the certificates folded away now, her fingers tapping against her skirt in a rhythm of impatience.

"I am retired," Dr. Mott said. The words emerged as a separate thing from her breath, which clouded and vanished in the chill air. "My license—"

"Is valid until revoked," Marion said. "We have checked."

Dr. Mott looked at the body. Bernard Croft lay with his throat turned upward, the wound sealed by frost into a dark line. His livery had stiffened into an attitude of parade rest. She noted the way his right hand curled, the fingers gray against the damask of his coat. Her own fingers twitched toward the bag's interior, where a flask pressed against the forceps.

"The law requires—" Dr. Mott began.

"The law requires a coroner," Helena said. "The storm requires you."

Above them, a floorboard creaked. Dr. Mott glanced toward the ceiling, where the plaster showed a water stain in the shape of a continent. Footsteps moved across the upper corridor, hesitant, then more certain, retreating from the cellar stairs. Teddy Bryant had abandoned the assembly to pace the upper house, his borrowed tweed brushing against the walls in the dark.

Dr. Mott opened her bag. The instruments lay wrapped in cloth that had yellowed at the edges. She withdrew a scalpel and held it up to the light from the window. The blade showed no tarnish; she had maintained it despite the dissolution of other standards. The metal caught the gray illumination and threw it back onto Bernard's frozen face.

"I will need the room cleared," Dr. Mott said. Her voice had steadied, falling into the cadence of professional remove, though her thumb still trembled against the instrument's handle. "And a lamp placed to my left. The cold affects the coloration of tissues, and I will require accurate notes."

Helena nodded. Marion hesitated, then followed her toward the drawing-room door. Dr. Mott waited until their footsteps faded. She set down the scalpel and reached again into the bag, past the forceps, until her fingers closed around glass and liquid warmth. She did not drink yet. She placed the flask upon the settle, within reach, beside the obsolete account book someone had left open to a page of debts.

The scalpel gleamed. She turned back to the body, and the cold closed around her like an archive door sealing shut.
