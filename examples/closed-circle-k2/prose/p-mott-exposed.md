Dr. Elspeth Mott shifted her weight from one foot to the other. The floorboards creaked beneath her boots, a record of her station beside the draped form on the settle. She gripped the medical bag’s handle; the brass fixtures had chilled against her skin, and the leather exhaled peppermint against her wrist. Her fingers spasmed once, then steadied.

The investigator entered from the vestibule. He held papers bound with twine in his left hand, the cellar ledger wedged beneath his right arm. His boots deposited slush on the marble in intervals that matched the dripping from above. He stopped four feet from Dr. Mott and consulted the uppermost sheet.

"Your handwriting," he said.

Dr. Mott’s thumb found the bag’s seam. Across the room, Teddy Bryant maintained his posture against the mantelpiece, his shoulders squared toward the assembly as they had been since his confession. Grease marked his cuffs in dark crescents where he had handled the port bottles. His hands hung at his sides, the fingernails rimmed with black from the cellars.

"The prescription for the patriarch," the investigator continued. "Dated three days before his death. Bernard recorded the delivery to the east wing."

Dr. Mott calculated the distance to the cellar door. The heirs had rearranged themselves when the investigator entered; they now formed a barrier between her and the exit. She lowered the bag onto the settle beside Bernard’s covered form. The brass clasp clicked against the wood.

Teddy stepped forward. His borrowed tweed pulled at the seams. "I struck him," he said. "I have stated this."

The investigator did not turn. "Your hands bore residue from the 1874 vintage. The examiner found no cellar matter on the wound. Dr. Mott’s bag contained the scalpel that matched the incision recorded in the autopsy notes."

Dr. Mott reached for the bag’s clasp. Her hands trembled; she withdrew them and clasped them behind her back. The tremor migrated to her shoulders. Above, the chandelier dripped, and she calculated that the frozen key had descended another three millimeters since Helena had withdrawn the match. Ice fractured along a new axis with a sound like splitting slate.

"Your confession was declined," the investigator told Teddy. He addressed the room. "The chauffeur attempted to pervert the inquiry. The physician administered the fatal dosage and concealed the instrument."

The heirs exhaled as one. Their breath rose in a visible cloud toward the cracked plaster. Dr. Mott observed that Marion Ashworth had dropped her ledger; it lay open at page eleven on the Persian rug, the ink bleeding into the fibers where the thawed water had pooled. No one retrieved it.

Dr. Mott stood with her hands secured behind her back. The assembly observed her without courtesy. She understood that her medical authority had served as the final ledger entry, the account now closed. The room’s temperature had not changed, yet she perceived the precise moment when the pretense of her competence shattered, leaving only the bare record of her actions.
