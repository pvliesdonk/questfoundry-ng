Dr. Mott's hands rose to adjust the collar of her coat. The irons shifted against her wrists and the left hand betrayed her. It trembled with a motion distinct from the rattle of the metal, a fine oscillation that traveled up the metacarpals and transmitted itself to the brass clasp of her medical bag. The bag swung from her fingers and the peppermint scent wafted across the space between her and the seventeen heirs.

She observed the tremor as if it belonged to another body. The assembly noted it too. Their faces held the stillness of archival dust settling on obsolete account books. Dr. Mott attempted to still the hand by pressing it against the leather, but the tremor continued beneath the surface, oxidizing her composure into something tarnished and visible. The constable touched her elbow to resume the escort, and she realized she had halted at the threshold of the frost-locked corridor.

Her medical authority had resided in the precision of her gestures, in the steadiness required when she held the scalpel or signed the prescription. That authority now lay abandoned, a frayed damask thread unraveling from the hem of her professional demeanor. She did not look back at Teddy Bryant. She let the constable guide her forward, and the bag slipped from her fingers onto the settle beside Bernard's form, where the brass clasp aligned with the ash on the rug. The tremor persisted as the corridor absorbed her.
