Helena Vance calculated the ledger of their mutual ruin. She held the documentation of fourteen months of Pimlico assignations; Marion Ashworth held the review that had remained unpublished for three years. If Helena placed the portfolio upon the escritoire, Marion would place the review upon the salver. Both women would depart the house with their standing dismantled, their archives reduced to identical ash. The calculation permitted no fractional advantage. She returned the glove to her left hand and smoothed the emerald velvet over her wrist. The frayed hem brushed her slipper as she turned from the assembly.

The staircase to the lower regions descended through a passage unheated since November. Her breath marked the air in discrete exhalations that faded before reaching the stone risers below. She counted twenty-three steps. The wine cellar maintained a temperature of four degrees; she knew this from the household accounts she had audited in September. The vault door stood ajar. Beyond it, Teddy Bryant stood with his back to the entrance, addressing the racks of 1874 and 1876 vintages as though they constituted a bench of magistrates. His borrowed tweed jacket hung loose across his shoulders. He held a sheet of paper before him with both hands, the grease under his fingernails visible even at her distance. He said his name and former occupation, then paused and began again from the third word. The paper trembled. She remained at the threshold, observing the marks his fingers had left upon the page. He began a third time. She did not announce her presence.
