Bernard Croft's departure registered as a pressure change in the vestibule. The outer door sealed behind his spine with a mechanism that had not been oiled since the last thaw. Helena Vance stood on the third riser and counted the seconds until the draft ceased to disturb the damask drapes. She ascended the remaining stairs. The drawing room door stood ajar by the width of a ledger page. She entered.

Marion Ashworth stood before the hearth where the fire had not yet settled into coals. The logs still shifted, shedding sparks onto the andiron. She held her kid gloves in one hand and a leather portfolio in the other. Ink stained her fingertips. She did not turn when Helena entered.

Helena closed the door. The latch engaged with a click that lingered in the cooling air. She set her own portfolio upon the escritoire. The documentation of Marion's fourteen-month assignation with the Pimlico solicitor lay within, alphabetized and dated with Bernard's characteristic precision. Marion's unpublished review of Helena's final performance occupied a similar position in Marion's grasp.

"You hold my obituary," Helena said. "I hold your divorce papers."

Marion's shoulders stiffened. She placed her gloves upon the mantelpiece. The kid leather left a damp outline on the marble.

"We burn them," Helena said. "Both. Tonight."

Marion turned. Her pince-nez caught the firelight. Her gaze moved to the bell cord, then back to Helena. Her fingers traced the edge of the portfolio with a motion that suggested arithmetic.

"The review first," Marion said.

"Simultaneous," Helena said. She withdrew the matches from her sleeve. "Or not at all."

Marion extended her portfolio toward the grate. The pages bent under her grip. Helena moved to meet her, the damask of her gown brushing against the frayed hem. They stood before the unsettled fire, each holding the instrument of the other's destruction, waiting to see who would release first.
