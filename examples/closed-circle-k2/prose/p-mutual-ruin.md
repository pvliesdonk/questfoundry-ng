Marion stood at the escritoire. The certificates lay spread upon the wood, their edges stiffening in the cold. Across the Persian rug, Helena Vance held the leather portfolio against her chest. The emerald velvet of her gown had brushed through the ash that marked the path from the dead hearth; gray dust smeared the frayed hem. The embers had completed their collapse into cinders. No heat rose from the iron grate.

Helena stepped forward. The portfolio's brass corner caught the dim light from the windows. Marion shifted her weight. She calculated the distance between the documents on the escritoire and the nearest flame, but the hearth contained only cooling ash. The certificates in her possession recorded a birth three years too late and two districts east of the required registry. Helena's portfolio contained fourteen months of assignation dates recorded with Bernard's archival precision, each entry corroborated by railway receipts and hotel ledgers. Each woman held sufficient evidence to annihilate the other's social standing.

"You intend to read that review," Helena said. Her voice carried the crack of crystal under pressure.

"You intend to file that affidavit," Marion said.

Helena's gesture toward the certificates remained extended three seconds longer than courtesy allowed. The gesture trembled. Marion observed the frayed threads of the emerald hem suspended motionless above the rug, the ash clinging to the nap of the velvet. The silence between them developed the texture of ice upon tarnished silver.

Marion drew a sharp inhalation. She calculated the time required to feed the certificates to the dying embers, the likelihood that Helena could reach the bell cord before the act completed. The ink on her fingers had transferred to the edges of the papers, leaving smudges that resembled the frost patterns on the window glass. To destroy the certificates was to preserve the portfolio; to preserve both was to remain locked in the room until the thaw released the authorities who would seize all documents as evidence.

Helena stepped closer. She placed her portfolio upon the escritoire beside the certificates. The leather binding touched the stiff paper. Neither woman retreated. The assembly beyond the door had ceased its murmur, or the frost had thickened enough to muffle sound. Marion's breath shortened. She observed the way the cold had contracted the silver inkwell cap until the lid no longer fit flush against the glass base.

The documents on the escritoire formed a complete inventory of mutual ruin. The price of any movement was now absolute. They stood in attitudes of reciprocal vulnerability, and the ash upon the rug marked the only boundary that remained between them.
