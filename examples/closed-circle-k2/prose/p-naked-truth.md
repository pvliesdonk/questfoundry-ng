Marion climbed the stairs. Below, Helena stood at the escritoire, the emerald velvet of her sleeve trailing near the tongs and braziers arranged before the ice. The seventeen heirs had retreated to the perimeter, their postures forming a silent ledger of debt and embarrassment. Marion did not look back. The draft from the great hall followed her up the steps, carrying the scent of melted wax and dust.

She entered the study where the wall safe stood behind a panel of frayed damask. The portfolio lay heavy in her arms, the oiled leather damp from the hall's condensation, its brass corners cold against her palms. She placed it inside the safe, arranging the pages with the precision of an alphabetical entry, ensuring the survival of Bernard's catalogued indiscretions. The lock clicked shut with the finality of a balanced account. She withdrew the key and slipped it into her bodice, where the cold brass settled against the welt shaped like the bow. Her ink-stained fingers brushed the fabric once to ensure the weight did not shift.

The archive would survive the chaos as court evidence now, preserved to fuel the scandal that approached with the breaking storm. The great hall stood exposed and drafty beneath her, its civility intact but its secrets catalogued in the pages she had locked away. Frost sealed the windows from within, and the oxidized silver of the fallen chandelier armature lay tarnished against the Persian rug, marking the boundary of the assembly seventeen feet below. The damask covering Bernard on the settle remained undisturbed. She did not look down at them.

Behind a locked door in this upper room, the frozen will waited in its block of ice, determining which heir would own the ruins.
