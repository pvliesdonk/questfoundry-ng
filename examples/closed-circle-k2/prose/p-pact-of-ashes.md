Helena Vance rose from the settee. The frayed damask released her emerald velvet slowly, as if the upholstery calculated the cost of separation. She crossed to the hearth where no fire had been laid. The grate held only ash from previous days, gray and settled as an obsolete account book closed for the final time.

Marion Ashworth stood by the tea table. Her ink-stained fingers touched the unpublished review, then the portfolio containing the documentation of assignations. The critic's pince-nez reflected nothing. She said, "Simultaneously."

Helena understood. She reached for the tinder box on the mantel. Her movements transmitted themselves through the room with the exactitude of a ledger entry. She struck the match. The flame consumed the sulfur with a brief, administrative efficiency.

They held their documents over the unlit grate. Helena's portfolio dangled its pages; Marion's review curled at the edges from the heat rising before ignition. Neither woman spoke of the devastating critique contained in the folded paper. Neither named the Pimlico solicitor in Helena's documentation. The silence calculated itself, establishing a reciprocal jurisdiction where exposure of one guaranteed exposure of the other.

Marion released her paper first. It drifted toward the ash. Helena opened her fingers. The portfolio fell. Both documents settled among the gray remains of previous fires. Helena knelt, extending the match. The flame touched paper. The pages darkened, curled, and became illegible. Marion's devastating assessment of Helena's final performance, the lines that would have terminated her theatrical legacy, transformed into carbon and rose through the flue. The documentation of Marion's fourteen-month assignation followed, the ink weeping into the ash until the dates and names became indistinguishable from the residue of burned coal.

Helena stood. She brushed dust from her emerald gown. The tan line on her palm remained, but the evidence of her knowledge had vanished into the chimney's updraft. Marion adjusted her pince-nez. The wire left no mark on her nose. They had buried each other's secrets beneath the oxidized silver of the tea table and above the frayed damask of the settee, preserving Helena's stature as a grand dame through the simple expedient of mutual complicity. The fire died behind the screen. The cold remained in the objects, never in the air, and their new bond calcified like ice on the inside of a window, transparent and unbreakable.
