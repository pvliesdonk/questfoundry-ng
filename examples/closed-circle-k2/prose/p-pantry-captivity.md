Teddy felt the pressure in the room shift before they touched him. Seventeen heirs moved as one ledger entry corrected in red ink. Someone took his elbow. The tweed sleeve resisted, then surrendered.

They guided him through the service door to the pantry. The single bulb swayed above the flour bins, casting shadows that moved like disturbed archival dust. He stood rigid against the ice-crusted barrels while Helena Vance removed the curtain cords from the window dressings. Her emerald velvet gloves trembled as she wound the fabric around his wrists. The oxidized silver grommets clicked against the tins of baking powder.

"Your fraud terminates here," someone said. Teddy did not turn to identify the speaker.

The cords bit into his skin, frayed damask fibers rough against the grease stains beneath his nails. He slumped against the flour bins, his shoulder blades hitting the paper sacks with a sound like obsolete account books dropped from a height. The heirs arranged themselves in the narrow space, their breathing visible in the cold air.

Marion Ashworth descended from the upper landing. She moved past the assembly without her pince-nez, her fingers pressed against her bodice where the safe key had left a welt shaped like its bow. She reached the wall safe beside the pickled herring barrels and tested the lock mechanism. The metal resisted, then held. She checked it again.

Teddy watched her hands tremble. He understood they had catalogued seventeen failures, debts, and assignations in Bernard's portfolio, and that the evidence upstairs would persist beyond the melt to incriminate them all. His compulsive clock-watching had ceased; he could not see the mantel clock from this angle.

"Remain still," Helena said. Her posh accent held, but her thumb twitched against the cord's end.

Teddy squared his shoulders toward the assembly. The flour dust coated his borrowed tweed, turning the fabric the color of tarnished silver. He calculated the precise weight of the water accumulating on the ceiling above, the critical mass of droplets waiting to fall. The heirs maintained their rigid postures, their pretense of gentility collapsed into this physical restraint.

Marion checked the safe lock a third time. The sound of metal against ice carried through the pantry.

Teddy closed his eyes. The chill lived in the objects around him: the frozen water pipes, the oxidized silver latches on the tea chests, the archival dust of seventeen years of service he had attempted to claim as his birthright. He waited for the droplet to fall.
