Helena stood at the escritoire. The officers had removed the arrested heirs, leaving the alphabetized pages scattered across the marble where they had fallen. She retrieved a cup from the sideboard. The porcelain was thin and traced with hairline cracks that ran like entries in a debtor's index across its glaze. The tea within had cooled to the temperature of the room, forming a meniscus like tarnish on silver. She lifted it toward her lips.

Her fingers had grown rigid from holding the portfolio against her bodice through the long hours of exposure. The bone china handle received the pressure of her grip without complaint for three seconds. Then it separated from the bowl with a sound like archival vellum tearing under strain. The cup fell. The fragments scattered across the Persian rug, mixing with the ash that still marked the path toward the settle.

Helena looked at the handle still cupped in her palm. The break was clean and white, exposing the porous interior of the ceramic where the glaze had not reached. She set the handle upon the open ledger beside the escritoire. The gesture completed itself without her volition, her composure cracking like ice under weight. The remaining heirs observed her hand retract from the porcelain. The room held its breath, and the silence that followed was no longer the silence of civil restraint but of a seal broken, acknowledging that the violence concealed by etiquette had finally surfaced.
