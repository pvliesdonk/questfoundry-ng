She placed the poker against the andiron. The brass handle rang against the oxidized silver, a brief note that died in the ash bed. She withdrew the portfolio from beneath her arm. The leather had stiffened to the consistency of frozen bindings, the kind that preserved obsolete accounts in closed repositories. Her fingers found the clasp and released it.

The documents lay inside, arranged in their prescribed order. She removed them with her left hand, feeling the weight shift from weapon to witness. The paper had yellowed at the edges, fraying like damask left too long in strong light.

She extended her arm toward the assembled heirs. The motion framed the fireplace behind her, where Bernard's blackmail materials had blackened to ash. The carbonized foolscap held its shape, resting in the andiron where no draught could lift it. The room had already begun to seal against the storm.

The seventeen heirs maintained their perimeter. Their postures remained rigid as the ice sheeting the windows, which had begun to spider with new fractures. She held the papers at arm's length. The edges trembled.

"Teddy Bryant," she said. Her voice carried the crack of crystal under pressure. "You are the impostor."

Teddy stood near the escritoire. His shoulders squared toward the assembly. Grease marked his borrowed tweed. His nails, visible against his clenched hands, showed the black of his former trade. He said nothing.

The outer doors surrendered to the wind. The storm sealed the great hall, trapping the assembly with her fabrication and the destroyed truth in the andiron. The heirs stood motionless. Helena held the pose, her arm extended, until the silence completed itself.
