Dr. Elspeth Mott drew the penknife across the nib with a stroke she had learned in surgical lectures thirty years prior. The blade met the shaft at a precise angle and produced a rasp like bone grinding against bone. She rotated the quill one quarter turn and drew the knife again. The sound continued, rhythmic and dry, filling the vault beneath the swinging bulb where the assembly stood without motion. The ledger lay open on the escritoire before her, its pages smudged with freeze-thaw damage that blurred the ink into obsolete account shorthand.

Her hands trembled. She steadied her right wrist with her left fingers, applying pressure as she had once applied tourniquets in the wards. The quill shavings accumulated on the leather surface, curling like fragments of archival dust dislodged from forgotten bindings. She had placed her medical bag on the floor beside the chair, the peppermint insufficient to mask the gin she had not yet consumed but would require before touching Bernard's stiffened corpse again. Her thumbnail tested the point against her coat sleeve, finding the resistance satisfactory.

Teddy Bryant remained standing by the settle with his hands unclasped. Marion Ashworth stepped back from the escritoire, her ink-stained fingers leaving no mark on the tarnished brass. Helena Vance stood at the window, her emerald velvet glove pressed against the sash where rime had sealed the frame shut. None of them spoke. Dr. Mott dipped the quill into the inkwell, drew a single line in the margin to test the flow, and waited for the room to resume its interrupted motion.
