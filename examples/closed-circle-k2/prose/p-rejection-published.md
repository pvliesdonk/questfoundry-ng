Marion adjusted her pince-nez. The wire frames settled against the bridge of her nose with the familiar pressure of administrative precision. She held the folded review between her ink-stained fingers and surveyed the assembly.

Helena stood beside the escritoire. Her emerald velvet glove rested upon the damask surface, the frayed hem of her gown brushing the frozen ring left by Dr. Mott's glass. The documentation of Marion's assignations lay spread among the heirs like a ledger opened to accounts now rendered obsolete by common knowledge. Marion calculated that silence had ceased to purchase anything of value.

"I will read the review," Marion said.

She did not wait for response. The paper crackled as she unfolded it. She drew a breath—not the sharp inhalation that preceded her judgments, but a steady expansion of the lungs that pressed her ribs against the corset's architecture. The seventeen heirs turned. Dr. Mott's empty pockets trembled against her hips. Teddy Bryant's grease-stained cuffs hung motionless at his sides.

Marion's voice assumed the register she reserved for the public record. "The performance at the Imperial Theatre on the fourteenth of January exhibited the technical competence of an amateur reciting in a provincial parlor. The gestures extended beyond their appropriate duration. The voice, formerly capable of cracked crystal resonance, now produced tones that wavered between keys without committing to either. The interpretation suggested an embarrassment that should have died unrecorded, preserved only by the mechanical apparatus of the modern stage and the misplaced loyalty of an audience too polite to vacate the premises."

The words settled over the room like archival dust disturbed from ledger covers.

Helena's glove moved from the escritoire. Her hand rose halfway to her throat and arrested there, the gesture held too long, oxidized silver of her bracelet catching the dim light. The seventeen heirs exhaled as a single organism. A gentleman dropped his pocket watch; the crystal shattered against the marble, scattering fragments that resembled ice across the floor. A lady struck the escritoire; the inkwell rattled but did not tip. The heirs lost their rigid spacing and clustered toward the center of the room, their alliance of propriety dissolving into mutual panic.

Marion refolded the review along its existing creases. She placed the document upon the escritoire beside the damp portfolio. Her ink-stained fingers left a precise smudge on the paper, marking the completion of the account.
