The oxidized silver band on Helena's left hand caught the chandelier light and held it, a frozen gleam against the damask of her skirt. She had worn it for thirty-seven years. The stone faced upward, reflecting the assembly of seventeen heirs who stood in their rigid postures around the hall, their silence a ledger entry noting depreciation. The ring had signified a contract; now it marked only inventory.

She understood that her marriage to the patriarch had been a performance sustained by mutual blackmail and the careful balancing of accounts. The emerald velvet of her gown, frayed at the hem, brushed the escritoire as she shifted her weight. The documentation in her portfolio—Marion's assignations, her own theatre receipts—seemed suddenly as obsolete as the accounting shorthand in the plaster cracks. She had maintained the illusion of social order through the precise choreography of gesture and interruption, but the ring's glare exposed the calculation beneath the courtesy.

Helena removed the glove from her right hand. The gesture took twelve seconds. She placed the bare hand on the escritoire beside the ledger, the tan line stark against the ink stains. The seventeen heirs turned their faces toward her movement. She did not speak. The ring continued to catch the light, meaningless and bright, as the room's civility dissolved into the grotesque geometry of exposed debts and frozen keys. The assembly recognized that the performance had ended; the curtain had fallen on nothing.
