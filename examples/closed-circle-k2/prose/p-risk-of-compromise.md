Teddy observed the tremor in Dr. Mott's hands. The sharp smell of peppermint that surrounded her medical bag masked something sharper. He calculated that if she touched the body in this state, the incision would wander and the evidence would blur. The diagnosis would become fiction, and the fiction would serve whoever held the pen. He realized that her intoxication risked the destruction of forensic proof and a false determination of cause. Any incision she made would lack precision, and the wound matter would mix with her error.

He descended the twenty-three steps to the wine cellar. The temperature dropped with each step. The lone bulb swayed overhead in the drafts, casting shadows across the 1874 and 1876 vintages. His breath formed visible clouds that rose toward the ceiling where ice coated the stone, and water had gathered to a critical weight above. He located the disturbed dust between the port bottles. His fingers, marked by grease, brushed the frayed damask concealing the flat metal flask. He lifted it, felt the weight indicating three-quarters full, and set it down. He reached for the fire iron standing against the rack. The oxidized silver of the handle had dulled to grey, matching the archival dust on the obsolete account books nearby. He held the iron above the glass. The ice crusting the barrel bases glinted in the swinging light. He stood with his spine fixed and his weight distributed evenly, facing the assembled bottles, and weighed the cost of smashing the supply against the complicity of keeping it for leverage. The iron trembled slightly in his grip.
