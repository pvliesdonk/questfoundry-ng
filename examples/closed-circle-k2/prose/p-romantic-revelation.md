Dr. Elspeth Mott withdrew the photograph from her medical bag. The silver gelatin had oxidized at the edges, tarnishing the emulsion to a bruised grey, and the image showed her own profile bent over an account book in the library three days prior. She had not known he observed her. On the reverse, in ink that matched the grease under his fingernails, he had written the date and her initials with a precision that suggested inventory rather than affection. The notation recorded her solitude with the same clerical detachment Bernard applied to wine counts. She pressed the card into his trembling hands.

Teddy looked at the image. His posh accent slipped as he drew breath. He did not deny the observation. His fingers closed around the photograph, hiding the dedication from the assembly. The gesture confirmed what the timestamp and the angle of the shot implied: he had documented her movements with the same compulsive attention he applied to clock-watching, collecting her presence as evidence of a devotion he had never declared aloud.

She stepped back. Her empty glass remained on the mantelpiece where she had placed it, but her thumb found the bag's clasp and withdrew, the reflex uncompleted. She understood now that his confession regarding the patriarch's death was not improvisation but calculation. He intended to hang for a crime she had committed. The distinction between his guilt and her own mattered less than the ledger of his devotion, balanced against her continued existence with the exactitude of obsolete account books.

The assembly shifted. Marion Ashworth refolded her ledger's corner. Helena Vance adjusted her grip on the portfolio. Ice cracked in the chandelier above, and a droplet fell through the breath-fog to strike the parquet with a sound like a finger snapping. The inquiry ceased to be a search for justice and became instead a record of contaminated passion, preserved in ice and silver nitrate, awaiting the thaw that would expose them both.
