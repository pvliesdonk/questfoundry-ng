Teddy sealed the cellar door behind him. The bolt drew home with a sound of finality that suggested ledgers closing. He stood in the passage and brushed the rack grease from his cuffs, leaving a smear on the borrowed tweed that matched the tarnish on the oxidized silver buttons. The draft ceased.

Helena Vance entered the Great Hall from the vestibule. Her emerald velvet gown caught on the doorframe; she freed the frayed hem without pause or acknowledgment. She advanced toward the chandelier where ice sheathed the frozen key. The seventeen heirs remained stationed around the settle, their postures rigid as the archival dust that settled on their shoulders. Bernard's form lay beneath the damask covering, and beside it Dr. Mott's medical bag rested on the marble, the brass fixtures dulled by the ash that marked the path toward the escritoire. The copper taste of suspended violence lingered in the frozen air.

The house bifurcated. Below, the single exposed bulb continued its arc above the ice-crusted barrels, and water gathered to critical droplet mass on the cellar ceiling coated in black ice. Above, frost obscured the inside of the windows and the dying fire scattered cinders across the Persian rug. Helena extended her gloved hand toward the suspended ice. The gesture held too long, theatrical and precise, rendered absurd by the tarnished grandeur of the display. The heirs watched without speaking. The pretense of manners cracked, and the absurdity of the frozen spectacle eroded the remaining civility until only the precision of the ice remained, and the locked door, and the darkness that waited below with its cargo of dust and silence.
