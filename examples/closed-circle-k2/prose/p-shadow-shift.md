Teddy heard the step first. It crossed the ceiling plaster above the Great Hall with a rhythm distinct from the settling of old timbers. The shadows shifted as the figure passed overhead, elongating against the frieze like ink bleeding into the margin of an obsolete account ledger.

Seventeen heirs had been arranging their charges of embezzlement and false identity, their voices climbing and falling in the arrested air. Now they stopped. Marion Ashworth's kid glove slipped from her hand and struck the parquet. Dr. Mott's fingers rested motionless on the brass clasps of her medical bag. Helena Vance stood with her emerald velvet gown hem frayed, her posture rigid as tarnished silver.

Someone above stepped again. The chandelier, its frozen burden still sheathed in ice upon the oxidized silver armature, swayed in the silence. The assembly maintained their formal stances, shoulders aligned and chins lifted, but the pretense of order exacted its first visible strain. A gentleman's hand hovered near his pocket watch without checking the hour. A lady's fingers brushed the escritoire's edge, seeking support. Another heir stood with his weight shifted to one foot, frozen mid-transfer. They did not speak. The vocabulary of interruption and laconic threat dissolved into breath held so long it became a physical comedy frozen mid-gesture.

The shadow paused above them. The step hesitated, then moved on toward the east wing. The room remained sealed, a vault of ice and archival dust where cold lived in postures and the silence accumulated like water to critical mass.
