Helena stood motionless as the light decayed. The shadows advanced across the Aubusson carpet in discrete increments, elongating the legs of the rosewood chairs until the furniture appeared to shift its station. She observed the phenomenon without turning her head. The portfolio remained pressed against her ribs, its brass corners indenting the velvet.

The room's geometry faltered. The distance between the settle and the trestle table seemed to contract, then expand, as the dying firelight destabilized the fixed dimensions. The chandelier above—the frozen arms encased in their sheath of ice—caught the last amber wavelengths and refracted them into spectral fractures that danced against the wainscoting. The suspended key gleamed within its gelid prison.

Marion adjusted her pince-nez. Dr. Mott set down her empty glass with a click that registered too loudly in the silence. Teddy's hands remained clasped behind his back, though his shoulders had risen a fraction toward his ears. Helena noted these adjustments as evidence of the same calculation she performed: the cost of maintaining posture against the room's silent declaration that nothing here remained permanent. The frost on the inner windows had advanced another millimeter since she descended; the rime sealed them within a chamber that pretended to architecture but functioned as a vault.

She shifted her weight. The portfolio's leather warmed against her glove, though the brass maintained its cold. The furniture settled into apparent stillness, but the afterimage of movement lingered in her peripheral vision, suggesting that the objects had simply paused mid-gesture, awaiting the next dissolution of light.
