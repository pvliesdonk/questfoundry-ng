Helena crossed the remaining distance to where Teddy stood. The poker's weight settled in her grip, a cold ledger entry against her palm. She extended the iron toward him, handle first. He received it without speaking, his grease-marked fingers closing around the tarnished brass.

She turned toward the andiron. The ice block rested there, its surface webbed with cracks that revealed document lines like entries bleeding through damask. She retrieved the tongs from the stand with her left hand. The metal chilled her palm through the emerald velvet.

The heirs stood around the settle, their arrangement fixed as oxidized silver in a closed drawer. Helena gripped the ice block through the tongs, lifting it from the grate. The ash beneath remained undisturbed, a bed of carbonized matter that had consumed Bernard's alphabetical divisions. She calculated that this grey particulate secured their conspiracy; the secrets that poisoned the house had been reduced to archival dust, and no record remained to contradict the validation they sought.

She positioned the ice above the ash bed, steadying it for the blow. The frozen water sheathed the testament in reflections that held no warmth.

Teddy raised the poker. The descent was exact, a bureaucratic stroke. The iron struck the frozen will. A fracture propagated from the impact point with the patience of accumulation. The block split. Water seeped onto the andiron, carrying fragments down to the grate where they dissolved among the remains. The document within lay exposed, ink softened but the patronymic legible, determining the succession despite the damage.

He lowered the iron. She released the tongs. The heirs observed the validation of the claim, their faces closed as obsolete account books. Outside, the storm completed its seal of the estate, blocking the roads with snow that accumulated like tarnish on silver, trapping them with the evidence of their complicity.
