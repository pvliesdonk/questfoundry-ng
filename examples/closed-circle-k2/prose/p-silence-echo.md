Dr. Elspeth Mott stood with her hands clasped behind her back. The assembly held their positions without movement. She observed that the investigator had ceased speaking, and the absence of his voice occupied the room as tangibly as the furniture. Seventeen heirs maintained their stations: Marion Ashworth remained bent above the ledger on the rug, her ink-stained fingers hovering over the bleeding text; Teddy Bryant kept his shoulders against the mantelpiece, his grease-marked cuffs visible against the stone; Helena Vance stood beside the escritoire with her portfolio pressed against her emerald velvet bodice.

The chandelier dripped. The sound marked intervals that no one counted. Dr. Mott felt the silence accumulate against her skin like a pressure. She did not shift her weight. The medical bag rested on the settle beside Bernard's draped form, the brass clasp catching the dim light from the dying fire. The peppermint scent had faded from the leather.

Someone exhaled. The breath rose visible and slow toward the cracked plaster, joining the mist of seventeen other exhalations that had preceded it. The frozen key in the chandelier descended another fraction; ice cracked with a sound like a ledger snapping shut. No one spoke. The stillness had weight, a physical mass that pinned each witness to their assigned coordinates in the vault of the Great Hall. Dr. Mott understood that the silence was the true verdict, spoken by the room itself.
