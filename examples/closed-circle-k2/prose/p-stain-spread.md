Helena withdrew the document from the portfolio. The damask lining had darkened where the paper rested, the fibres drinking the excess moisture. She pressed a cambric handkerchief to the lower margin, applying pressure with the heel of her hand. The fabric touched the page and absorbed nothing; the water had already settled into the weave, a stain spreading laterally across the patriarch's final clause.

The ink softened beneath the pressure. She lifted the handkerchief and saw that the word following the heir's Christian name had dissolved into a gray bloom, its boundaries indistinguishable from the paper's watermark. She had read it moments before, had held the syllables in her mouth to test their weight, and now the letters existed only in her memory, a reading unsupported by the physical record.

Teddy shifted his weight. The grease on his cuffs caught the light from the dying fire, a dull tarnish on the tweed. She folded the handkerchief to a dry corner and pressed again, but the stain had achieved its final perimeter, a darkened oval that respected neither margin nor line. The document declared the estate's transfer to an heir whose patronymic now read as a smudge, a canceled entry in a ledger that would require external confirmation to execute.

Seventeen heirs stood in their obsolete postures, their gloves hanging at their sides like uncracked spines. Helena replaced the document in the portfolio. The succession remained determined, but the precision of the record had been compromised by the same entropy that claimed the ice block. She closed the clasp. The portfolio weighed in her hands like a bottle emptied to its dregs.
