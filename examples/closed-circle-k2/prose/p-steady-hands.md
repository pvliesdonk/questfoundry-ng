Dr. Mott descended the twenty-three steps. The iron on her wrists weighed against the movement, a cold ledger entry binding her to the stone. The bulb above the racks swung in a draft from the sealed door, and her breath marked the intervals in visible plumes that faded against the ice-crusted barrels. She counted the vintages without speaking, her fingers tracing the oxidized silver caps until they found the gap between the 1874 and the 1876 ports. The dust there had been disturbed; she noted this with the part of her mind that still made clinical observations, filing it beside the tremor that had returned to her hands since the constable applied the irons.

She reached behind the 1876 bottles and found the glass. The gin smelled of juniper and desperation, a clear liquid that promised the temporary repeal of physical law. She did not pause to calculate who had placed it there. She drank deeply, and the burn spread through her chest with the familiar warmth of a frayed damask blanket retrieved from storage. Her hands steadied. The tremor subsided into a manageable vibration that she could disguise as cold.

She turned toward the settle where Bernard lay beneath the frayed covering. Her medical bag rested beside him, the brass fittings aligned with the ash on the rug. She opened the bag and removed her instruments, laying them on the stone ledge with the precision of a woman filing obsolete account books. The scalpel caught the swinging light.

She began the examination. Her speech slurred when she called out measurements, the numbers blurring at the edges like ink on damp linen. She dropped the calipers onto Bernard's frozen flesh, and the metal struck the skin with a sound that echoed against the vaulted ceiling. She retrieved the instrument and attempted to note the depth of the wound, but her handwriting degenerated into illegible scratches that resembled the black ice coating the ceiling above. The peppermint oil on her gloves could not mask the gin rising from her pores. She dropped the scalpel. It clattered against the stone floor, and she bent to retrieve it, her balance shifting dangerously until she steadied herself against the ice-crusted barrel. The water dripping from the ceiling had accumulated to critical mass; it fell onto the back of her neck, but she did not flinch. She resumed her work, her authority dissolving with each measurement she mangled, until the findings she recorded bore no reliable resemblance to the body before her, only to the urgent, steadying burn that had preceded them.
