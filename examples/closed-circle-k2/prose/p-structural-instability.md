He looked up at the oxidized silver arms. The ice sheath had thickened since morning, encasing the crystal in a tarnished sleeve that bent the light into spectral accountings. The groan came not from the frozen water but from the metal above, a bureaucratic protest of weight exceeding ledger entries. The ceiling hooks were original to the house, installed decades before the patriarch's first inventory. Bernard had noted their condition in the book marked 1923, the year of the silver jubilee and the first major leak in the east wing. Now the iron showed rust blooming through the plaster like dust on frayed damask. He calculated the additional mass: ice density multiplied by surface area, plus the frozen key suspended in the central cluster.

He lowered his gaze to the assembly. His voice barely rose above a whisper. He stated that the hooks had corroded through to the core. The structure would not tolerate additional load. Any further accumulation—of ice, of weight, of movement—would shear the fittings from the ceiling joists.

Helena adjusted her grip on the portfolio. Marion stood motionless beside the trestle. Dr. Mott's hand hovered above her medical bag. Teddy Bryant had ceased his pacing near the mantelpiece. The chandelier swayed another three millimeters on its compromised anchor, and Bernard noted the hairline crack advancing across the frozen plaster. He tapped his ivory cigarette holder against the ledger and counted the seconds until physics overruled etiquette.
