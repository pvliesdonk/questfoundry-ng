Helena Vance observed the constable close his notebook. The iron on Dr. Mott's wrists had produced no definitive entry—only gastric discoloration adjacent to cranial fracture, causes listed without reconciliation. The physician's tremor had transmitted itself to the calipers, rendering measurement an administrative fiction. The seventeen heirs retained their semicircle formation, but their attention had decentralized, drifting toward the cellar door and the ice block dripping on the andiron.

Helena touched Marion's elbow. The critic's fingers bore stains that had transferred to the escritoire's edge during the wait. Marion turned. Her pince-nez reflected the gaslight as a flat disc, obscuring the eyes behind the wire. Helena gestured toward the drawing room arch. Marion hesitated, then stepped back from the assembly.

The drawing room contained a hearth where no fire had been laid, and a settee upholstered in frayed damask that released dust when Helena seated herself. Marion remained standing near the window, her wool coat shedding meltwater onto the floorboards. Helena removed her emerald glove. The tan line across her palm marked where the chandelier chain had burned during the ascent. She extended the hand toward Marion, not in greeting, but as evidence of shared exposure.

Marion produced a folded paper from her pocket. The unpublished review. Helena produced her portfolio. The documents lay between them on the oxidized silver of the tea table, two obsolete account books calculating mutual ruin. Marion's fingers touched the edge of her review, then withdrew. Helena's hand hovered over the portfolio. Neither woman reached for the bell cord. The investigation had stalled in the room beyond, but here the accounting proceeded in silence, establishing a private jurisdiction where survival required the cremation of certain ledgers.
