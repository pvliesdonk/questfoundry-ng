Teddy Bryant stepped to the center of the breath-fog. The parquet registered his borrowed shoes with a click that recalled forgotten stage directions. He unclasped his hands and raised them palm-up, the posture of a student awaiting the prompter's cue. The assembly's seventeen faces turned toward him, oxidized silver in the failing light.

"I killed him," he said. His accent held steady at the vowels' edges. "I struck him with the poker from the drawing-room grate. He fell against the wine racks at eleven o'clock." He detailed the angle of the blow, the weight of the iron in his grip, the particular sound of bone against stone. He claimed the weapon remained in the cellars, hidden behind the vintage port where he had placed it after wiping the shaft with his handkerchief. He spoke for four minutes without interruption, furnishing specifics about the patriarch's posture, the direction of the blood's flow, the sequence of his own retreat up the stairs.

Marion Ashworth stepped forward from the settle. She held the ledger at page eleven, her finger resting on the entry that recorded Bernard's presence in the cellars at the hour of death. She did not look at the page. She looked at Teddy's hands. Her gaze traveled from his palms to the mantelpiece where the poker stood, untouched, its tarnish undisturbed by recent use. She said nothing. She refolded the ledger's corner, once, twice, three times. The crease aligned with the crack in the plaster behind her.

Dr. Mott set down her empty glass. The crystal struck the coaster with a precision that arrested the room's attention. She looked at Teddy's fingernails, still rimmed with grease from the automobile, and then at his face. Her expression remained professionally neutral, but her thumb moved to her bag's clasp and withdrew, the gesture incomplete.

Teddy watched Marion map his description against the known facts. He saw her inventory the errors: the weapon's location, the time's impossibility, the absence of blood on his cuffs. She traced the discrepancy in the air with her ink-stained finger, a shorthand glyph of negation. The assembly shifted. The breath-fog settled lower, obscuring the floorboards' divisions. Someone touched an oxidized necklace. The frozen key in the chandelier dripped once, twice.

Teddy maintained his stance, the posture of guilt held one beat too long, awaiting the curtain that would not fall.
