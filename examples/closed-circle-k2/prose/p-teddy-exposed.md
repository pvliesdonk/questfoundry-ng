She had expected monogrammed linen or the stale residue of theatrical greasepaint. Instead, the portmanteau yielded layers of pressed wool and a smell of railway stations, the particular ozone of third-class compartments preserved in tweed. The brass clasps had surrendered to her ink-stained fingers without protest, and now she worked through the strata with the methodical patience she applied to obsolete account books, seeking the patriarch's correspondence amid the artifacts of a borrowed life.

Beneath a collar box she found the documents bound in frayed ribbon, certificates whose embossing had already begun to oxidize at the edges, silver turning to tarnish where thumbs had worried the paper. She held the birth record to the light from the failing lamps. The date inscribed there placed him three years younger than the heir's registry required; the parish named was St. Jude's, Whitechapel, not the Somerset rectory recorded in the family Bible she had consulted two winters prior. The discrepancy sat upon the page with the authority of a final notice, a ledger entry that could not be amended.

She looked up. Teddy Bryant stood by the mantelpiece, his borrowed tweed hanging loose across shoulders accustomed to grease rather than tailoring. His fingers, dark beneath the nails with engine oil, twitched toward his pocket watch though the hour had ceased to matter. He did not meet her eyes. The posh vowels he had employed at dinner had contracted into something harder, more consonantal, a diction appropriate to the address now exposed upon the certificate in her hand.

The papers constituted proof that severed his claim at the root, reducing his legitimacy to a matter of forged ink and borrowed station. She held in her hands the evidence that would unravel the estate's performed order, the single fact that transformed the assembly from heirs and guests into suspects and frauds. The cold had entered the documentation now, freezing the lie into a solid object she could present, display, or conceal. She closed her fingers around the oxidized silver of the truth, feeling the weight of archives that had suddenly become a weapon.
