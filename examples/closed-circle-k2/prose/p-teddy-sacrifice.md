Teddy stood before the settle where Bernard's body lay. The ice in the chandelier had cracked further; another droplet fell and struck the parquet with a sound like a ledger snapping shut. He unclasped his hands and let them hang at his sides, the grease under his fingernails visible against the borrowed tweed of his cuffs.

"I am prepared to confess," he said.

His posh accent held steady for three syllables, then slipped on the final consonant. He did not look at Dr. Mott, though he knew she stood by the mantelpiece with her empty glass and her bag reeking of peppermint. He addressed Marion instead, or perhaps the assembly entire, his gaze fixed on the oxidized silver of Helena's necklace where it caught the dying light.

"The truth will annihilate two lives," he said. "Accept my death and let the matter rest."

Marion held the ledger against her chest. Her ink-stained fingers whitened at the knuckles. Teddy watched her calculate the cost of his offer with the same precision she applied to the water damage in the accounts. Behind him, he heard Dr. Mott's glass move on the mantelpiece, a tremor of contact against tarnished brass.

He stepped forward. The parquet creaked under his weight. He had forgotten his lines in the Duchess of Malfi twenty years past, standing in a warehouse theatre with Helena's voice prompting from the wings. Now he improvised his final performance with the same desperate attention he had applied to clock-watching, synchronizing his gestures to the cracking ice above.

"Hang me," he said. "Burn the certificates. Let the thaw bring only one corpse to the authorities."

Helena adjusted her grip on the portfolio. The damask napkins within concealed documentation that would destroy Marion's standing, but Teddy did not need to see them to know they existed. He understood the arithmetic of sacrifice: one life weighed against two, the balance exact as obsolete account books.

Dr. Mott stepped toward the dying embers. Her medical bag swung at her hip, and Teddy caught the flash of silver scalpel within, the instrument she had not used on Bernard because she dared not drink before the examination. He wanted to tell her to drink now, to take the flask from her bag and swallow the peppermint liquor until her hands stopped trembling. Instead, he maintained his stance, his greasy fingers curled against his palms, awaiting the verdict.

Marion lowered the ledger. Her pince-nez caught the light. She had the photograph now, or the certificates, or the knowledge of the crack in the ice block's southeast face; she had enough evidence to hang them both. Teddy saw the choice calcify in her posture, the rigid alignment of her shoulders as she weighed his noble sacrifice against the brutal arithmetic of fact.

The frozen key in the chandelier dropped another three millimeters. The assembly held its breath. Teddy stood ready to complete his confession, his borrowed identity frozen mid-gesture, awaiting her decision.
