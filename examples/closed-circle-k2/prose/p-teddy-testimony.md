Teddy Bryant turned from the racks. He held the sheet at chest height. The paper had absorbed moisture from the air and buckled between his thumbs. He saw Helena Vance at the threshold, her emerald sleeve visible in the dim light. Seventeen heirs stood behind her on the stairs, their breath forming a single cloud that drifted toward the vaulted ceiling. He lowered the rehearsed statement. He did not need it.

He extended his right hand. The grease under his fingernails caught the lamplight. He cited the gap between the 1874 and 1876 ports. He mentioned a corkscrew left aligned with the third ring of the rack. He described a glass with lip prints standing on the ledge behind the 1875 vintage, the port level reduced by three fingers. He stated that Bernard Croft had descended at eleven o'clock to appropriate wine for his own archive, not to commit homicide. The dust patterns confirmed it.

Marion Ashworth stepped forward from the rear. She held the ledger open to page eleven. Her finger hovered over the entry. Dr. Mott stood beside the settle where Bernard's body lay, her bag clasped before her. Helena Vance removed her glove. The tan line on her finger showed pale against the cellar dimness. No one spoke.

Teddy reached into his pocket. He withdrew the glass and placed it on the stone ledge. The lip prints faced upward, preserved in the cold. He stated that the household had permitted the butler's larceny for decades, trading silence for discretion. He stated that this tolerance had now purchased Bernard's innocence in the matter of the patriarch's death, and his guilt in the matter of the cellar accounts. The embezzlement exceeded four hundred pounds across fourteen years.

Seventeen pocket watches consulted the hour. Marion's ledger trembled. The assembly stood in the cold air, their composure frozen mid-gesture. They had prosecuted no thefts while a murderer walked among them. Their silence admitted the absurdity. The dust settled over the lip prints, and the vault sealed their collective shame in the space between the vintage racks.
