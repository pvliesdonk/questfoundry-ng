Marion counted the chimes. Twelve. The sound flattened against the damask and died. She stood before the grate with her arm extended, and the portfolio trembled in her grip. The pages contained the review she had drafted in January, alphabetized by fault, cross-referenced by line.

Helena held her own documentation at the same angle. The matches rested between her thumb and the striker, unspent. The emerald velvet of her sleeve had frayed at the hem; Marion could see the threads that caught the last light from the embers.

"You counted twelve," Helena said.

"I counted twelve."

Below, in the Great Hall, the assembly of seventeen heirs had ceased their murmuring. The solicitor had entered, or the ice block had cracked. Marion could not see through the door, but she heard the shift in posture, the collective inhalation that preceded legal pronouncement. The deadline was no longer theoretical.

Marion calculated the distance to the bell cord. Seven feet. Two seconds to traverse it, three to summon the assembly, five to destroy Helena's composure with the truth of her final performance. By then, however, Helena would have shouted the dates of Marion's assignations to the stairwell. Both portfolios would survive. Both women would fall.

The arithmetic offered no remainder. Marion looked at the fire. The embers had sunk to a grey that matched the archival dust on her spectacles. She looked at Helena's hand, which had held the pose of a tragedian for forty seconds without tremor.

Marion opened her fingers.
