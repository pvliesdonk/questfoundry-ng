Teddy pressed the nib to the drainage orders. His hand remained steady against the escritoire, though the tremor moved inward toward the bone. He wrote the signature that finalized the estate's transfer, the ink congealing into a darker stain than the water had left on the previous pages. Above him, the plaster cracked along a line that ran from the escritoire to the settle. The grate below the mantel held only ash, the carbonized remains of Bernard's archive coating the andiron. The walls groaned as the thaw shifted their frozen weight. Teddy squared his shoulders toward the empty room, maintaining the posture of an heir who understood theatrical presentation but had never studied load-bearing walls.

He descended to the cellars. The single bulb swung above the ice-crusted barrels. He poured the 1874 vintage into a glass and spoke to the empty racks. His voice lacked the posh accent he maintained upstairs. The thaw dripped through the foundation's cracks, pooling on the black ice that coated the ceiling. The ash of burned secrets lay above in the grate, unable to confirm the identity he had assumed. He raised the glass. The wine did not stop the tremor in his fingers.

He paced the great hall alone. The chandelier lay ruined on the Persian rug, its oxidized silver armature twisted among the debris. The fireplace was cold and empty of evidence, the grate holding only residue. His footsteps echoed against walls that bore his signature on drainage orders and estate ledgers. The house would stand long enough to pass to heirs who would accept the patronymic as administrative fact. The walls acknowledged him as master but would never know his true name. He measured the distance between his performance at the escritoire and the empty grate where proof had once sat. The distance remained absolute, a fixed interval that no signature could collapse.
