Teddy separated his hands. The lubricant on his cuffs had marked the tweed in a manner that recalled the spreading damp beneath the chandelier. Seventeen heirs filled the freezing hall, their arrangement excluding him from their collective stance. Marion Ashworth advanced from the escritoire. She held certificates between ink-stained fingers, the edges aligned at right angles.

"We require documentary proof," she said.

Her voice carried the flatness of a ledger entry, a statement of debit requiring settlement. The tan line on her right hand remained visible, a band of lighter skin against the gray light filtering through the ice-sealed windows. Teddy observed the seventeen sets of eyes directed toward his posture, noting the absence of the courtesy that had governed the previous hours.

"Your birth record," Marion said. She extended the certificates toward the assembly without looking at them. "The registry at Somerset. Or the certificate of the heir."

Teddy remained stationary. The frozen key in the chandelier descended another millimeter, striking the accumulated ice with a sound like a finger tapping glass. He calculated the distance to his portmanteau in the upper corridor, the time required to retrieve the forged papers, the probability that the grease on his fingers would smudge the ink.

"Failure to produce," said a gentleman near the mantelpiece, "constitutes prima facie evidence of fraudulent intent."

The declaration removed the pretense of hospitality. The heirs shifted, creating a boundary between Teddy and the settle where Bernard's body rested beneath the dripping fixture. Dr. Mott hovered over her medical bag, her fingers twitching toward the brass clasp and then withdrawing. Helena Vance stood beside the escritoire, her portfolio abandoned in favor of witnessing the accusation.

"The will remains frozen," someone noted.

The observation established the temporal constraint. The ice block on the andiron retained its integrity despite the fissures in its southeast face; the document within remained unreadable, the succession unresolved. Teddy understood that hesitation would constitute acknowledgment of the fraud, a confession rendered in the silence between the ticking of pocket watches.

He moved his hand toward his breast pocket, then arrested the gesture. The pocket contained only grease stains from the cellar railing he had grasped three hours prior, marks that would not align with the surgical precision required of an heir's wardrobe.

"The documents," Marion said, "or the accusation stands recorded before witnesses."

The chandelier swayed on its oxidized silver chains. A droplet of meltwater struck the Persian rug at the exact point where the ash path from the cellar terminated. Teddy observed the water darken the weave, spreading in a pattern that resembled the bloodstains he had wiped from the poker that morning. The heirs maintained their positions, their breath forming clouds that dissolved before reaching the ceiling.

Teddy stepped toward the center of the assembly. The floorboards creaked beneath his weight, the sound absorbed by the frost that had penetrated the joinery. He calculated the remaining thickness of the ice encasing the testament, the hours until natural thaw, the impossibility of maintaining the deception against documentary evidence.

"Produce the certificates," Marion said, "or forfeit the claim."

The demand hung in the air, colder than the vapor from their mouths. Teddy stood in the space between the chandelier's drip and the escritoire's edge, the fingers that had memorized lines of Webster now required to authenticate a lineage he had stolen from a warehouse theatre. He reached toward his coat with a hand that bore the grease of his true employment.
