Teddy stood at the escritoire in the great hall. He initialed the ledgers with a pen that scratched the page. The east wall shed its molding; a strip of gilt plaster fell to the Persian rug and settled among the ash. His hand wavered. The signature blurred at the edges where grease from his fingernails touched the ink, and the forgery lost definition. Upstairs, the constable examined Marion's key, turning the metal in the light from the landing window. Seventeen heirs maintained their positions around the settle, but their shoulders squared toward the sounds of administrative processing. The collapse of the estate accelerated alongside the exposure of its guests.

He descended to the cellars. The exposed bulb swung above the ice-crusted barrels. Teddy gripped a bottle of vintage port and drank. He knew the portfolio rested in the estate safe upstairs, and that the documents inside contained sufficient leverage to destroy his masquerade. His voice rose, addressing the racks. He realized Bernard's files held proof of his origins, the borrowed tweed and the grease under his fingernails documented in alphabetical precision between embezzlement and fraud. The racks trembled with his grip on the bottles.

Teddy inventoried the fixtures in the great hall. He calculated which creditors would flee when Bernard's files surfaced from the estate safe upstairs. The preservation of blackmail materials ensured the exposure of seventeen indiscretions, and that exposure threatened the estate he had fought to save. The chandelier lay ruined on the Persian rug, its oxidized silver armature twisted. Years later, the National Trust would receive the property, and tourists would queue to view the display case with the reproduction ledger. The house would become a museum of bureaucratic exactitude, preserving the evidence of his origins alongside the tarnished chain of the fallen fixture.
