The ice block had begun to weep. Marion noted the film of water on the mahogany beneath the trestle, the way the document inside the ice had blurred at its edges, the ink feathering into fog. The assembly maintained their intervals around the trestle, preserving the geometries of etiquette even as the cold eroded posture and patience.

Helena stood with the portfolio pressed against her ribs. The brass corners indented the velvet of her gown. She proposed moving the ice block closer to the dying grate, trading legibility for speed against the creditors' filings. Dr. Mott objected; she cited the risk of thermal shock and the certainty of water damage spreading through cracks already visible on the southeast face. Teddy remained at the mantelpiece, his hands clasped behind him, offering no method. The debate continued in lowered voices, each proposal weighed against the urgency of succession.

Bernard circled the chandelier. His liver-spotted hands touched the oxidized silver arms, testing the tension of the frozen sheath that encased the crystal. He moved with the deliberation of a man consulting obsolete account books, his pale eyes tracking hairline fractures in the ice above. He paused beneath the northern arm and tilted his head, calculating load-bearing tolerances in a whisper that barely registered. The assembly fell quiet. He stepped back, his ivory cigarette holder tapping once against his thumbnail, and announced that the structure would not survive the night.

The room's genteel order curdled. Marion watched the chandelier sway three millimeters on its corroded hook, the frozen key within shifting like a specimen in tarnished glass.
