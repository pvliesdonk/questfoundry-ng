Teddy stood among the seventeen heirs. The vault pressed them into rigid postures of suspended inventory. He counted the visible assets: eleven ledgers, six medical instruments, one body on the settle, and the chandelier with its cargo of ice. Helena Vance held her portfolio against the emerald velvet bodice; the threads at the hem had frayed to expose the underlying damask. Marion Ashworth stood near the archway with her fingers still curled from releasing the documents she had held. The assembly had arranged itself into rows that suggested obsolete account books, spines aligned by protocol. No oxidized silver remained untarnished on the sideboard; the service had blackened to match the archival dust coating the ledger Marion held against her hip.

A gentleman in the third rank withdrew a chronometer from his waistcoat. His thumb found the stem. The click discharged against the plaster like a percussion cap. Seventeen bodies flinched. The sound exposed the fragility of their composure, marking the passage of time as an active liability in the room's frozen inventory. A lady touched her necklace; the oxidized silver left a smudge on her glove.

Dr. Mott's hand hovered over her medical bag. The brass clasp had left a tarnished semicircle on the marble beside it, dark against the stone. Teddy observed the grease on his own cuffs and calculated the distance to the door. No one spoke. The heir who had checked the watch returned the instrument to his pocket without glancing at its face. The assembly remained fixed in postures of rigid inventory, awaiting the next chime, while the ice above them cracked without falling.
