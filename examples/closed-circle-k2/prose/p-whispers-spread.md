Helena snapped the fan shut. The report cracked across the escritoire's marble surface like ice shifting in a cellar vault. The seventeen heirs turned toward the sound. The foolscap lay spread across the stone like obsolete account books opened for liquidation. Helena withdrew her emerald velvet glove from the surface; the fabric caught on a splintered corner of the portfolio, fraying a thread. She did not adjust it.

A gentleman in cellar-stained cuffs leaned toward the lady in watered silk. His mouth moved against her ear. The lady's hand rose to her collar, fingers finding the tarnished silver clasp of her brooch. She spoke to the gentleman beside her, and his posture shifted, shoulders squaring toward Marion with the alignment of a ledger entry corrected in red ink.

The whispers circulated through the seventeen heirs with the methodical progression of a bottle passed among condemned men. Each recipient added a clause, a refinement, a speculation upon motive. The woman in the corner adjusted her pince-nez and addressed the widowed aunt beside her; the aunt responded by stepping backward, her heel catching on the Persian rug's frayed edge. They separated. The assembly divided into clusters of two and three, each unit maintaining the distance of adversaries across a counting-house floor.

Marion stood beside the escritoire. A gentleman approached her with the inclination of a solicitor offering condolences. His query regarding her health arrived with the precision of a creditor noting the date. Marion's fingers twitched toward her empty ring finger, then arrested. She returned his pleasantry regarding the weather with a statement about the roads. Her voice carried the bureaucratic exactitude of a bankruptcy filing. The gentleman nodded, his eyes calculating the inventory of her compromised assets.

The room had become an archive of suspicion. Every gesture of courtesy now served as interrogation, every withdrawal of a glove as evidence of concealed stains.
