The whistle cut through the windowpane at a pitch that turned glass into a vibrating membrane, a scream rendered in brass and breath. Marion Ashworth lifted her fingers from the safe's dial. The mechanism snapped back to its null position with the sound of an account book snapping shut. Seventeen heirs stood in configurations that had lost their structural integrity. The cousin's hands went still near his pocket where sulfur waited. Helena Vance remained seated, but her palm slipped from her collar to the worn fabric of the chair arm.

The signal continued, measured and official, indicating policemen who would catalogue the chamber's artifacts without regard to the heirs' theatrical composure. Marion noted the alteration in their stances with the precision of a clerk auditing depleted stock. Spines that had held perpendicular to the molding now curved, and the angles of their jaws slackened into admissions of vulnerability. The starch in their shirt fronts seemed to soften with the dissolution of their isolation. The cousin's facial muscles tightened, redirecting his attention toward the entry, reckoning flight above burning. The immunity granted by impassable roads and severed wires evaporated with each repeated cry.

Teddy Bryant, engaged in straightening his coat at the pantry entrance, dropped his arms. The movement stopped before reaching the lapels, suspended incomplete. Marion observed the heirs pivot toward the noise, their expressions displaying the failure of their bureaucratic pretense. The brutality underlying their postures of office had emerged, exposing the violence that their masks of courtesy had concealed, and she reckoned that the coming of outside law would exact the price of their seclusion. Respiration in the chamber grew shallow, confessing that the drifts had served as their sole concealment.

The tone of the whistle shifted, marking nearness, and Marion comprehended that the documents she held concealed beneath her corset would become entries in a docket maintained by strangers.
