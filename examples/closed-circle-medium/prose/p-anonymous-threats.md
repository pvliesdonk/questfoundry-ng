Marta Valen slipped the thin envelope from the brass tray and unfolded the paper. The ink was dry, the handwriting slanted and anonymous. It read only one line, a demand: withdraw the heiress’s name or face consequences. Her eyes narrowed. The lodge’s stone walls had heard louder threats, but this one bore no signature, no address, and it rested heavy on the night table. She tucked the note back into the tray, its edge cold against the wood, and turned to the hearth where the fire sputtered low.

Outside, the storm pressed against the panes, and the wind carried a distant clatter from the sheriff’s office a few miles down the mountain road. A courier in the deputy’s uniform arrived breathless, the leather satchel thudding against his chest. Marta watched him place the satchel on the counter of the small makeshift clerk’s desk, where a row of uniformed men began to file paperwork in neat piles. Their movements were methodical, their faces impassive, as if the ink they stamped could seal any rumor that might escape the lodge.

The note lay untouched beside the hearth, the words waiting in the dim light while the night deepened. Marta felt the weight of the unspoken threat settle over the room, a thin veil between the quiet of the fire and the steady rhythm of the sheriffs’ pens. She kept her hand on the smooth stone rail, ready to answer when the next demand came.
