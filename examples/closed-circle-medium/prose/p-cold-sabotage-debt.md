Simon Kade lifted his eyes from the frosted glass and listened to the murmur of guests complaining about the chill. He noted that the complaints had grown louder since the furnace had sputtered out, each shiver spoken in the low tones of the dining hall. The engineer's remark about a missing intake valve had reached his ears, and he inferred that the cold might be purposeful. A thin thread of doubt wove through his thoughts, but his face remained the picture of steady composure. He kept his posture immaculate, his uniform unmarred by the frost that curled around his boots.

He moved toward the miner who lingered near the hearth, the pipe wrench still clenched in scarred hands. He reached into his coat, the leather pouch nestled against his heart, and slipped it into the inner seam of Cole Duvall's overcoat. The pouch settled with a faint rustle, unnoticed by the man whose shoulders stayed rigid against the draft. Simon's hand lingered a moment, the metal of the clasp cold against his palm, before he withdrew and offered a brief comment. "The night grows colder," he said, his voice low enough to blend with the crackle of coal. He watched the miner glance down, the seam bulging just enough to be felt, and felt a quiet resolve settle within him. The weight of the debt was finally balanced, and the quiet promise of hidden passageways seemed less burdensome. He felt the old iron hinges of the pantry door vibrate faintly as the wind found its way through the cracks, the scent of pine sap mingling with soot. In the dim light, the coal glowed like dying embers, each flicker casting long shadows that crawled across the floorboards. Outside, the blizzard hammered the lodge walls, and the furnace's breath fell to a whisper, leaving the stone corridor to echo with a deeper, ash‑gray cold. He closed his eyes briefly, hearing only the distant howl of the storm, and resolved to watch the furnace's rise with the same vigilance he kept the lodge's secrets.
