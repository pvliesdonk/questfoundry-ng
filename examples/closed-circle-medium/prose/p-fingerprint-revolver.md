Marta traced the outline of the revolver with her eyes, the polished walnut catching the lamplight. The barrel bore a set of prints she recognized from the night she had cleared the bar, the pattern identical to the ridge she left on the glass when she poured tea. She felt a cold knot tighten in her stomach, knowing the metal could bind her name to a murder she had not committed. Marta turned from the table and slipped into the narrow hallway that led to the Highpine Library. The door creaked on its iron hinges as she pushed it open, and the dim lamp inside threw a weak circle of light onto the dust‑laden shelves. Rows of vellum‑bound volumes loomed like silent sentinels, their spines blackened by age. Above the stacks a brass fire alarm hung, its glass cover dulled by years of neglect, its bell silent as the storm hammered the shutters. Marta knew the alarm had not been tested since the lodge’s heating system failed, and any spark could summon a blaze that would consume the rare books. Marta scanned the nearest shelf, her fingers grazing a leather‑bound treatise that had been set aside for the evening’s reading. The paper slipped under a thin ledge, and a folded envelope fell to the floor with a muted thud. She bent to pick it up, the envelope bearing the same seal as the note she had received earlier, the wax still warm from the fire’s faint heat. The clue she had hoped to retrieve lay there, hidden among the volumes, while the alarm above waited, inert. The lamp flickered once, casting a brief shadow that danced across the alarm’s red eye before the darkness settled again.
