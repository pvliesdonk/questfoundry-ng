The deputies lingered, their ledgers spread across the oak table, the faint tick of the brass clock marking a patience that did not belong to the storm outside. Marta watched the sheriff’s men file reports, their voices low and methodical, while the firelight threw shadows that seemed to linger longer than the smoke. She felt the weight of the anonymous note in her pocket, the reminder that her story remained unchallenged. In that stillness she resolved to move on her own, confident that the alibi she had given still held.

She turned away from the crowd of uniformed men and stepped into the hallway that led to the library. The stone walls were cold under her palm, the pine sap from the doorframe giving a faint, bitter scent that she inhaled without thinking. Marta moved with purpose, her mind cataloguing the places the sheriff’s investigation had not reached. She recalled the hidden compartment behind the third shelf, the faint click she had heard when Jordan had earlier examined the old revolver. The thought that the evidence might still be concealed gave her a narrow line of hope. She pushed the heavy oak door open, the hinges groaning, and entered the dim room, her eyes scanning the rows of books for any sign of the missing clue.

Across the lodge, Cole Duvall slipped toward the service entrance, his pipe wrench clutched tight, his brow set against the howling wind. He pushed the iron latch with a sudden force, the sound echoing off the stone as the door began to give. Marta saw him from the doorway, his figure a dark blur against the storm-lit hallway. Before he could pull the door fully open, a deputy lunged, grabbing the miner’s wrist and wrenching him back. The impact sent Cole stumbling, his wrench clattering to the floor and his breath catching in a harsh gasp. The deputy’s grip tightened, and the miner’s shoulders slumped as he fell against the stone, the sudden violence ending his desperate flight.
