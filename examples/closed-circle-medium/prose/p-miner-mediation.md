The hall smelled of cold ash and pine sap, the furnace sputtering like a dying heart. Jordan Blythe stepped from the doorway, notebook tucked under their arm, eyes sweeping the stone floor where shadows clung to the edges. Across the room, Cole Duvall hunched by the service door, the pipe wrench slick with sweat, his jaw set hard against the chill. Simon Kade stood a few paces away, his uniform immaculate despite the frost, his hand resting on the brass latch that held the door shut.

Jordan raised their voice, low enough to carry over the hiss of the dwindling fire. “We cannot let the door open while the wind howls outside,” they said. “If you step out, the cold will sweep the guests into the storm, and the furnace will die completely.” The miner’s eyes flicked to the locked door, then to the trembling faces of the other guests huddled near the stove.

Jordan turned to Simon, noting the tension in his shoulders as he kept the latch firm. “You have hidden the debt in his coat,” they said, “but the risk of a sudden move is greater than any payment owed.” Simon’s jaw tightened, the line of his mouth barely moving.

The miner tightened his grip on the wrench, the metal biting his palm. Jordan measured the potential fallout: a forced escape could turn the fragile calm into a panic, a stray shot from the revolver on the table could draw the sheriff’s eyes, and an open door could let the storm flood the lodge. Yet holding Cole under a threat of violence could spark a flash of anger that would spill over the rounded stone.

With a steady breath, Jordan placed a hand on the miner’s shoulder. “You will stay until the fire steadies,” they said. “Any attempt to leave will be met with force, and we will keep the peace for the guests.” Cole’s shoulders slumped, the wrench clattering to the floor.

The corridor remained still, the iron hinges cold as judgment, the flickering ember a reminder that a single misstep could shatter the fragile truce.
