The fire crackled low as the investigator leaned across the oak table, eyes fixed on the folded page clutched in Jordan's hand. "If you send that out now, the case will drown in headlines," they said, voice low enough to blend with the hearth's hiss. Jordan traced the edge of the paper with a thumb, feeling the weight of each accusation against the heiress. The storm hammered the lodge walls, and a thin plume of smoke curled around the ink as if questioning its truth. After a moment, Jordan slipped the sheet back into the coat pocket, the notebook still pressed in the other hand. The decision settled like a stone in the chest: the theory would stay unpublished, potentially keeping the investigation free of public frenzy.

When the morning paper arrived, the front page was empty where Jordan's exposé would have been. The editor called later, brief and apologetic, explaining that the newsroom had held off on any lodge story until the sheriff cleared the field. No gossip columns named the heiress, and the Gazette printed only a weather notice. With the press silent, deputy patrols moved through the snow‑bound roads without cameras flashing, and the investigators could question witnesses without the glare of headline scrutiny.

Inside the common room, a handful of reporters gathered around a low table, notebooks open, eyes flickering between the fire and each other. One whispered about a missing ledger, another mentioned a rumor that the old revolver had been seen in the basement weeks before the murder. Their conversation stitched together fragments of suspicion, keeping the lodge humming with the low murmur of a mystery still alive. Jordan watched from the doorway, noting how each rumor added a new thread to the larger tapestry of doubt.
