Jordan turned the thin sheet of paper over, the ink still glossy, and slipped a careful signature across the byline. The article, their most daring theory yet, named the heiress as the architect of the tragedy. They folded the page along the faint crease, tucked it into the inner pocket of their coat, and rose from the study's stone chair. The hallway's iron door shivered as the wind rattled its hinges, and Jordan paused beside the lamp that flickered low. A messenger from the Highpine Gazette waited by the desk, his cap pulled low, and Jordan handed him the folded paper without a word. The clerk nodded, slipped the bundle into a satchel, and disappeared down the narrow passage, leaving only the echo of footsteps on the cold floor.

By midday the Gazette's front page bore the accusation, the headline stark against the white paper. Copies arrived at the lodge on a tray of steaming tea, and the guests gathered around the fire to read. Murmurs rose into a low clamor as eyes widened on the claim that the heiress had engineered her own death. Marta Valen stood at the edge of the circle, her voice tight, and warned that the story would spark panic across the valley. The deputy in the sheriff's uniform watched the crowd, his jaw set, and said the town would need to keep order. Jordan noted how the room's temperature seemed to rise, not from the hearth but from the heat of the accusation. Outside, the storm battered the lodge, but inside the fire of public opinion burned brighter than any furnace could. The investigation, which had moved in shadows, now faced an onslaught of questioning, demanding answers that the stone walls could no longer hide.
