Jordan slipped their notebook into the pocket of their coat and moved between the cracked oak desks, eyes scanning for any scrap of ink that might clarify the night’s murder. A low murmur rose from the hearth where Simon Kade stood, his hand resting on the cold iron of the stove frame. "Someone's tampering with the furnace," Simon said, his voice steady despite the chill. Cole Duvall gripped his pipe wrench tighter. "If we don't fix it, the cold will crawl through the rooms," he said, his tone edged with tension.

Jordan heard the exchange and felt the weight of the words settle like soot on the stone floor. Their mind turned to the missing intake valve that the engineer had mentioned, a flaw that could plunge the lodge into a deadly freeze. A panic among the guests would scatter their focus, let the killer move unchecked, and cripple the fragile investigation that hung on the hidden clue. Jordan decided then that the sabotage must stay unseen, that the quiet dread was safer than a scream of terror.

They stepped closer to the hearth, the fire a thin thread of orange against the darkness. The conduit where the valve should have been lay exposed, a jagged gap in the metal. Jordan lifted the strip of cloth from their coat, a plain piece of canvas they had kept for sketches, and pressed it over the opening, sealing the breach as silently as a breath held in a storm. The flame flickered, steadied, and the room retained a modest warmth that kept the guests shivering but calm.

Jordan pressed a hand to the pantry door, feeling the cold iron of its latch. The door was already shut, the hinges pressed firmly against the stone from Cole’s earlier motion, and it would not give. They felt satisfied and slipped the notebook back into its cover, the pages waiting for the moment when truth could be spoken without endangering the fragile calm.
