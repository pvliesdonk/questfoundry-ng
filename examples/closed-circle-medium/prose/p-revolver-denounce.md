At dawn the revolver lay on the desk, its barrel still warm from recent handling, waiting for Jordan Blythe to speak. The 1876 Vermont piece, polished walnut and iron cylinder, bore fresh fingerprints that matched the hostess’s hand. Jordan lifted the notebook, turned to the page where the fingerprint chart sat, and traced the ridge with a gloved finger. The heat faded under their palm, confirming the weapon had been fired minutes before. The evidence settled like ash in their mind, aligning with the timeline they had compiled.

Jordan moved to the central table, the revolver catching the weak light, its grip reflecting the stone walls. Around the room guests shuffled, blankets rustling, the storm outside rattling the panes. The deputy at the doorway rested a hand near his holver, eyes flicking between the metal and the hostess.

Jordan cleared their throat and said, "Marta Valen’s alibi does not hold. The revolver bears her fingerprints and was warm when found, indicating recent handling. She could not have been in the library at the recorded hour." The murmurs died, eyes fixed on the weapon as if it were a judge. The barrel, still faintly warm, seemed a lone ember against the ash‑gray stone, a silent accusation hanging in the still‑cold air.
