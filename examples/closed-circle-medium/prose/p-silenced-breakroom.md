Jordan stepped into the break room and found the miners scattered over the benches, their faces gaunt in the amber glow of the single bulb. The furnace outside had gone cold hours earlier, and the room was thick with a silence that pressed against the stone walls. He saw Cole Duvall lean against the rusted service door, his scarred hands clasped around a pipe wrench that rested uselessly on the floor. The other men lifted their mugs, stared into the steaming liquid, and let the steam rise without a word. When Simon Kade passed by, he nodded at Jordan and said nothing about the intake valve, his eyes flicking briefly to the hinge before returning to the group. A low murmur of thanks for the coffee floated out, then died. Jordan sensed the secret that bound them—something about the missing valve, a debt unfinished—hanging in the air as dense as the frost that gathered on the windows. He watched as Marta Valen entered, carrying a tray of burned biscuits, and placed it on the table without looking up. The miners shifted their weight, their shoulders tightening, but no one raised the subject that had driven wedges between them earlier. The room held its breath, each exhale a soft rasp, and the weight of the unspoken grew until it settled like a slab of cold stone on Jordan's own chest.
