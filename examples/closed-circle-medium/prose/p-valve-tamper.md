Jordan listened as the lodge’s maintenance engineer, a thin man with grease‑stained hands, read from his notebook. “The main intake valve on the furnace is not where it should be,” he said, voice low, eyes flicking to the iron door where the pipe should have been. “It was secured this morning, but by evening the fitting was gone. I think someone removed it on purpose.” Jordan felt a tightening in his chest. The missing piece meant the furnace could not draw enough air, and the heat that sputtered over the last hour would die quickly.

He noted the engineer’s words and the way the other guests shifted, the thin blankets drawing tighter around shoulders. The report was clear: a valve absent, a sabotage hint. Jordan’s mind catalogued the clue, weighing it against the earlier murder scene. If the fire could be starved, the lodge’s already fragile warmth would turn to cold, a weapon in a place already sealed by storm.

He moved toward the hearth, the stone floor cold beneath his boots. The furnace glowed weakly, its iron hinges creaking as the draft slipped through. In the amber light, Jordan saw Cole Duvall standing just beyond the brazier, his dusty overalls heavy, hands clenched around a pipe wrench. The miner’s gaze was fixed on the metalwork, his posture uneasy as if waiting for an instruction. Jordan’s breath rose in a thin puff, and he realized the engineer’s suspicion was not idle; Cole lingered where the valve should have been, a silent witness to whatever hand had tampered with the lodge’s lifeline.
