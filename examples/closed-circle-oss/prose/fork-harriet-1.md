Harriet pressed her shoulder against the cold railing of the stone bridge spanning the canal. The night fog clung low, muffling the wooden planks beneath her boots. From the square beyond a distant murmur rose, the steady cadence of the Farmer Union’s march moving like a tide hidden beneath the quiet. The weight of order settled on her badge, a heavy iron press against her chest as the council’s demands lingered in the air. She could feel the pull of the protest swelling, a restless surge that threatened to upset the fragile peace the town had bought. A torch flickered on the opposite bank, its flame catching the iron rail and throwing a brief flash across the water. A cold wind slipped through the gap, lifting a few loose leaves that spun like whispered rumors. Harriet tightened the strap of the revolver at her hip, the metal cool against her skin, a silent promise that she could act if the balance tipped. She stood still, ears tuned to the distant chant, mind weighing the surge of dissent against the rigid demands of the council. The bridge held her, the night held its breath, and the union’s rumble rolled onward, a low thunder that seemed to echo in the hollow of the stone.
