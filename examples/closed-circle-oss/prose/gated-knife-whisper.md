Lucy pushed the swinging door of Marjorie's diner, the ivory‑handled knife clenched in her palm. The fresh scar on her right hand pressed into the wood as she stepped toward the counter. She brushed a stray strand of hair from her shaved head and steadied the weight of the blade. Across the narrow gap she leaned, voice low, and whispered that the Ornate Knife had vanished. The empty sheath lay in the brass cash drawer, a silent cue that something else waited. A sour weight settled over the room, and the clink of a spoon against a mug rang like a warning. Marjorie Lynch hovered nearby, flour dusting her apron, eyes flicking to Lucy’s scarred hand. Thea Swan lingered at the doorway, tray balanced, but Lucy did not meet her gaze. The furnace behind the wall hummed, and the air seemed to contract around the counter. Lucy felt the familiar cold of steel in her memory of the missing knife, the weight of her revolver at her hip a quiet reminder that violence lingered. She pressed the empty sheath into the hollow of the counter, the wood biting into her scar. The room held its breath, waiting for the next move.
