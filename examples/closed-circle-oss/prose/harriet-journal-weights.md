Harriet Wellman sat at the oak desk in the deputy’s office, the scar on her cheek catching the lamplight as she pressed the pen to the battered page. The ornate ivory-handled knife rested on the blotter, its cold steel a silent weight beside the folded note Edgar had slipped to her earlier. She turned the page over, the ink spreading into the fibers, and recorded the price of the arrests she had ordered.

She wrote that the farmer‑union banners now hung limp, their colors faded against the soot‑stained walls, and that the market alleys felt narrower than the cracked pine that lined them. The night outside pressed on the shutters, a darkness so thick it seemed to swallow the town’s breath. Each line in her journal marked how the quiet enforced by the cuffed protesters cost the community more than any fine, noting the widening gap between her badge and the villagers who once trusted her.

Harriet closed the journal, the leather cover thudding against her palm, and stared at the knife and the note, the symbols on the parchment glinting in the wan light. The weight of the night settled on her shoulders as the town’s streets grew tighter, a reminder that order had a heavy, unyielding price.
