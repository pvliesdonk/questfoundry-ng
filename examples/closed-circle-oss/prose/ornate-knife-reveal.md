Lucy leaned forward, shoulders pressed against the iron furnace, and whispered that the knife lay hidden beneath the coal. The ivory‑handled blade caught the furnace glow, its polish bright against the soot. She kept the revolver at her hip, the metal cold under her coat. Across the room Harriet Wellman stood by the ledger table, her trench coat swaying as the crowd shifted. Harriet's scarred cheek tightened when she saw the exposed blade, and she held the ledger steady with one hand, her eyes flicking over the faces that hovered near the fire. The murmur of the farmer union chant drifted in through the stone arches, a low tide of voices that rose and fell. Lucy watched Harriet's gaze linger on the knife, then turn back to the restless townsfolk, a thin line of unease crossing her features. The furnace hissed louder, the heat curling up the wall. The blade's edge glimmered, a silent promise of danger in the dim light. Lucy felt the moment hold its breath, waiting for the next move. She noted the ticking of a distant gear in the mill’s lower chamber, a reminder that time slipped even as the crowd shouted. The iron door behind the furnace creaked, the sound threading through the chant like a warning. With the knife’s cold glint still burning in her mind, Lucy steadied her breath, ready to decide.
