Harriet moved through the diner's clamor, slipped Thea's warning note into her coat pocket, and the raucous voices drowned the faint glint of metal on Edgar's desk. The metal edge caught a flash from the lantern, a quick spark that vanished beneath the shouts.

She stepped to Edgar's table, badge flashing, and produced iron handcuffs. The cold steel snapped around his wrists, biting the scar on his cheek. Blood welled from the old wound, bright against his pale skin as the crowd hissed and jeered. Harriet lifted the stone slab of the desk; the ornate ivory‑handled knife lay exposed, its blade gleaming like a shard of ice. Beside it a torn page of the Blackwood Ledger rested, ink still dark. Lucy rushed to the humming device on the floor, her hands trembling as she battered the fragile mechanism. She drove a wrench into the casing, the red wire sparking before she snapped it free. The ticking charge fell silent, the bomb's death throes ending in a hiss of released pressure.

Harriet's pen scratched across the leather of her journal as she recorded the distant echo of a gunshot that cracked the night beyond the town's roofs. The scarred arrest faded beneath the lantern's glow, and she lifted the ledger page, brushed a sip of coffee from Marjorie's cup, and slipped the paper beneath the foam. The note settled among the froth, hidden from the patrons.

Through the grimy window Edgar saw the outline of the Old Mill, its broken tower smoldering against the night sky. The cold wind carried a faint clang of unseen gears, a reminder that danger lingered beyond the diner's walls.
