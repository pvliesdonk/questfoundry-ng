Harriet Wellman stood at the edge of the council chamber, her trench coat flaring against the draft that slipped through the cracked windows. The council members rose from their benches, their gazes converging on her like bars of cold steel. She felt the weight of those eyes coil around her shoulders, tightening like a vice. Mayor Caldwell adjusted his gold tie, his smile brittle, and leaned forward as if to speak, but his words fell into the hush that the council had forced. Harriet's hand rested on the grip of the revolver at her hip, the metal cold under her palm. She noted the ledger on the oak table, its pages still blank, a reminder of the secret they guarded. The room's ticking clock marked the pause between accusations and decisions. She recalled the farmers' chant outside, now muffled by the council's rebuke. A whisper of wind rattled the shutters, and the scent of coal lingered from the furnace down the hall. Harriet tightened the strap of her badge, feeling the metal press against her chest, a reminder of the authority she bore. The council’s stare grew sharper, and she understood that any hesitation would be taken as weakness. She drew a slow breath and steadied herself, ready to meet the pressure.
