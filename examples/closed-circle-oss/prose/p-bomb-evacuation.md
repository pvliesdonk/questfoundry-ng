Lucy Frost watched the ticking device pulse through the stale air of the diner’s back room. The metal click rose steadier as the crowd filtered out, the sound a metronome against the clatter of rain on the stone street. Clifford Moon slipped past the dim doorway, his slicked‑back hair catching the weak lamp glow, and disappeared into the darkened alley beyond the market stalls. The sound of his boots faded, leaving the bomb unaltered on the cracked wooden table.

Harriet Wellman moved with purpose. She knelt beside the device, her scar catching a flash of light as she pressed a gloved hand to the cold metal, then stood and stepped back, her trench coat flaring against the chill. She raised the radio at her belt, her voice clear and brief as she called for the bomb squad, the channel crackling with static. The town’s shutters rattled in the wind, and through the fog‑laden windows Lucy could see silhouettes of townsfolk pressing against the panes, breaths fogging the glass, eyes fixed on the doorway where the ticking heart of danger waited.

The rhythm of the bomb’s pulse seemed louder now, echoing off the soot‑blackened walls. Lucy noted the ivory‑handled knife lying half‑buried in dust at the doorway, its polished curve catching the lamp’s sputter. The blade was slick with a faint sheen of rain, its missing sheath suggesting it had been dropped in haste. Harriet’s badge pressed against her chest as she spoke into the receiver, her tone steady while the town held its breath, the silence outside broken only by distant shouts and the relentless ticking.

Inside the diner's dim interior, Lucy felt the weight of the moment settle on her shoulders. She knew the choices before her: to aid Harriet with the radio, or to claim the knife that glinted in the doorway. The rain hammered the roof, the ticking persisted, and the night waited for her decision.
