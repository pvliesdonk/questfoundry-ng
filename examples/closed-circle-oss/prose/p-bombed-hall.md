Edgar heard a faint ticking echo from the far wall of the community hall, and the metallic scent of gunpowder drifted down the aisles. The sound knotted the air, and a thin line of panic slipped through the crowd as heads turned toward the source.

Lucy Frost stared at the bar, her eyes hard and alarmed, the scar on her palm catching the dim light. Across the room Marjorie Lynch lifted her chin, a hopeful smile breaking the tension even as the ticking grew louder. Edgar saw the two women lock gazes for a heartbeat, the contrast sharp as steel.

Marjorie stepped forward, voice steady, and urged that the doors remain open, insisting the evening crowd needed the rally’s morale to hold. Harriet Wellman stood by the heavy oak doors, badge flashing, her hand on the latch as she listened. Edgar felt his revolver pressed against his hip, the weight a reminder of the danger humming in the hall.

The ticking rose, a thin metronome that would not be ignored.
