Lucy Frost stood in the dim back room, rain drumming the stone street outside. Harriet Wellman knelt by the trembling device, slipped the radio to her ear, and spoke into it with a voice that did not rise. Her words cut through the static as she ordered the bomb squad to the diner. Across the doorway Marjorie Lynch lingered, her flour‑dusted apron hanging loose, eyes fixed on the empty frame where the bomb sat. When the call completed, Lucy moved to the swinging door. She lifted the old alarm lever, its clang echoing off the cracked pine floor, and shouted for the townsfolk to leave. One by one, frightened faces slipped past her, their shoes scuffing the wet boards, the hallway filling with the sound of hurried steps. The diner's interior fell to a hollow hush as the last patron crossed the threshold. In the sudden quiet Harriet opened a battered notebook, the leather cover creasing under her thumb. She began to write, the pen scratching a record of the night’s dread, noting the unexploded bomb, the empty doorway, and the unanswered question of Edgar Barnes’s fate. Marjorie stood nearby, watching the ink dry, her gaze later turning to the same journal as Harriet flipped through testimonies, comparing Edgar’s alibi with the statements she had gathered. On the counter, the ivory‑handled knife lay half‑buried in dust, its curve catching the sputtering lantern light. Lucy slid a glass of amber whiskey across the wood toward Marjorie, the liquid catching the faint gleam of the blade. The cold metal and the warm drink rested side by side, waiting for a decision.
