Mayor Caldwell felt the stone floor pulse beneath his boots while the furnace in the rear hall snarled with heat. He watched Deputy Sheriff Harriet Wellman move among the farmers whose wrists the watchmen were cuffing, her badge pressed to her chest and a thin scar tracing the cheek illuminated by the lantern. The chant that rose only moments before sank into a low, uneasy murmur as the iron cuffs clanked against the wooden table. The farmers knelt, their faces hard, and a young man muttered a promise of retaliation that drifted through the crowd like winter wind.

Caldwell stepped forward, clasped Harriet’s forearm, and thanked her for restoring order, his gold tie tightening against his throat. A tight grin stretched across his lips, a veneer masking a knot of dread coiling in his gut. He turned his gaze to the brass‑bound safe set against the far wall, the iron latch catching the lantern’s dim light. With deliberate motion he lifted the Blackwood Ledger, felt the weight of the hidden debts, and slid the book into a concealed compartment behind a false panel. The safe clicked shut, sealing the ledger from prying eyes.

He noted the lingering vow of revenge that the farmer‑union carried down the cobbled streets, a chill that would not fade, and sensed the cracks in his polished public image widening with each silent promise. A leather‑bound journal lay open on the nearby table, its pages waiting for ink.
