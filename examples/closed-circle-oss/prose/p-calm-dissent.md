Marjorie Lynch stepped out of the diner into the dimming street and felt the weight of the night settle over Grayhaven. She moved past the market stalls where the last lanterns sputtered, their light casting long shadows on cracked cobblestones. The crowd that had roared hours earlier now lingered in clusters, voices lowered to murmurs that drifted like cold wind through the alleys. Harriet Wellman stood near the union banner, her badge glinting under the faint glow, and she raised a hand in quiet support of the protesters. Marjorie noted the firm set of Harriet’s shoulders, a signal that the deputy sheriff aligned herself with the farmers despite the council’s recent demands. The banner’s ragged cloth flapped against a gray sky, its threads trembling as if aware of an unseen current. Marjorie heard a low chant rise from a group of workers near the fountain, a rhythm that rose and fell without reaching a crescendo. The chant did not fill the square, but it lingered, threading through conversations and the clink of iron mugs in the tavern. She sensed the town’s pulse slow, a tentative calm that steadied the streets for a breath. Yet beneath that surface she felt an undercurrent, a ripple of dissent that brushed against the edges of the gathering, unseen but audible to those who listened. Marjorie tightened the strap of her coat and turned her gaze toward the western lane, where the next stir of unrest might take shape.
