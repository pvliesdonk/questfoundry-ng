Edgar stood at the rusted hatch of the old mill, the lantern he had left flickering on the stone slab. Harriet Wellman slipped through the narrow opening, her trench coat sweeping dust as she followed the cramped direction the riddle had given. The air in the cellar pressed cold against Edgar’s cheek, a scent of rust and old grain filling the hollow. Harriet moved toward rows of tables that leaned like cracked pine, her badge catching the dim light that filtered through the cracked iron lid.

Lucy Frost stood near the far wall, her shaved head glinting under the weak beam of her flashlight. Edgar watched as she swung the light back and forth, the narrow strip cutting through soot‑blackened gloom and exposing the tops of stacked tables. The beam lingered on a table whose surface was caked with ash, then slipped down the side, revealing a gap in the wood.

Harriet gestured, and Lucy followed her out of the cellar, the stone stair behind them disappearing into the mill’s foundation. They emerged into the front of Marjorie Lynch’s diner, where the smell of frying oil mingled with a metallic tang that seemed to rise from the kitchen itself. Lucy knelt beside a wooden table in the corner, lifted a warped plank, and a clunky iron device lay hidden beneath. The metal pulsed with a muted thrum, a ticking that matched the low hum of the furnace above. Lucy’s scarred palm brushed the tangled wire, and she frowned, the flashlight’s glow catching the fresh dark scar on her right hand.

Edgar noted the tension in the room as patrons turned their heads toward the sound. The device sat there, inconspicuous yet unmistakable, its presence a silent threat. He felt the weight of his revolver at his hip, the badge pressing tighter against his chest as the ticking grew louder. A narrow stone stair led up toward the back of the diner, while a set of wooden doors at the far wall opened onto the town’s community hall, the hallway beyond faintly echoing the same ticking rhythm.
