Lucy felt the cold hallway floor under her boots as she turned from the shattered back wall. The red wire from the bomb lay curled near the cracked oak, its metal still warm from the brief spark. She reached down, fingers closing around the insulated strand, and pulled hard; the wire snapped and the humming charge sputtered out, leaving a thin scarlet line on the floor. The sound of a hurried footstep broke the silence. Clifford Moon darted toward the doorway, his polished coat slick with fresh blood that streamed down his sleeves in dark rivulets. He glanced back, intent on escaping the room. Lucy seized the lapel of his coat, her hand white on the soaked fabric, and yanked him backward. Each step forced the crimson to spill onto the stone tiles, droplets pattering like rain on tin. She dragged him toward the narrow arch that led to the kitchen stairs, the coat dragging a trail of blood across the ground. The lantern on the wall threw a wavering circle over the stone, highlighting the fresh stains. Harriet Wellman stood near the counter, a lantern steady in her grip, a leather‑bound journal open on the dented desk. The pale scar on her cheek caught the dim glow as she watched the struggle, her revolver at her hip a silent promise. She pressed a pen to the page; the scratch of steel on paper echoed the distant clank of a gear. Harriet recorded the frantic climax in neat, hurried script, noting Clifford’s sudden flight, Lucy’s grip on his coat, and the scarlet trail left behind. She snapped the journal shut with a soft thud and slipped it into her coat. The hallway stretched ahead, walls of cold stone narrowing the space, a draft carrying the scent of damp earth. Lucy glanced back once, saw the blood still seeping from Clifford’s coat onto the tiles, and felt the sting of copper‑tinged blood on her scarred palm, the wound pulsing with each breath. She steadied herself for what lay beyond the arch, the echo of footsteps fading into darkness.
