Clifford lingered in the doorway, his coat catching the dim glow that slipped through the rain‑slick panes. Harriet stepped forward, her trench coat flaring, and she seized Edgar by the collar. Her scar caught the lantern light as she slammed handcuffs onto his wrists, the metal biting his shirt. Thea slipped through the crowd, pressed a folded note into Harriet’s palm, and vanished before anyone could question her. Lucy barked orders, her voice cutting through the clatter of plates and the low hiss of the bomb in the back room. The patrons shuffled toward the swinging door, eyes wide, while Lucy’s whistle shrilled, a thin thread of sound that pulled them outward.

Clifford’s gaze fixed on the stone slab where the ornate ivory‑handled knife lay, its surface polished to a bright sheen. A stray shaft of light pierced the gloom, striking the blade and throwing a flash across Clifford’s narrowed eyes. The flash sang cold across the steel, a brief glare that marked the thin line between order and chaos. He felt the weight of the moment settle, his own hand twitching toward the handle that Harriet now held.

Harriet adjusted the cuffs on Edgar, tightening the bolts as the blade’s flash faded. Lucy continued her frantic commands, guiding the last of the diners into the rain. The knife rested heavy in Harriet’s grip, its missing sheath a silent reminder that another weapon might still linger in the shadows.
