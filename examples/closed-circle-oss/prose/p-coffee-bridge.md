Lucy lifted the kettle and poured steaming coffee into metal cups that sat on the battered tin counter. The sound of the kettle hissed as she worked, and the murmur of the protestors rose like a low tide around the firepit. Caldwell's warning about the unrest threatening town order lingered in the heated air, a thin echo that brushed the backs of the crowd. She handed a cup to a farmer whose hands were stained with soot, and to another who clutched a clenched fist. "The unrest could tear the town apart," she said, her voice flat, eyes scanning the faces that gathered near the iron ribs of the pit. The protestors accepted the coffee and settled on the cold stones, their chants softening for a moment as the bitter liquid warmed their throats. Lucy watched the crowd swell, noting how the tension seemed to shrink the space between the town's elite and the workers. She thought of Caldwell's promise to keep order and felt the weight of his words pressing against the simmering flame. A clink rang as she set another cup down, and Old Man Well stepped from the shadow of the mill’s doorway. He leaned close to Harriet, his voice low. "Force will shatter the fragile peace," he warned. Harriet nodded, her revolver still at her hip, and turned her gaze toward the firepit where the ledger smoldered. Lucy felt the iron clang of the pit echo the warning, and the steam curled around her gloves as the night grew colder.
