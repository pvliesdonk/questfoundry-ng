Harriet slipped the lantern from her coat, its flame trembling against the draft that slipped through the cracked stone of the mill. The rusted iron latch on the low hatch creaked as she forced it open, revealing a shaft of dim light that fell on a mass of tangled gears and weathered beams. A low murmur rose from the darkness, the sound of wind through the empty wheels, and she heard the whisper that the ledger lay hidden there, sealed behind the machinery like a wound kept covered. She pressed her hand against a bundle of rusted plates, feeling the cold bite of iron; a thin panel shifted and gave way, exposing a narrow cavity where a leather‑wrapped book rested. The Blackwood Ledger, its edges still warm from the furnace hours before, lay flat against the stone, its pages edged with dust.

She angled the lantern toward the nearest beam, its flame catching on a shard of ivory that glinted from the wood. The ornate knife, its handle polished to a bright sheen, was wedged deep in the grain, the blade half hidden by splintered pine. The light traced the curve of the hilt, and Harriet felt the weight of the violence it suggested. She wondered who would hide such a weapon where only a flickering lantern could expose it.

Beyond the stone arches, the farmer union's chant rose, a steady drum that pressed against the mill's walls. Harriet tightened her grip on the lantern, the ledger heavy in her palm, the knife cold in her sight.
