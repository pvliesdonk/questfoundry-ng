Harriet pressed her shoulder against the timber counter, the wood cold beneath her coat. Lucy Frost's voice slipped low over the clatter of dishes, a warning about the ivory-handled knife that had disappeared from the evidence locker. The words landed like a rusted blade, and a prickling sense of concealed danger settled along Harriet's spine. Across the room, Edgar Barnes moved with his usual measured steps, his thin spectacles catching the lantern light. He paused beside Marjorie Lynch's battered mug, unfolded a small piece of paper, and slipped it beneath the rim before anyone could see. Harriet's eyes followed the motion, the folded note a silent confirmation that hidden messages were already circulating among the patrons. The diner hummed with murmurs, but her mind lingered on the draft she had felt earlier in the old mill's stone foundation, a faint sigh that hinted at a secret passage beneath the town. She felt the weight of the badge against her chest tighten as the implication grew: somewhere below, a path waited, its chill promising both risk and revelation. She tightened her grip on the counter's edge, feeling the cold metal of her badge thrum against her palm, while the distant clank of the mill's gears seemed to echo the promise of what lay hidden.
