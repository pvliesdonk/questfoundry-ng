Edgar Barnes felt the diner's low ceiling press against the humming of the ticking device as Thea Swan burst through the doorway. She clutched a tray tightly, her bob haircut flashing in the sputtering lamplight, and her eyes widened at the tension that seemed to pulse through the cramped room. Edgar watched her stride toward the far corner where a rusted iron door marked the entrance to a cellar. Thea halted at the doorway, her breath catching as an ornate ivory‑handled knife caught the dim light and threw a brief flash across the stone steps. The blade lay atop a crude altar, its surface still dark with fresh blood. Edgar followed her gaze down into the cellar, his badge pressing against his chest as the cold air brushed his skin. He saw the altar, a slab of rough stone stained by the same crimson wetness that clung to the knife's edge. Thea stepped closer, her hand trembling as she traced the rim of the altar with a finger. Lucy Frost stood near a battered table, her revolver at her hip, and whispered that the knife had vanished from the evidence locker. Her voice fell low, barely above the drip of water that fell from a cracked pipe in the ceiling, each solitary plink echoing off the stone walls. Edgar heard the sound, noted a single droplet gathering on the floor and disappearing into the darkness beyond. He saw frantic footprints in the dust, a jagged path that led away from the altar toward the far side of the cellar. A ledger lay open on a low shelf, its pages yellowed and the ink faint, suggesting an older secret that waited beneath the floorboards. The water drip fell again, the sound steady, promising a hidden source beyond the immediate gloom. Edgar's mind held the scene together, the knife's glint, the whispered warning, the drip, the footprints, and the ledger, each detail a thread in the tightening knot of dread that filled the cellar. The air felt heavy, as if the very stone waited for a decision.
