Lucy Frost took the cold steel wrench from the bar shelf and set it on the cracked oak counter. She knelt beside the humming metal box that sat in the corner, its ticking pulse louder than the wind outside. She lifted the cover and exposed a coil of red insulation tangled with the main circuit. Lucy pulled the red wire free and, with the flat of the wrench, she sliced it cleanly. The wire gave a sharp snap and a brief arc of blue‑white light flared across the wood, the timer sputtering as the remaining seconds faltered. The device shuddered, a metallic cough escaped its belly, and a sudden flare of sparks burst outward. Shrapnel ripped the air, and a jagged fragment struck Lucy’s forearm, tearing the fresh dark scar on her right palm. Blood welled onto her sleeve as the bomb coughed one last time, then fell silent. A hollow clang echoed when the broken casing hit the floor, the scent of ozone mingling with iron. Across the room Deputy Harriet Wellman stood, her badge glinting under the lantern, the scar on her cheek catching the erratic light. Harriet’s eyes narrowed as she surveyed the aftermath, her hand hovering near her revolver but never drawing. Lucy winced, pressed a palm over the wound, and felt the sting of copper‑tinged blood dripping onto the wood. She reached for the blood‑stained ivory knife that lay beside the shattered device and gripped its handle, the crimson smear a silent witness to the violence. Then she knelt lower, found the tarnished metal latch beneath the ash‑stained table, and pulled it open, the mechanism giving a dry click as a narrow passage yawned dark. Finally she stepped toward Deputy Harriet’s scarred gaze, meeting the deputy’s steady stare as the room settled into a tense quiet. The only sound was the slow drip of blood from Lucy’s arm onto the floorboards, each drop a reminder of the narrow margin by which the diner was saved.
