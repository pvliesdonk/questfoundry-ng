The diner’s windows were cracked pine, letting the bitter wind slice the soot‑blackened room. Lucy felt the cold steel of the wrench in her palm as she stepped toward the metal box hidden beneath the counter. A spark leapt when the casing gave under her grip, licking the air for a heartbeat. Harriet entered, trench coat sweeping the floor, badge pressed hard against her chest, a torn fragment of the Blackwood Ledger clutched in one hand. The ragged edge caught the dim light, its missing corner spelling a name Lucy could not read. Patrons whispered, their voices rising like a low‑grade engine, and Harriet’s scar glinted as she gestured toward the fragment, demanding truth from the men who swore Edgar Barnes had lingered near the crime scene. 

Edgar stepped forward, spectacles flashing, and murmured a rhyme that floated over the clatter of plates. “The ledger burns where shadows meet the stone,” he said, thin voice cutting the tension. Lucy watched his thin fingers trace the folded note, the syllables feeding the suspicion that clung to his thin frame. Her breath fogged in the cold air as she slipped a screwdriver between the bomb’s wires, prying open the second panel. The humming charge sputtered, then fell silent when she lifted the blue wire free, a small arc of light sparking from the exposed copper. 

The gossip swelled; heads turned toward Edgar, accusation thickening the room. Harriet raised her voice, the scar catching the light as she thrust the ledger fragment into view, the torn page a reminder of hidden debts. Lucy’s hand trembled, the wrench warm against the metal as she lifted another panel, the inner mechanism humming faintly. The ivory‑handled knife lay half‑covered in ash near the counter, its cold blade a silent warning. From the doorway Mayor Caldwell’s voice cut through the clamor, low and urgent, urging a swift arrest before fear boiled over. Lucy pressed the wrench against the final wire, feeling the fresh dark scar on her palm throb under the pressure. The bomb’s heart stilled, the din of frightened townsfolk falling to an uneasy hush. Lucy stood still, wrench still warm in her grip, eyes flicking between the knife, the ledger fragment, and Mayor Caldwell’s looming silhouette beyond the doorway. The night was far from over.
