Edgar stood on the cracked pine floor of the diner, the cold metal of the handcuffs still biting his wrist and the weight of his revolver settled against his hip. The soot‑blackened windows let in a wan winter light that fell across the battered service counter. Across the room Harriet Wellman bent over a battered notebook, her pen scratching a thin line as she logged the echo of distant gunfire that had rolled over the canal. She wrote the words in short, measured ink, each stroke a quiet counterpoint to the low hum that lingered after the blast. She tightened the handcuffs around his wrists, sealing his fate, before returning to her notebook.

Lucy Frost moved among the remaining patrons with steady steps. Her voice, low and firm, instructed them toward the swinging door at the far end of the room. She brushed the brass whistle in her pocket as she gestured, and the diners filed out, some clutching heavy coats, others gripping worn satchels. The heavy wooden door slammed shut with an iron clang, sealing the diner from the street and from the wind that howled outside.

The bomb that had threatened the back room lay dormant now; its ticking pulse had faded into a hollow silence. No longer did the low thrum press against the walls, and the only sound was the soft rustle of winter wind sneaking through the cracks in the shutters. Edgar felt the weight of the moment settle on his shoulders, the same weight that had settled on Harriet’s badge when she pressed it to her chest. The space where the ornate knife had glinted moments before remained empty, its missing sheath a reminder that danger had not been fully extinguished.

As the room emptied, the lamps dimmed to a weak amber glow and the distant gear‑click of the mill drifted up from the street below. Edgar’s eyes lingered on the blank space where the bomb’s timer had once sparked, and a fragile calm held the diner in its grip, waiting for the next choice to break the stillness. The quiet before the storm lay heavy, like winter steel waiting to be forged.
