Harriet Wellman left the diner's doorway, rain slick on the cobblestones and her badge heavy against her chest. She had taken the crumpled ledger fragment from the table and slipped it into her coat before stepping into the night. The garden behind Edgar Barnes's house lay in shadow, a low stone bench tucked beside a rusted tool chest. Under the bench a single sheet of soot‑darkened paper rested, its ink forming a line that bound Edgar to a concealed debt. Harriet knelt, the lantern she carried throwing a thin pool of light over the page, and felt the weight of the new evidence settle in her gut.

From the far side of the street Lucy Frost's voice cut through the rain, the alarm lever clanged against iron as the device in the back room whined louder. The bomb's timer clicked in a steady rhythm, each pulse striking against Harriet's pulse. The sound rose, a metallic echo that seemed to reverberate off the garden walls.

Mayor Caldwell appeared at the gate, his gold tie a flash of authority in the gloom. "We must lock him up before the story spreads," he said, his eyes fixed on the fragment she held. Harriet met his stare, the scar on her cheek catching the lantern's glow, and felt the weight of his expectation press against the uniform she wore. She weighed his demand against the doubt that now gnawed at her.

Lucy’s alarm blared again, a harsh red light flashing above the swinging door as patrons surged onto the rain‑slick street. Harriet moved toward Edgar, whose thin shirt fluttered in the wind. The cold handcuffs at her belt clinked as she slipped them onto his wrists, the metal biting into the fabric. She tightened the cuffs just enough to hold him, then stepped back, her hand hovering near the clasp. In the crowd's surge, a narrow gap opened, and Edgar slipped a foot toward the garden's shadow, the opportunity hanging in the air as the bomb ticked faster and the mayor's voice rose above the clamor.
