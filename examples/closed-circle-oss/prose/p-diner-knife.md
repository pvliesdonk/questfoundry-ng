Lucy ran a rag over the cracked pine bar, the motion steady as the steam hissed from the kitchen. The room thrummed with low chatter; farmers whispered about the missing ledger while town wives swapped rumors about the council clerk. Thea Swan slipped behind the counter, her bob hair tucked under a white coat, and began to fill cups with quiet efficiency. Mayor Caldwell entered through the double doors, his gold‑tie gleaming against the soot‑blackened windows, and raised his voice just enough to cut through the hum. He promised order and reminded the patrons that the town would remain steady despite the recent murder.

The diner's hum rose as Clifford Moon rose from his seat. He reached for the heavy ivory‑handled knife that lay beside his plate, the handle catching the flicker of the oil lamp. As he lifted it, the blade reflected a thin line of light across the polished floor, a flash that seemed out of place among the soot‑gray walls. On the edge of the bar a folded note rested next to a jar of sugar; the paper was smeared with ink, the words demanding the return of the knife written in a hurried hand. Lucy watched the gesture, the knife's ivory surface gleaming for a heartbeat before he slipped it into his coat.

Clifford moved toward the back door, the knife now hidden, and disappeared into the narrow hallway that led to the rooms above the kitchen. From the doorway Lucy saw a sliver of dim light leaking from his cramped quarters, the silhouette of a man crouched over a wooden table. He set the knife on the surface and began to rub it with a piece of cloth, the feathered strokes coaxing a brighter sheen from the ivory. The glint caught Lucy's eye, steady and cold, as the night wind rattled the diner's metal shutters.
