Edgar Barnes stood in the old mill’s doorway, rain drumming the cracked pine walls. The air carried a thin metallic tick, steady as a clock, coming from the concealed bomb left in the square. He pulled a folded note from his coat, the paper trembling in his fingers. He muttered, “When the hand never rests, the stone will fall,” his voice low, the rhyme matching the pulse that reverberated through the timber.

He stepped forward, boots splashing water across the stone, and felt his heart throb in time with the ticking. A lantern held by Deputy Harriet Wellman flickered nearby, her badge catching a brief shaft of light before the glow faded. The rhythm grew louder, each beat aligning with the throb in his chest, a quiet accord between flesh and device.

Edgar’s gaze fixed on an iron latch marking a hidden cellar beneath the mill floor. The latch rattled faintly as the ticking persisted, a reminder that routine could crumble under unseen pressure. He steadied his breath, aware that the riddle he had spoken might be the only map to the danger below. The wind sighed through broken windows, carrying the scent of wet iron and old wood, and the mill seemed to hold its breath, waiting for the next move.
