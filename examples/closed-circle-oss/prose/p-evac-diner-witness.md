Rain hammered the cobblestones as Mayor Caldwell stood in the doorway of Marjorie's Diner, the gold of his tie catching the lantern's sputter. Lucy Frost pulled the alarm lever and shouted, her voice cutting through the wind. The bomb lay silent in the back room, its casing cold and unmoving, while patrons surged toward the swinging doors, coats flapping and children clutched to mothers. He saw Harriet Wellman slip back inside, badge flashing beneath the lantern's glow, and move toward Edgar Barnes, the thin spectacles glinting in the light. He watched her lift the cold handcuffs from his wrists, whisper something low, and let his frame slip free. He noted Harriet's scar catching the firelight as she stepped back, her hand hovering over the Blackwood Ledger lying on the counter. Mayor Caldwell's reprimand rang in Harriet's ears, a sharp rebuke that seemed to echo in the night air.

He heard Lucy sweep the crowd outward, her torch sweeping the floor, and felt the weight of the town's eyes on him as he guided the fleeing crowd. He brushed his fingers against the ledger for a brief moment, his hand hovering over the paper before he pressed forward. He surged forward, pulling a trembling patron behind him, his eyes catching the flash of the ornate ivory‑handled knife half‑buried under a table. He turned his gaze to the dark street where Edgar slipped, a smoldering scrap clinging to his coat, and vanished into the woods beyond the alley, the underbrush swallowing his footsteps. Harriet called his name, declared his freedom, and the accusation hung unanswered in the rain. He felt his own polished image pressing against his chest as the night settled around the unresolved truth.
