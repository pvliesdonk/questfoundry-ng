Lucy Frost watched the lantern flame flicker as Harriet Wellman bent over the crumpled ledger fragment, the scar on her cheek catching the weak light. Lucy saw Harriet’s finger trace a faded line that linked Edgar Barnes to a hidden debt, while a nearby patron repeated a testimony placing Edgar near the cellar. Lucy felt Harriet’s brow tighten as another voice insisted the man had argued with the victim moments before the murder. Lucy raised her voice and shouted for everyone to move toward the swinging door, pointing to the exit with a hand that cut through the clatter of chairs.

Edgar Barnes lingered by the counter, his spectacles glinting, and he began to recite a low rhyme: “Tick‑tock, the clock will strike, blood will fall where shadows like.” The words floated through the diner, each syllable a thin blade that sliced doubt from the air. Lucy heard the bomb’s pulse thrum beneath the floorboards and felt the timer click louder as the minutes slipped by. Lucy guided a trembling family past the bar, her grip firm on the door edge, and ushered an elderly man onto the rain‑slick street.

Rumors swirled as Harriet overheard a claim that Edgar had been framed, the crowd’s whispers rising to a roar that flooded the space. Lucy pulled the old alarm lever again and the alarm wail screamed across the room, its clang echoing off cracked pine. Lucy blew the whistle held at her throat, the sharp note tearing through the din. A patron leaned close and murmured that Lucy’s brother had been seen handling the Blackwood ledger, the words slipping into her ear like cold wind. The scent of smoldering timber drifted in from the old mill, a bitter hint of fire curling among the departing bodies. Lucy kept her grip firm on the door as the alarm screamed and the bomb’s timer clicked louder, the diner emptying into the night.
