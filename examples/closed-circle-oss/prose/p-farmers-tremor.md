Harriet tightened the grip on her revolver as the shattered glass rang through the square. The sound sent a thin vibration through the gathered bodies, a tremor that rode the chant of the Farmer Union and the crackle of the firepit. She watched the farmers shift, their shoulders tensing, eyes flicking to the orange tongues licking the iron ribs before darting back to the crowd. A brief glance at the flames lingered in their gaze, as if searching for a signal hidden in the heat. Harriet noted the way Lucy's stare held the glass now empty, the way the bartender's jaw tightened, but she kept her focus on the uneasy movement of the union members. The tremor seemed to coil in the air, a knot of suspicion tightening around the onlookers, a sense that secret pacts might be shifting in the shadows. The ember‑streaked air brushed her cheeks, carrying the faint scent of charred pine from the fire. Beyond the pit, the street opened toward the diner district, the narrow lane slick with recent rain and littered with ash. Harriet stepped forward, the cobbles cold under her boots, the murmuring chant fading behind her as she moved toward the neon‑glow of the diners.
