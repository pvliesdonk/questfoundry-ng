Harriet stood near the cracked stone wall of the diner's backroom as Old Man Well spoke low, his words grinding like unseen gears beneath the floorboards. The air carried a thin metallic taste that brushed her tongue, a hint of fear she could almost swallow. She listened to the phrase about hidden mechanisms and felt a fresh suspicion rise that tunnels might wound through the mill beneath the town. She did not wait. She slipped the crumpled warning note into the inner pocket of her coat, the paper as thin as a moth wing. A faint scrape cut through the diner's clamor, a thin sound like a blade drawn from wood. Harriet turned, eyes catching a narrow seam where a floorboard had been pried loose. An ivory‑handled knife lay embedded in the gap, its polished surface catching the lantern light and flaring for a heartbeat. The blade rested untouched, but the gleam on the handle suggested recent use. The crowd’s roar swallowed the moment, yet the image of the knife lingered like a shadow across the stone wall. Harriet pressed a hand to the scar on her cheek, feeling the cold bite of blood as a reminder of the night’s violence. She stared at the knife, the hidden tunnel possibility now a weight in her mind, and tightened her grip on the note, ready to follow whatever path the metal hinted at.
