Edgar slipped through the infirmary doorway, the antiseptic smell sharp in his nostrils. Thea entered the room, her coat buttoned tight, the rolled sleeve covering the fresh scar on her right wrist. She kept her gaze on the cracked floor, turning her head away from Lucy as she moved toward an empty cot. Lucy leaned against the far wall, her lantern casting a narrow circle, and whispered to Thea about the town’s past donor‑fraud scandal that might have stained the infirmary’s name. Thea’s shoulders tightened and she stiffened, as if the rumor had landed on her skin. Harriet stood by the wooden desk, pulled a stack of charts aside, and unfolded a newspaper clipping; the headline suggested a nurse‑donor fraud controversy, and she slipped the paper into the inner pocket of her coat. The low tick of the bomb in the community hall drummed through the stone walls, each pulse a hammer against Edgar’s ears. He felt the cold barrel of his revolver at his hip and pressed his shoulder against the heavy door, pushing it a fraction inward toward the hall where the ticking grew louder. A thin draft slipped through the cracked window, carrying the scent of oil and rust. Edgar glanced at the lantern that flickered on the desk, its light catching the polished shine of the ornate knife still in its evidence locker. He wondered if the whispered warning would draw attention to Thea, but the sound of the ticking kept his focus. With a steady breath he stepped into the hallway, the door groaning as it gave way to the growing urgency of the pulse.
