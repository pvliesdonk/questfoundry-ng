Harriet stood beside the rusted firepit, the heat curling up like breath in the night. The Blackwood ledger, its pages already browned, thudded onto the emberbed grate and began to curl and blacken. She remembered Mayor Caldwell's speech from the council hall, his gold tie glinting as he promised to cleanse the town's image by erasing every stain. His words had sounded like a command to burn, not to hide, and the flames now answered both. Harriet felt the scar on her cheek sting as the fire licked the edges, and a cold resolve settled in her gut. The blaze turned the ink to ash, and with each crack she saw the lies and the debts dissolve together. She knew the fire was not merely destroying paper but also the cover the powerful families wore. A gust from the square brushed the soot‑blackened windows, carrying the chant of the farmers still echoing beyond the diner district. The heat pressed against her coat, urging her steps toward the crowded streets where the mayor waited to speak again. Harriet tightened the grip on her revolver, the metal cold as steel, and turned her gaze to the path that led away from the pit. The fire burned, and she would let it decide what truth survived.
