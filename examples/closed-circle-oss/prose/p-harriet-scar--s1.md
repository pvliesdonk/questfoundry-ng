Harriet Wellman's scar caught the flickering lantern light, a gritty line of pale flesh that seemed to pulse with the dim glow. The scar reminded her, in a way steel never could, that the badge she wore bore its own cuts. She stood behind the cracked oak counter, badge pressed to her chest, revolver at her hip, watching the remnants of the explosion settle into ash. Lucy Frost cradled her injured palm, the fresh dark scar on her right hand still slick with blood, and she slipped the blood‑stained ivory knife back onto the table with a trembling hand. The metal clink sounded louder than the hush that followed the bomb's death throes. Across the room, Clifford Moon lurked near the doorway, his coat splattered with the same crimson that stained Lucy's palm. He glanced toward the narrow hallway that ran behind the kitchen, his eyes flicking to the open latch in the floorboards. Harriet's gaze lingered on the scar again as the lantern sputtered, the light tracing its curve like a warning. She felt the weight of the law settle into her bones, the scar a cold reminder that justice could wound as easily as it could shield. The hallway stretched dark, inviting yet uncertain, and the faint smell of ozone lingered in the stale air.
