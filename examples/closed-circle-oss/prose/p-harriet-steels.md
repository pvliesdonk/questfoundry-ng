Harriet stood behind the battered wooden counter, the lantern’s orange flare painting trembling shadows across the floor. Lucy’s voice cut through the clamor, a sharp command that pushed the patrons toward the swinging doors. The bomb in the back room ticked louder, each pulse a metal heartbeat that rattled the panes. Edgar Barnes lingered near the doorway, his handcuffs missing and his gaze fixed on the ticking device. Harriet felt the weight of her revolver at her hip and the cold press of her badge against her chest. A thin line of heat from the lantern fell across her cheek, catching the old scar and turning it to a flash of steel. She tightened the strap of her holster, the familiar snap echoing like a promise. The scent of soot and pine mingled with cold iron, filling the air. Harriet raised a hand, palm flat on the counter, and let the scar catch the light once more. Duty settled over her like a coat of iron, unmoving despite the bomb’s growing thunder. She glanced at the ledger lying open on the adjacent table, its pages yellowed and half‑turned, a reminder of the mayor’s hidden dealings. The firelight traced the edge of the notebook, casting a thin stripe across the wood. Harriet’s mind flicked to the protest arrests she had ordered, the faces of farmers still echoing in the council chamber. She pressed her thumb to the scar, feeling the old line as if it were a compass pointing toward what must be done. The ticking grew frantic, the metal clank louder, and Lucy’s shouts rose above it, urging anyone still inside to flee. Harriet took a breath, the cold air biting her lungs, and steadied herself for the moment when the device would finally give.
