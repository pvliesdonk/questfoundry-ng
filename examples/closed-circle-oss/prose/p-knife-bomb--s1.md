Harriet Wellman stepped into the alley behind the diner, the rain slicking the cobblestones beneath her boots. The streetlamp above flickered, throwing a thin wash of light on the wet stone where an ivory‑handled knife lay half‑buried. Its polish reflected the weak glow, a cold reminder that something had been dropped in haste. She knelt, brushed a hand over the blade, and noted the missing sheath, the empty leather that should have cradled it. The knife's edge caught a glint, then a dull hue as grime settled over the ivory.

A low tick rose from somewhere beneath the cracked floorboards, rhythmic and patient. Harriet's eyes widened a fraction; the sound had been lost amid the rush of patrons and the clamor of the alarm. She listened, the ticking growing clearer, each beat a hidden pulse of danger. The bomb, small and concealed, ticked unnoticed by the fleeing crowd, its timer winding toward a moment she could not afford to miss.

She glanced back toward the diner doorway, where Lucy's voice had carried the warning. The alley stretched ahead, the knife glinting like a promise of conflict, the ticking a silent countdown. Harriet's badge pressed against her chest, the weight of her revolver at her hip steadied her hands. She made a decision, the threat of the unseen device sharpening her resolve as she turned toward the building, ready to act.
