The rain‑slick cobblestones glistened under the dim sodium glow of the street lanterns. Harriet stood at the mouth of the alley, badge pressed to her chest, the scar on her cheek catching the light. She looked down the stone slab that led away from the diner and saw the tip of an ivory‑handled knife half‑buried in the grime. Its blade caught a stray reflection, a brief flash that seemed out of place among the puddles. Harriet knelt, fingers brushing the cold metal, noting the missing sheath and the fresh smear of dried blood that hinted at a second weapon.

A low, rhythmic tick drifted up from the shadows, barely audible over the wind that rattled the shutters. The sound came from deeper within the walls, hidden behind the crates that littered the passage. Harriet's eyes narrowed; the ticking did not belong to any clock she had heard in the market. She pressed the metal of the knife against her palm, feeling the chill of steel and the weight of unseen danger. The radio at her belt buzzed faintly, the channel for the bomb squad blinking green. She held the knife a moment longer, then rose, badge gleaming, hand already moving toward the receiver.

She weighed whether to shout for the bomb squad, the ticking growing louder.
