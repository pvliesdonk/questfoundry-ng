The firepit's heat rose, sending thin tongues of flame that brushed the stone floor. The orange glow caught a glint where ash had shifted, exposing the ivory tip of a knife that lay half buried in the cinders. The polished handle threw a brief flash, a cold reminder that a blade had once drawn blood. Lucy's eyes narrowed on the relic, and she pressed the barrel of her revolver into her palm. She muttered low, “That weapon belonged to the old clockmaker whose revenge was spoken of in every tavern.” Harriet leaned forward, fingers digging into the fabric of her coat, and she stared at the knife as if weighing a story against the smoldering pages. The ledger on the table hissed, its edges curling toward the fire, while the iron scent of the furnace filled the room. Around them the diner's regulars shifted in their seats, faces illuminated by the flickering light, each breath a puff of gray. Lucy counted the seconds the fire hissed, feeling the weight of the revolver settle deeper in her grip. She knew the town's grudges ran like veins beneath winter stone, and that a single spark could resurrect old wounds. The ivory handle seemed to pulse for a heartbeat, a silent promise that the past could cut anew.
