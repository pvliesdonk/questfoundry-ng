The rain had driven a cold wind through the cracked pine beams of the diner district, and the iron gate behind the firepit loomed like a toothy maw. Lucy felt the damp draft curl around the soot‑blackened windows as she stood near the bar, the revolver heavy against her hip. A hand from the kitchen thrust a folded sheet onto the counter; the ink was smeared, the letters jagged and urgent, demanding the return of the ivory‑handled knife. The note lay beside a jar of sugar, its edges frayed by the kitchen’s rush.

Clifford Moon leaned over the polished surface of the knife, his slicked‑back hair catching a flash of lantern light. He lifted the weapon, its ivory sheen catching his eye for a heartbeat, and then slipped the note into the inside pocket of his coat. At the same instant Mayor Caldwell entered the back room where the rusted firepit served as a makeshift incinerator. He pulled a heavy leather ledger from the stack and flipped it open, the battered cover sighing as the pages fanned.

The mayor’s eyes moved across the ledger’s ink‑stained columns, his gaze pausing at names written in red and at debts marked with a blackened X. He traced a line with his thumb, the weight of each entry pressing down like a cold gear in a clock. Lucy watched his expression tighten as he read his own name among the obligations, the same ledger that had been missing since the clerk’s murder.

Around them the diner murmured, the clatter of plates and low conversation masking the tension. Clifford turned toward the dim hallway, the knife now hidden beneath his coat. The firepit’s orange glow cast long shadows across the floor, and the wind rattled the metal shutters with a metallic clang. Lucy’s hand tightened on the revolver, the metal of the grip steady against her palm, as she weighed the next move in a room where every object seemed to hold a threat.
