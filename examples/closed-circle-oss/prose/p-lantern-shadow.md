Marjorie Lynch stood at the register, her fingertips resting on the brass cash drawer as the oil lantern on the bar flickered. The wavering flame sent thin bars of light across the cracked pine floor, and the shadows it drew stretched toward the far wall where Harriet Wellman leaned over a ledger. Each pulse of darkness seemed to carve a thin line of dread in the back of Marjorie's mind. She watched the lantern's glow dance over the soot‑blackened tiles, feeling a cold certainty settle like iron in her throat. Outside the narrow windows, the silhouette of the old mill rose against the night, its broken roof outlined by the winter wind. The lantern's light caught the edge of a dark parcel being slipped between two cloaked figures near the doorway, and Marjorie's breath caught for a moment. She noted the exchange without turning, the thin smell of oil mixing with the faint scent of coal. The shadows lengthened, imprinting a feeling of looming treachery that she could not shake. She tightened the strap of her apron and turned her gaze toward the mill, the lantern still wavering, as if urging her forward. She felt the weight of the town's quiet rumors settle on her shoulders, and she knew the next step would bring her closer to the hidden danger.
