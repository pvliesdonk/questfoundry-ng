The garden behind the diner lay under a thin veil of frost, the wind slipping between cracked stone walls. Lucy followed the scent of damp earth to the plot where Harriet knelt beside a wilted rosebush, a torn piece of parchment clutched in her hand. The fragment gleamed in the lantern light, its ragged edge catching a stray spark that seemed to pulse with the same rhythm as the ticking device Lucy had just silenced. Harriet pressed the scar on her cheek against the paper, her voice steady as she asked Edgar Barnes why his alibi placed him far from this ground. Edgar lifted his spectacles, his thin fingers trembling, and muttered a rhyme about shadows and stone. While Lucy kept her eyes on the bomb case half‑buried under the bench, she felt a cold splinter bite her palm as she pried the inner panel apart. She pressed a torn cloth to the wound, the blood staining the damp soil as the copper coil hissed once more. Harriet reached for the set of iron handcuffs she kept in her coat, snapped them on Edgar's wrists, and tightened the clasp as Lucy wrestled the bomb's red wire. The metal bite of the cuffs rang against the garden stone, a reminder of the mayor's demand for a swift arrest. Mayor Caldwell stepped into the garden, his gold tie bright against the grey sky, and placed a hand on Harriet's shoulder, urging her to act before panic spread. Lucy pulled the red wire free, a small spark sputtering before the charge could surge, and the humming ceased. With the bomb's heart quiet, Harriet lifted her gaze to the ledger fragment, then turned to Edgar, her eyes softening. "He goes free," she said, unfastening the handcuffs and letting the metal clatter to the ground. The freed man stepped back, his spectacles catching the lantern glow, and vanished toward the shadowed lane beyond the garden wall. Lucy felt the warmth of the wrench still in her hand, the cut on her palm throbbing beneath the glove as the night air carried the scent of pine and iron. The garden fell silent, the torn ledger fragment resting on the cold earth, waiting for the next decision.
