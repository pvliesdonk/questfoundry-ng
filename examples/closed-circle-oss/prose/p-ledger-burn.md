Lucy kept her eyes on the back room where Harriet leaned into Clifford, her hand gripping the coat edge tighter. The deputy’s stare cut through Clifford’s smooth smile, and a low murmur slipped past the clatter of plates. A fragment of conversation floated by, a whispered talk of the ledger that seemed to settle like ash on the air. 

Harriet turned her head toward the voice and caught a handful of words: the ledger could ruin families, a warning spoken in hushed tones. She held the information in the space between her brows, the rumble of the rumor echoing in the room’s stale heat. 

Lucy watched the shift in Harriet’s shoulders and then spoke. She turned to Harriet without breaking her stare and said, “The firepit behind the town hall will take that paper for good.” She pointed toward the rusted firepit, its orange glow trembling against the stone floor. The heat stirred the ash beside the ledger, revealing the tip of an ivory‑handled knife half‑buried in the cinders. A fresh mud‑boot print lay beside the smoldering ledger, a silent clue that someone had slipped away unnoticed. Lucy kept her revolver steady in her palm, the metal cool against her skin, as the three of them measured the choice between burning secrets and preserving them.
