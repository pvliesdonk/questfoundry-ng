Mayor Caldwell stood at the council table, his gold tie pressed tight against the collar of his coat. Harriet's notes drifted across the oak surface, a thin rustle that seemed to carry a cold that pushed through the hall. The air grew sharper, and the chill wrapped itself around his shoulders like dry wind slipping through a cracked window. He felt the pressure of that cold settle deeper, a weight that pressed against his spine. A frost now blanketed the streets of Grayhaven, turning the cobblestones to pale glass under the lanterns. The townspeople moved in muted steps, their breaths forming vapor that hung in the night. Without the Blackwood Ledger, a hollow sense settled over the market square, as if the town had lost a stone that once held its balance. Caldwell watched the empty space where the ledger had been, noting how its removal left families exposed and the council's authority trembling. He recalled the command he had given to the furnace keeper, the crackle of flame that turned the book to ash, and the relief that had washed over him when the last page fell. Now the absence of ink and numbers left a gap that whispered through conversations, a reminder that debts could no longer be hidden behind parchment. His eyes drifted to the council bench where the aldermen sat rigid, their shoulders hunched as the cold seeped into the room. The frost‑cloaked streets stretched beyond the square, each footfall muffled by the ice.
