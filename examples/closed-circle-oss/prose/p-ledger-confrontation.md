Her revolver rested in her palm as rain drummed against the tin roof above the diner district. Mayor Caldwell opened the Blackwood ledger and let his eyes sweep the column of names. Clifford Moon leaned over the table, his glossy coat catching the lantern’s glow, and his gaze snagged on a line where the surname Moon appeared beside a red‑inked debt. His jaw tightened and his expression darkened as the realization settled.\n\nThe mayor’s face hardened the moment his thumb rested on that entry, the lines around his mouth tightening like a clenched fist. Harriet Wellman stepped forward, the scar on her cheek briefly catching the firelight, and fixed Clifford with a stare that held the weight of accusation. She pressed him for the ivory‑handled knife, her voice low but steady, each question pressing harder than the last. Lucy watched the uneasy tension rise, the air between them thickening as Harriet’s hand curled tighter on her coat.\n\nFrom the bar Lucy kept her eyes on the exchange, noting how Clifford’s shoulders shifted, his posture tightening as Harriet’s questions cut sharper. He offered a brief, tight reply that did not ease the pressure, his words falling flat over the clatter of plates. The orange glow of the rusted firepit behind town hall painted wavering shadows across the stone floor, its heat promising to turn paper to cinder. The ledger lay open on the table, its red ink a silent threat to any family still bound by its pages. Lucy’s grip stayed steady, the metal cool against her skin, as she measured the volatile scene.
