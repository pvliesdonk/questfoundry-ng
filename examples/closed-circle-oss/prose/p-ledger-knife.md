Harriet stood beside the crumbling stone wall of the old mill, the night air thin and sharp. Old Man Well leaned against the same wall, his wiry frame a shadow against the broken plaster. He whispered, his voice low, that the Blackwood ledger had been slipped into a hidden compartment beneath the floorboards. Harriet watched his fingers trace the seam where the timber met the stone, the grain dark with age. He pressed a rusted nail and a shallow cavity opened, revealing a narrow cavity lined with dust. Inside, a stack of ledger pages lay tucked, their blackwood covers still intact. The old clockmaker nodded, his eyes fixed on the hidden stash, and then fell silent. A sudden orange flare burst from the furnace at the far end of the room, and in that brief blaze an ornate ivory‑handled knife caught the firelight, its polished edge glinting as it protruded from a splintered beam. The blade hung like a frozen thunderbolt, the light bathing its surface before the glow faded back to soot‑blackened shadows. Harriet reached forward, the cold metal of the ledger pressing against her palm, while the knife’s point remained fixed in the wood. She felt the weight of the secret lodged in the darkness of the mill, the ledger concealed and the knife exposed, each a clue to the crimes that haunted Grayhaven.
