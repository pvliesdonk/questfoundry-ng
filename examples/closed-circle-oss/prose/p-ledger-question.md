Lucy stood on the stone step at the mouth of the coal‑furnace basement, amber heat spilling up from the coal fire below. The air tasted of soot and metal, and the furnace hiss mixed with the low murmur of the gathered townsfolk. Mayor Caldwell held the Blackwood ledger in his left hand, his gold tie catching the furnace light as his eyes moved across the columns of debts. He traced the inked names with a steady finger, and when his gaze fell on the line that bore his own name, his jaw tightened and a scowl settled on his face. Harriet stepped forward, her trench coat brushing the gritty stone floor, and stopped beside Clifford Moon. Harriet’s voice cut through the murmur, demanding Clifford explain how the ornate knife was tied to the recent murder. She pointed at the ivory‑handled blade, its bright polish stark against the dark wood of the table, the sheath conspicuously absent. Clifford’s smile faded; he said nothing, his eyes narrowing on the knife. Harriet pressed her question again, fingers gripping the edge of the ledger as she repeated the accusation, her tone sharper than before. While she spoke, Mayor Caldwell’s scowl deepened; he pressed his thumb against the ledger’s worn leather, the weight of his own name pressing down like a cold stone. Lucy watched the tension rise, the furnace’s steady hiss marking each heartbeat in the cramped chamber. The iron‑bound doors beyond the furnace loomed, their hinges cold and unmoving. Lucy felt the cold metal of her revolver against her hip and sensed the pull of the stairway that led deeper into the coal‑furnace basement.
