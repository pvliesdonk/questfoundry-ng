Clifford Moon lingered behind the counter of Marjorie's diner, the thin dawn light spilling across soot‑blackened panes and dust‑caked floorboards. The scent of boiled beans hung heavy beside the faint tang of coal from the furnace beyond the back door. He slipped his hand into the inner pocket of his coat and let a low syllable escape, "leeches," the word curling into the room like a secret breath. Marjorie Lynch, her flour‑dusted apron brushing her knees, caught the sound as she poured coffee for the off‑duty constable. She turned her head, eyes narrowing, as if the whisper had brushed against her ear.

Lucy Frost entered the diner's doorway, the clack of her boots echoing against the cracked pine floor. She swept a muscular arm across the bar, her shaved head catching the flicker of the oil lamp. Her gaze fixed on Marjorie, and she spoke in a blunt tone, "Don't accuse without proof, Marjorie. A rumor like that could set the whole town on fire." The warning hung in the stale air, the weight of her words pressing against the thin walls.

Lucy continued, her stare as cold as the iron hinge on the kitchen door, "If you stir the pot without solid proof, thefolk will turn on each other and chaos will blossom from that little spark." The diner's regulars fell silent, the hum of the furnace a distant thrum beneath the tension. Clifford felt the pressure of the moment settle over his shoulders, the code he had muttered now a knot of danger in his chest. He watched the exchange, aware that any false move could push Grayhaven toward a fracture he did not intend to make.

Outside, the market stalls began to unfurl their awnings, the rust‑red canvas catching the morning wind. The cobblestones glistened with the night's dew, and the distant clatter of carts hinted at the day’s bustle. Clifford caught a fleeting glimpse of the street beyond the diner, a path that could carry him deeper into the town’s restless veins.
