Lucy stood at the furnace’s edge, the iron door yawning like a mouth. The fire licked the low ceiling, throwing orange shards across the soot‑blackened walls. She felt the heat settle on her skin, a steady pulse matching the rhythm of the flames. Across the room, Deputy Harriet Wellman kept her badge pressed to her chest, the scar on her cheek catching the glow. The scar throbbed, a low red beat Lucy read as resolve. Harriet’s eyes narrowed, not on Lucy but on the bundle of papers tucked beneath a coal slab – the evidence the town had fought to keep hidden.

Lucy’s right palm bore a fresh, dark line where a blast of heat had left its mark weeks before; the scar was a reminder of the danger she had survived. She glanced at the bundle, then at the growing blaze, and let a piece of timber fall into the furnace. The flame surged, swallowing the papers in a roar that cut short any chance of their being read. The heat hissed against the metal, and Lucy’s hand hovered over the still‑warm ash, feeling its lingering warmth.

Harriet’s throat tightened, but she made no move to halt the inferno. Instead she stared at the empty space where the documents had lay, her scar pulse steady as the fire claimed the last proof. In that silent accord, Lucy chose preservation of the town’s fragile peace over the truth that might shatter it.
