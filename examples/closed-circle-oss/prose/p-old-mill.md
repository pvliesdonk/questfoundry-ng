The cracked windows of the Old Mill flickered with ash‑colored light, revealing a faint glimpse of the Blackwood Ledger tucked among the debris. Harriet Wellman stepped inside, the scent of soot heavy in the stale air. A low murmur from the Farmer Union drifted through the smoke, imprinting a wary awareness of collective silence on the gloom. The ledger lay smudged with ash, its pages still bound and concealed, a quiet accusation waiting in the shadows. She brushed aside broken wooden planks and knotted rope, feeling the weight of the ledger against her fingertips as if it were a concealed weapon. The distant diner's heated debate rose over the courtyard, clinking mugs and sharp words about truth and power cutting through the night air. Harriet listened, noting how the farmer's chorus softened only when the mayor's voice tried to smooth the edges. The ledger's edges curled, and a cold resolve settled in her chest; she could guard the evidence or return to the rally where accountability fought for a louder voice. The mill's stone floor creaked beneath her boots, echoing the tension that thrummed like unseen gears. She weighed the choice to hide the smudged ledger in her coat and rejoin the debate, or to linger in the mill's ash‑colored gloom, letting the whispers settle into dust.
