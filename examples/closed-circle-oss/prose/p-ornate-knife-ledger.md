Lucy felt the wrench still warm in her hand as the last pulse of the bomb faded. The metal slab beneath the counter caught a glimpse of ivory, and the Ornate Knife lay half‑buried in ash, its polished handle cold enough to bite her skin. She pressed her palm against the bomb’s cracked casing, keeping it steady while the red wire lay snapped beside her.

Harriet slipped through the back door, coat sweeping the dusty floor, and disappeared into the night. Lucy followed her gaze to the garden beyond the diner, where a low lantern hung from a branch and threw a thin circle of light on a crumpled page. Harriet knelt, unfolded the torn fragment of the ledger, and brushed dust from the edge. The ink formed a half‑written name, the letters rough as broken pottery, and hung in the air like dust.

Lucy watched the scene, the knife’s chill reflecting in the lantern glow, the fragment’s name lingering in her mind. The bomb’s heart lay disarmed on the counter, its red wire snapped cleanly away. The night wind slipped through the cracked pine windows, carrying the scent of pine and iron. In the hush that followed, the ivory‑handled blade remained a silent warning, its edge whispering danger.
