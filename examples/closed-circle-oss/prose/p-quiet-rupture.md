June 14, 1887

I set my pen to the ledger and recorded the night’s grim order. I wrote that Edgar Barnes was placed in iron cuffs and taken to the holding cell, his face ashen in the lantern light. The report of his detention traveled through the room as a thin gust, and I felt the reverberation of a far‑off gunshot pulse through the walls, a reminder that peace was still fragile.

Lucy Frost emerged from the back, a forearm darkened by a fresh bruise where the wrench had slipped. She held the wound steady, her breath shallow, and told me the bomb’s red wire had given under her grip. The device lay limp on the floor, its casing dented, the ticking that had rattled the diners now silent. I noted the blood that stained her skin and the missing sheath of the ornate knife that had been left on the slab.

The townsfolk gathered around the battered counter, eyes shifting between the subdued deputy and the trembling mechanic. I sensed murmurs of disapproval rising against me for taking a teacher into custody, but also a thin current of relief that the explosive threat had been removed. I wrote that the balance in Grayhaven hovered between the firm hand of the law and the chaotic edge of unrest.

I closed the journal, tucked the page into my coat, and stepped out into the cold night. The mill’s dark silhouette loomed beyond the street lamps, and the echo of that distant report lingered behind me, a reminder that order and disorder walk the same road.
