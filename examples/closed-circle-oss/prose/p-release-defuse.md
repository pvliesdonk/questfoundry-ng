Mayor Caldwell adjusted his gold tie as the lantern light pooled on the cobblestones. He stepped onto the council steps and raised his voice, reminding Harriet Wellman that any further mercy would force the council to trim the precinct's budget. Harriet pressed the badge to her chest, her scar catching the glow, and answered that the evidence did not bind Edgar Barnes, so he would walk free.

Edgar moved away from the square, his thin coat flapping in the cold wind. He tossed a singed fragment of paper onto the stone, and the scrap fluttered briefly before settling among the stones. The crowd watched his silhouette dissolve into the line of trees that bordered the promenade.

Lucy Frost knelt beside the battered bar and lifted the bomb's casing with a steel wrench. Her fingers, raw from the metal, tightened around the red cord until it snapped, a hiss fading into the night. A small flame licked the fresh wound on her forearm, and she pressed a rag to the cut as a bead of sweat traced her temple.

From the edge of the square the farmer union raised their tools and sang a steady chant, their banners unfurling beside the council steps. The men gathered around Harriet, their solidarity a visible counterweight to the elite's pressure, and their shouts lifted the atmosphere with a thin thread of hope.

Mayor Caldwell watched the union's rally and felt the weight of his warning settle like a stone in his gut. He noted the way the crowd's voices swelled against his caution, the image of the town he guarded trembling with each chant.

Harriet slipped a leather‑bound journal from her coat and opened it on a wooden crate. She wrote in a steady hand about distant fires on the horizon and the quiet resolve that steadied her now that the danger had passed. The ink did not tremble, and the entry closed without naming a decision on Edgar's arrest.

Lucy steadied herself after the device was rendered harmless. Her forearm throbbed, and her hands trembled as she set the wrench down, the pain a dull echo beneath the calm of the night.

Mayor Caldwell surveyed the settled square, aware that his warning about funding lingered even as the bomb's threat vanished. The town's image hovered on the brink of a new choice, balanced between his caution and the union's renewed confidence.
