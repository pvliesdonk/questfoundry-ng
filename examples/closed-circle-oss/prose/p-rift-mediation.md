Lucy stood near the iron furnace, the heat curling up the stone walls of the Old Mill. The clang of metal and the low hiss of coal filled the vaulted space as townsfolk gathered on the soot‑blackened floor. Harriet Wellman lingered at the edge of the crowd, her trench coat swaying, scar tracing a line on her cheek. She watched the farmers and the merchants trade barbs, the distance between them widening like a crack in cracked pine. Lucy noted Harriet’s eyes narrow as she took in the strained faces of growers shivering under the elite’s polished stare.

Harriet’s gaze locked on Mayor Caldwell, who stood atop the ledger table with his gold‑tied tie catching the furnace glow. He raised a hand and spoke of mediation, his voice smooth and controlled. Lucy heard the words spill out, promising a meeting that would bridge the divide. The tension between the two was palpable; Harriet’s stare held a hard, questioning edge while Caldwell’s smile did not quite reach his eyes.

Mayor Caldwell continued, promising to bring the two sides together, but his tone carried an undercurrent of warning. Lucy sensed the thinness of his reassurance, a hint that any peace might be forced rather than earned. The furnace lit the basement deeper, casting long shadows that danced across the stone floor. In that dim light, Lucy felt the weight of the moment settle like cold steel, a promise that could either mend the rift or tighten its grip.

She tightened the strap of her revolver and waited, the air heavy with the scent of coal and the unspoken threat that lingered beyond the glow of the furnace‑lit basement.
