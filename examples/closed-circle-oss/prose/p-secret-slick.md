Harriet stepped into the dim light of the tunnel's mouth, the bruise‑darkened skin on her forearms aching under the chill that seeped from the stone. The walls were slick with fresh blood, a thin film that caught the dying torch glow and spread it like rust across the cracks. The weight of what she had found in the hidden chamber pressed against her chest as heavily as her badge, the iron clasp digging into her ribcage. She heard the muffled rustle of the crowd that had followed Silas, their boots echoing on the damp floor, and the low groan of the old mill's gears far above.

Ahead the tunnel opened onto a narrow stair cut into the foundation, its steps worn smooth by decades of footfall. At the bottom a doorway yawned, its wooden frame mottled with soot. Lucy stood in the gloom, a small lantern turned into a harsh flashlight that sliced through the settled dust. The beam fell on rows of stacked tables, their surfaces coated in a fine layer of ash that drifted like snow in the beam.

Harriet felt the air grow colder, the scent of iron and old grain mixing with the metallic tang of blood. She raised the ivory‑handled knife she had taken, its edge still glinting, and turned her gaze toward the cellar entrance, knowing the next step would lead her deeper into the mill’s hidden heart.
