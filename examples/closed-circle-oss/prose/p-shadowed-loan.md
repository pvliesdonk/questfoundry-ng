Lucy kept her revolver against her hip as she leaned against the makeshift bar that had been cobbled from an old crate. The iron‑bound doors of the Old Mill’s coal furnace loomed behind the patrons, their hinges cold against the soot‑blackened walls. The furnace hissed, sending a thin plume of heat upward through the vaulted space. Mayor Caldwell stood near the center of the room, a gold tie catching the amber glow as he opened the Blackwood ledger with a steady hand. Clifford Moon slipped the ledger across the rough table, his slicked‑back hair catching the light.

Clifford read the line that listed a loan tied to the Moon family and his brow tightened. His expression darkened with a sudden realization as the inked debt stared back at him. He lowered his eyes to the entry, his jaw setting like cold iron.

Mayor Caldwell’s face hardened over the same entry, the same black scrawl that now marked his own name. Lucy saw the mayor’s thumb press against the worn leather binding, the pressure visible in the set of his fingers. Harriet Wellman stepped forward, her trench coat scraping the stone floor, and placed a firm hand on Clifford’s arm. Harriet pressed her question again, her voice cutting through the murmur of the crowd. The tension rose as the mayor’s stare met hers, both aware that the ledger could ruin families if its secrets fell into the wrong hands.

Lucy watched the uneasy exchange from her bar, noting the way Harriet’s eyes flicked to the ledger and then to the furnace’s glow. Whispered rumors drifted through the crowd, suggesting the ledger held power to bring down the town’s elite. Harriet’s tone grew sharper, and Clifford’s smile vanished, leaving only a thin line of defiance. The furnace hummed louder, as if marking each heartbeat in the cramped chamber. Lucy felt the metal of her revolver press warm against her side, a reminder of the thin line between order and chaos.

The choices stand before her, each a path through the heat and the secrets that burn.
