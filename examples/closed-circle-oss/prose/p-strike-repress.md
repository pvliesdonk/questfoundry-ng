Mayor Caldwell stood on the raised dais beside the brass‑bound safe, the Blackwood Ledger still sealed within its iron frame. The winter wind pressed against the stone walls of the square, and the frost made the cobblestones gleam like dull glass. Harriet Wellman stepped forward, her gray hair pulled tight and a scar on her cheek catching the lantern light. She lifted her hand and barked, "Arrest them now." The watchman at the edge of the crowd drew a pair of iron cuffs from his belt and moved toward the wooden table where the farmer‑union members had gathered. Mayor Caldwell watched as the watchman snapped the cuffs around each farmer's wrists. The chanting that had risen moments before fell to a thin sigh, and a sudden hush settled over the crowd. The farmers knelt, their faces set, while Harriet inspected each set of hands with practiced efficiency. A young farmer shouted a promise of retaliation, and his voice carried through the silent square. The union members rose, their banners drooping, and began to file out of the square, their tools clutched tightly. Mayor Caldwell noted the anger in their eyes as they disappeared down the winding streets, a low murmur of vengeance drifting behind them. From the doorway of Marjorie Lynch's diner, Marjorie emerged, her eyes flicking to the safe. She lowered her voice and warned Caldwell that the ledger in the safe contained debts that could ruin his office. Mayor Caldwell stepped toward Harriet, clasped his hand to hers, and thanked her for keeping order. The lingering threat of the union flickered in his mind, a cold current that would not be easily quelled.
