Thea stood before the diners, the iron door behind her groaning as it settled. She felt the weight of the room on her coat as she unfolded the truth that had driven her from the infirmary. She spoke of forged records, of a name whispered in corridors, of the betrayal that had forced her exile. The words fell heavy, like cold steel on the worn table.

A murmur rose, then sharpened into a heated exchange. Some townsfolk demanded that she be held accountable, their voices sharp as the clink of mugs. Others called for forgiveness, their tones softer yet firm. The debate crackled around her, voices overlapping like gears in the old mill.

Lucy moved from behind the bar, her shaved head catching the lantern light. She set a glass of amber whiskey on the nearest seat and slid it toward Thea. Their eyes met, and in that brief glance Thea read a shared resolve forged in past fights.

Mayor Caldwell rose, his gold tie immaculate despite the soot staining his cuffs. He urged Thea to leave Grayhaven, to spare the town’s fragile image from the stain of her confession. His voice was smooth, as practiced as a speech he had delivered before crowds.

Harriet stepped forward, her badge pressed to her chest and the scar on her cheek catching the lantern’s flicker. She challenged the mayor, reminding him that the town needed truth more than a tidy veneer. Her tone was blunt, and she pressed the point that accountability mattered more than a clean reputation.

Around the table, the debate continued, some nodding, some shifting uneasily, the tension humming like unseen machinery. Thea sensed the town’s mood shifting, a tentative hope threading through the dread.

Beyond the heavy oak doorway that led to the alley, the Old Mill loomed, its windows glowing with ash‑colored gloom. The back door stood ajar, a path into the soot‑stained interior where the Blackwood ledger might still lie.

The choice hung between staying to listen to the diner's debate and stepping into the mill’s ash‑colored gloom.
