Thea slipped through the diner’s swinging door as night pressed against the soot‑blackened windows. Fluorescent tubes hummed overhead, their cold glare washing the room in a pallid wash. Beneath that light the secret she bore throbbed like a low thrum, steady against the ribs of her coat. She moved to a corner booth, the chair scraping faintly on the worn linoleum, and kept her gaze on the patrons. Each glance lingered a fraction too long, eyes flickering to the metal wrench mounted on the pegboard and then to the humming metal box tucked beneath the counter. A thin red wire coiled around the bomb’s circuit, its copper strand glinting faintly in the fluorescent glow. The alarm lever perched beside the door caught a glint of streetlight, its metal edge sharp and untested. Lucy Frost stood behind the bar, her scarred palm resting on the counter, the fresh scar on her right hand still dark against the skin. Thea tightened the collar of her white coat, feeling the weight of fabric press against the chill in the air. The tension in the room knotted tighter with every shift of a shoulder or rustle of a napkin. The night deepened, the fluorescent hum remained steady, and the red wire quivered in the dim, waiting for a hand to decide its fate.
