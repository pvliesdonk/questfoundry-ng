A gray sky pressed down on the market square, the farmers' banner trembling like a stitched sigh. Clifford Moon stepped through the cobblestones, the chill of evening settling on his coat as if ash were falling from a dying furnace. The streets carried low murmurs, voices of dissent slipping through the market stalls like a draft through cracked shutters. He noted Harriet Wellman's badge catching the wan light, the deputy's shoulders set firm beside the pole, her hand resting on the wood as if to steady the gathering. Around her, union leaders exchanged brief nods, their faces lit by the flicker of lanterns.

Clifford's eyes fell on the ornate knife sheathed at his hip, its ivory handle dulling under soot‑blackened light. He felt the weight of a damp envelope pressed into his pocket, the paper inside bearing a stark demand that the blade be returned. He eased the knife from its case, the steel cool against his palm, and glanced at the note, the ink still sharp. The murmurs rose a fraction, then fell, as if the wind itself hesitated.

He watched the banner's cloth ripple once more, the calm holding its breath before the storm. The steel in his hand seemed to pulse with the promise of a choice, waiting for his grip to give it voice.
