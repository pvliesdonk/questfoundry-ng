Lucy Frost stood beside the iron firepit, a cup of coffee steaming in her gloved hand. The half‑burned Blackwood Ledger lay on a rusted tray, its pages curling toward ash. Around the pit the square opened onto the diner district, neon signs flickering behind the stone walls. Harriet Wellman kept her revolver at her hip, eyes scanning the crowd. Lucy saw Harriet watch the farmers’ chant rise while the town’s elite lingered in polished silence, and Harriet’s brow furrowed, and she said, “The gap widens with each drumbeat.”

Mayor Caldwell stepped from the town‑hall doorway, his gold tie catching the firelight. He lifted a hand and spoke of mediation, his voice measured over the crackle of the flames. Lucy felt the tension tighten as Harriet’s stare met his; the air held a thin edge. Caldwell promised to bring the factions together, but the undercurrent in his tone suggested that order might be enforced as readily as the ledger could be turned to ash.

The firepit hissed, sending a thin plume of steam around Lucy’s cup. Lanterns flickered, boots echoed on cobbles, and the farmers’ chant rolled low like a tide against the elite’s calm murmur. Lucy tightened her grip on the revolver at her side, feeling the weight of the moment settle over the square, waiting to see whether words or fire would decide the town’s fate.
