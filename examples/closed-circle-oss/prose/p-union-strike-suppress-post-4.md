I pressed the pen to the battered page and let the ink bleed the truth I had tried to hide. The farmers I had cuffed marched out of the square with their heads bowed, their chants reduced to a thin hiss that lingered in the cold air. I watched them disappear down the cobbled lanes and felt the weight of each fallen voice settle on my shoulders. The order I imposed had bought a night of quiet, but the quiet was brittle, like frost cracking beneath a thin sheet of ice.\n\nWhen the watchmen filed the arrested men into the jail, I could see the suspicion in the townsfolk's eyes. Mothers whispered to their children, their fingers tracing the scar on my cheek as if it were a warning. The name Harriet Wellman had become a knot tied to the rope of the Union, a knot that tightened with each new arrest. I noted how the market stalls seemed emptier, how the farmer's union banners drooped, their colors dulled by the soot of suppression.\n\nI recalled the moment I ordered the cuffs, the firmness in my voice, the certainty that the law demanded it. Yet now I see the faces of the men I sent away, the empty seats at the town hall, and I hear the echo of promises of retaliation. The price of this order is measured not in fines or statutes, but in the widening chasm between my badge and the people I swore to protect.\n\nI closed the journal, feeling the scar on my cheek press against the leather binding, a reminder that the cost of peace can be a wound that never fully heals.
