Thea watched as Lucy pressed the edge of a crumpled note against the worn bar and slid it toward her. The paper was thin, its surface cold against Thea's palm, and the ink spelled a warning about listening ears hidden among the patrons. Lucy's shaved head caught the weak lantern light, and the fresh scar on her right palm traced a dark line that seemed to echo the warning. Thea folded the note back, feeling the paper's edge like a blade, and slipped it into the inner pocket of her coat. The tavern door thudded shut behind the last of the evening's visitors, and a hush settled over the room. Thea rose from the low bench, adjusted the stiff collar of her white coat, and paced the narrow space between the hearth and the bar. She kept her eyes on the empty booths, the shadows that pooled there, and the occasional glint of metal from the forge behind Lucy. Each breath she drew settled as a thin mist in the cold air, and her heart beat a steady thrum beneath her ribs, a pulse that matched the secret she carried. The night-filled diner seemed to swallow the hour, its walls humming with quiet. Outside, the street lamps flickered against a sky bruised with low clouds, and the town beyond the tavern's thin walls carried on unaware. No one questioned the nervous nurse who moved among the tables, nor did they hear the low hum of her concealed scandal. Thea stood watch until the first gray light crept through the soot‑blackened windows, the secret still hidden, the night still holding its breath.
