Cold wind drifted over the square, rattling the iron lanterns that hung above the council balcony. Edgar Barnes stood at the fringe of the gathering, his thin frame framed by the soot‑blackened stone walls. He watched Lucy Frost lean against the makeshift bar, the shaved scalp gleaming under the weak light, her revolver strap tight against her coat. He lifted his lips and whispered a riddle that curled around the Blackwood ledger, “When stone drinks the river’s mouth, what remains to tally debt?” His breath fogged the air between them, the words slipping into the crowd’s murmur. Lucy’s eyes narrowed, the steel of her stare matching the iron hinges of the nearby furnace. Edgar sensed the shift in the council’s faces, their brows tightening as the riddle settled over the strike’s chatter. He knew the workers’ protest was a surface, a distraction that veiled a deeper web of hidden accounts. The syllables landed like a stone skipping across water, each ripple urging Lucy to question the simple tally of labor and loot. He stepped back from the balcony, the coat rustling against cold stone, and let the distant hum of the mill’s gears fill the pause. The question hung in the frost, a knot tightening in Lucy’s thoughts as the council’s shadows stretched across the square.
