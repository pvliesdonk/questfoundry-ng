Mayor Caldwell watched the lantern light spill onto the square as a thin wind tugged at the cracked pine. Harriet Wellman stepped forward, badge pressed to her chest, and lifted a trembling hand. Her voice cut cleanly through the murmur, announcing that Edgar Barnes would be released, the alibi she had trusted holding enough doubt. Lucy Frost stood beside the battered bar, a cold steel wrench clenched in her fist, and pulled at the bomb’s casing. The red wire snapped under her grip, a hiss dying in the night, and a thin line of fire traced the wound on her forearm, staining the wool of her sleeve. The danger in the room fell away, leaving only the dull breath of the crowd. From the edge of the square a low chant rose, the farmer union rallying behind Harriet as the men tightened their grip on tools. Mayor Caldwell noted the tension easing, his gold tie catching the lantern’s glow.

Edgar Barnes slipped away after the announcement, his thin figure melting into the dark line of trees beyond the promenade. He dropped a half‑burned scrap of paper that flickered in the lantern light before it fell to the cobblestones. Lucy pressed a rag to her wound, the blood darkening the wood of the counter, and steadied the wrench as the last pulse of the device faded. The murder charge still hung in the air, unanswered, while Harriet lowered her hand, the metal handcuffs clattering to the ground. The woods swelled, the trees closing around Edgar’s silhouette as he vanished into shadow.

As the trees swallowed Edgar’s outline, Mayor Caldwell’s voice reached Harriet, warning that budget cuts were coming, a thin warning that cut through the quiet. Beyond the square, the old mill’s broken tower loomed, its arches casting deep shadows that seemed to invite a step into its dimmed walls. The night held its breath, the town’s image trembling on the edge of a new choice.
