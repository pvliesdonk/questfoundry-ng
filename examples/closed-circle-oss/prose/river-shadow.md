Cold water lapped the stone embankment as the river's hush settled over the narrow path that wound between the dead pines. The wind carried a thin mist that clung to Clifford Moon's coat, and each step he took sounded against the quiet like a muted drum. Up ahead a lone courier moved along the same trail, his boots splashing in shallow pools and a leather satchel swinging at his side. Clifford watched the messenger pause beneath the rusted iron hatch that marked the old mill's entrance, then continue toward the far side where the pine shadows gathered. The thought of the ornate ivory-handled knife pressed against his palm reminded him of the warning he had heard from Harriet Wellman earlier: evidence must not linger where it could be taken. He felt the blade's cold edge, its filigreed hilt catching a stray beam of dim light, and he made the decision to hand it over to Harriet as soon as he reached the mill. He slipped the knife into the inner pocket of his coat, feeling the weight settle obediently. The river's hush pressed tighter around the path, as if urging him onward, and the distant clang of an old gear in the mill's machinery echoed faintly. He quickened his pace, eyes fixed on the courier's retreating silhouette, aware that following it would take him deeper into the river's cold hush.
