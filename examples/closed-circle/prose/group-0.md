Beaumont Sterling presides over the grand foyer with the unyielding posture of a monarch defending his realm against the dual sieges of incivility and inclement weather. Beyond the reinforced glass of the entryway, a rather ill-mannered blizzard hurls itself against the manor, threatening to delay the evening's meticulously scheduled itinerary. Beaumont pays the meteorological tantrum absolutely no mind. He stands resplendent in his impeccably tailored velvet smoking jacket, his attention devoted entirely to the precise distribution of vintage champagne. His left eye executes a minute, rhythmic twitch as he hands a fluted glass to a shivering arrival, offering a smile of such rigid hospitality that it might have been carved from the very ice accumulating on the front drive.

The requisite murmurs of gratitude are exchanged with the appropriate degree of subdued warmth. One simply does not permit the burden of travel or the peril of sub-zero temperatures to interrupt the sacred sequence of initial introductions. With the damp overcoats surrendered to the cloakroom and the preliminary pleasantries successfully navigated without a single unpardonable faux pas, the congregation begins its migration. Beaumont herds his charges with the gentle but absolute authority of a seasoned orchestrator, guiding the assembly away from the drafty foyer and into the principal drawing room. The space is a sanctuary of heavily guarded refinement, anchored by a magnificent Persian rug that demands an almost religious reverence, currently free from any unsightly blemishes of spilled wine or wayward bodily fluids.

This fragile equilibrium of forced cordiality fractures the moment Sylvia Vance crosses the threshold. Draped heavily in pearls and wielding a sharp cashmere shawl with the aggressive precision of a duelist, she surveys the drawing room as though inspecting a regrettably substandard provincial inn. She makes a point of pausing before the velvet curtains, observing their texture with a distinctly uncharitable squint, before turning her gaze upon her host. Without the courtesy of a preamble, she announces that the decidedly drab upholstery does an exceptional disservice to the complexions of everyone present. She then inquires, with a volume entirely unsuited for polite indoor conversation, precisely when the culinary obligations of the evening will finally be fulfilled. Her demand settles over the room like a suffocating shroud. The remaining guests stiffen in unison, collectively bound by an unyielding devotion to good manners, leaving Beaumont to uphold his cheerful veneer while the social fabric of his gathering teeters on the precipice of absolute ruin.
