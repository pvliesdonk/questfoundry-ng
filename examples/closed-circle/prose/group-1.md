Beaumont retreats behind the sanctuary of the mahogany sideboard, snatching a tarnished silver serving tray and a linen cloth with the ferocity of a man preparing for a duel. The abrasive friction of cloth against silver serves as a mandatory physical outlet for the immense affront he has just endured. To critique a host's upholstery prior to the serving of hors d'oeuvres is an act of barbarism so profound it threatens the very foundations of civilized society. He rubs the metallic surface until his knuckles pale, seeking out an immaculate reflection that stubbornly refuses to manifest.

He murmurs a continuous, rhythmic litany of grievances directed at the tray, his tone hushed but intensely precise. Sylvia Vance operates under the severe delusion that royal lineage flows through her veins, demanding a level of deference that even actual monarchs might find excessive. Beaumont asserts to his distorted reflection that one cannot simply conjure an artisanal culinary triumph upon the immediate command of a guest who wields her accessories as blunt instruments. Her expectations constitute a direct assault on the orderly progression of the evening, a disruption tantamount to a physical assault upon the manor itself.

A sudden, sharp contraction in his lower abdomen forces him to momentarily halt his labor, an involuntary physical protest against the impropriety polluting his drawing room. He corrects his posture with absolute severity, for a gentleman of standing simply does not fold. He resumes the aggressive buffing, pointedly ignoring a phantom shadow that seems to dart along the periphery of his vision, and equally disregarding the highly troubling logistical nightmare currently occupying the walk-in pantry. Beaumont forcefully eradicates a stubborn smudge from the silver rim, whispering into the polished metal that if Madame Vance demands unimpeachable perfection, she is quite welcome to navigate the kitchen's myriad hazards and procure it herself.
