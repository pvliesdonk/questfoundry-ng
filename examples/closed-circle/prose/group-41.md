As he prepares to grasp the handles of the polished tray to complete this hazardous delivery, his gaze falls upon an unacceptable void resting beside the porcelain saucers. The designated placement for the crested silver sugar tongs remains entirely unoccupied. A profound stillness settles over his posture as the magnitude of this oversight crystallizes within his intellect.

This precise failure of preparation registers as a catastrophic collapse of civilized order. The presence of a deceased gentleman currently occupying a leather armchair in the adjacent study—requiring a continuous, elaborate pantomime involving a strategically positioned financial broadsheet to convince casual observers of a peaceful afternoon nap—constitutes a regrettable administrative burden. The absence of the proper implement for dispensing cubed sweeteners, however, represents a fundamental dereliction of the hostly arts. To implicitly require a guest of Mrs. Vance’s formidable pedigree to utilize her bare fingers for the transfer of sugar, or to degrade the service by offering a common stirring spoon, is an infraction that vastly exceeds the mere cessation of biological functions presently complicating the estate's schedule.

He fixes an unwavering stare upon the bare expanse of silver, acutely aware that his waiting patron possesses a discerning temperament entirely dedicated to the detection of such unforgivable lapses in decorum. The disposal of wayward corpses may be neatly managed with heavy curtains and strict discretion, yet a deficient tea service remains utterly exposed to severe scrutiny. The impending social reckoning approaches with absolute inevitability. Beaumont corrects the alignment of his waistcoat, gathering the fractured remains of his professional dignity to present the compromised tray, fully resolved to weather the ensuing lecture regarding his inexcusable lack of proper utensils.
