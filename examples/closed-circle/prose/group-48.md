As the novelty of Ms. Munroe's botanical interrogation threatens to wane, Beaumont perceives a distinct risk that the surviving guests might prematurely redirect their gaze toward the study and the deceased gentleman presently propped behind the evening broadsheet. A secondary diversion is swiftly becoming a logistical necessity. With deliberate composure, he extracts his gold-cased pocket watch from his silk waistcoat, testing the substantial weight of the chronometer within his palm. He evaluates the precise trajectory required to send the heavy instrument clattering into the furthest, shadowed corner of the adjoining parlor, calculating that a sudden metallic disruption would successfully command absolute visual priority from the assembled company.

However, as his thumb brushes the intricate enamel crest upon the lid, practical economics assert a forceful counterargument. The timepiece represents a staggering sum of ancestral wealth, and deliberately compromising its delicate Swiss escapement against the unforgiving oak floorboards constitutes an act of fiscal vandalism he is entirely unwilling to sponsor. He meticulously slides the watch back into its designated pocket, smoothing the fabric with immaculate care. It is profoundly improper to destroy one's own accoutrements merely to disguise a violent fatality, particularly when one's personal budget is already severely strained by the inevitable replacement costs of a blood-soiled Persian rug. Beaumont elects instead to elevate his chin and clasp his hands firmly behind his back, fortifying his hostly resolve as the frost-coated brass handles of the conservatory doors begin to rattle with considerable force upon their hinges.
