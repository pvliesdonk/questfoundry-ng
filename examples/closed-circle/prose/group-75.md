Sylvia reclines against an excessive arrangement of embroidered pillows, her complexion stubbornly retaining a distinctly unnatural shade of pallid olive. Rather than attribute her profound internal upheaval to the introduction of a lethal toxic agent into her digestive tract, she mounts a vigorous, meticulously articulated defense of her own biological superiority. She attributes her current prostration entirely to the lamentable quality of the botanical matter utilized in the drawing-room refreshment service. With a rigidly upheld chin, she declares that the Earl Grey served below stairs was demonstrably harvested from a distressingly common, economically priced cultivar, entirely unsuited for a palate of her refined aristocratic pedigree.

She firmly refuses the suggestion that a physician might be required, interpreting the very notion of medical intervention as an intolerable insult to her constitution. Instead, she issues a sweeping, multi-clause condemnation of Beaumont’s stewardship, asserting that the consumption of such deeply offensive, poorly fermented foliage would naturally provoke a severe rebellion within any person of genuine quality. Her insistence on diagnosing herself with nothing more than an acute case of culinary offense remains entirely unshakeable, even as a fresh spasm of involuntary muscular contraction forces her to clench the imported linens with an iron grip.

By attributing her catastrophic physical deterioration to a grievous lapse in domestic hospitality, she ensures her continued ignorance regarding the true, fatal nature of the afternoon service. This unwavering dedication to aristocratic denial affords Beaumont a precious window of unmonitored time on the primary floor. He may now attend to the deeply unseemly, viscous fluids pooling in various chambers without the threat of her immediate supervision, provided he can manage the delicate logistics of retrieving cleaning supplies from a pantry currently serving as a makeshift, highly compromising mausoleum.
