Elliot felt the air cling to his throat as the rain hammered the dock. Each inhale came thin, a ragged echo of vows he could no longer keep. The cold damp seeped through his coat, and his cough deepened, rattling his ribs like loose iron. He pressed his fingers against the jade dragon, its green veins cold against his skin, as if the stone could draw the fever away. The lantern above sputtered, casting flickering shadows over the water‑blackened planks. Around him, the distant clatter of crates and the muted thrum of the harbor faded beneath the hiss of his own breathing. He tried to speak, voice hoarse, but only a whisper rose, "Not today," he said, the words swallowed by the wind. The world narrowed to the steady patter of rain and the relentless drum of his heart. A shiver ran down his spine, not from cold but from the certainty that the illness was closing in, tightening its grip. He lowered his head, feeling the inevitable weight of his own collapse.
