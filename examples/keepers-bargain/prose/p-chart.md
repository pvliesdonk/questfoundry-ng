Elias clears the table with his forearm and unrolls the chart he came here to make, weighting its corners with sounding-leads. It is finished. The shoals lie on the paper the way they lie under the water, every false depth corrected in his careful ink, the soundings she watched him take set down in fair copy — and in the middle of it, where a prudent mapmaker would have written nothing at all, the hollow where the sleeper turns, drawn plain and unnamed, a depth with no bottom marked.

He does not say what it cost him to draw that and leave it nameless. He only steps back from the table, leaving the chart facing her, the lamp's slow gold crossing and recrossing the paper. The first honest map of this coast, and it is hers whichever way the morning goes. The truth of the water is on paper now; it does not say what she should do with it. That, too, is a kind of counsel.

She has to choose.
