They go back up to the lamp room together, out of the wind, though the wind finds its way in regardless through gaps her grandmother never sealed and Maren never has either. Elias stands where he stood the night she told him, hands loose at his sides, the lamp turning its slow gold behind them both.

He does not ask her to choose. She waits for him to — some version of him, some earlier version, might have — but the man in front of her has learned the weight of this room too well for that. He says only that he will stay if she stays, and sail if she sails, and that whichever way the water is deciding to break, it will not be him doing the deciding. His voice is steady. His eyes are not, quite.

Maren understands, standing there, that this is the kindest thing anyone has offered her since the ledger came into her hands: not rescue, not argument, just company inside the decision, and the decision left entirely hers. Outside, the tide keeps falling, and somewhere past the stilts the ship waits with its sails already loosed.

She has to choose. There is no more counsel to be had than this.
