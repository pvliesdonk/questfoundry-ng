They go back up to the lamp room together, out of the wind, though the wind finds its way in regardless through gaps her grandmother never sealed and Maren never has either. Elias stands where he stood the night she told him, hands loose at his sides, the lamp turning its slow gold behind them both.

He does not ask her to choose. She waits for him to — some earlier version of him might have — but he says only that he will stay if she stays, and sail if she sails, and that whichever way the water is deciding to break, it will not be him doing the deciding. His voice is steady. His eyes are not, quite. It is the kindest thing anyone has offered her since the ledger came into her hands: not rescue, not argument, just company inside the decision, and the decision left entirely hers.

There is no more counsel to be had than this. She has to choose.
