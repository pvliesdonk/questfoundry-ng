They go back up to the lamp room together, out of the wind, though the wind finds its way in regardless through gaps her grandmother never sealed and Maren never has either. Elias stands where he stood the night she told him, hands loose at his sides, the lamp turning its slow gold behind them both.

He does not ask her to choose. He says only that he will stay if she stays, and sail if she sails, and that whichever way the water is deciding to break, it will not be him doing the deciding. His voice is steady. His eyes are not, quite. It is the kindest thing anyone has offered her since the ledger came into her hands: not rescue, not argument, just company inside the decision, and the decision left entirely hers.

Before they go down he clears the table with his forearm and unrolls the chart he came here to make. It is finished. The shoals lie on the paper the way they lie under the water, every false depth corrected in his careful ink, and in the middle of it the hollow where the sleeper turns, drawn plain and unnamed. He weights its corners with sounding-leads and steps back, leaving it facing her. Whichever way the morning goes, the truth of the water is on paper now, and it is hers.

She has to choose. There is no more counsel to be had than this.
