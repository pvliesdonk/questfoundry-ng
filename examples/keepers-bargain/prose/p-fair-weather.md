Maren laughs before he can ask again, the sound coming easier than she expects. Bad instruments, she tells him, and worse weather — the shoals play tricks on every sounding line that's ever been dropped near the Stilt Light, ask any keeper before her. She says it lightly enough that he almost smiles, and while he is coiling his line to try again she folds the ledger's blank-lined page into the stove and watches it go, the ink curling black before the paper does.

It is a small thing, done and gone in a breath. It does not feel small.

Elias does not ask again, not directly, but something in him goes quiet after that, watchful in a way his questions never were. He keeps his soundings to himself now, writes in a hand she cannot read upside-down across the table, glances at the lamp room door sometimes with an attention that has nowhere honest to land. At meals he is polite, careful, a guest who has learned the house rules without being told them and resents the learning a little. The lie sits between them at every plate and cup, taking up a chair of its own, patient as the thing that turns over below the shoals when the tide goes wrong. Maren tells herself this is the kinder shape the truth could take. Some nights, watching him watch the water, she almost believes it. The days pass the way the weather does — one after another, indifferent, carrying her further from the moment she might have said something else.
