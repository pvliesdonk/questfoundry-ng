She waits for slack tide, when the water holds its breath between the tide's turning, and takes him down to the lamp room instead of giving him an answer on the ladder. The room is brass and salt-bloom, the lamp itself turning slow and patient in its gimbal, oil-fed flame steady behind glass her grandmother polished with the same rag Maren still uses. She tells him what the light holds. Not all of it — there is no telling all of it, no words wide enough — but enough. The bargain. The blank line. What sleeps under the shoals because the light does not.

Elias listens with his hands still, which she has not seen him do before. He asks no questions this time. When she finishes he looks past her, out through the salt-fogged glass toward the black water, and says nothing for the length of a swell.

He stays.

Not that night only — the chart cases go under the table and are not opened again. He learns the lamp's rhythm the way he might learn a coastline: by walking it, by standing in it at every hour until the pattern shows itself. He trims the wick when her hands ache from the cold. He learns which knots in the wood mean the wind is about to shift. Some nights he stands the watch beside her without a word between them for hours, chart-work abandoned, his manner gone steady and watchful, a man who has chosen a post he was never assigned. Maren finds she has stopped counting the days he has been ashore. She has started counting them a different way — as the sea outside pulls at the stilts, patient, unhurried, and something in it begins, very slowly, to stir.
