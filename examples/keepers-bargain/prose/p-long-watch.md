At dawn Maren sends the ship away. She stands on the gallery and watches it go, sails filling, hull small and then smaller against the grey, and does not let herself watch it out of sight — there is a wick to trim, and the trimming does not wait on grief. She goes up to the lamp room and does the work her hands already know: cuts the char, sets the flame, feeds it whale-oil by the cupful the way she has done every storm season of her life. The bargain holds. Downstairs, in the ledger, she takes up the pen and writes her name on the blank line her grandmother left her, the ink going dark and permanent in a hand not so different from the one before it.

The sea settles that same week, the way a dog settles by a fire once it has found the warmest board — a long exhale, a stillness that was not there before. The tremors do not come again. The shoals fill back up with birds. Along the coast, in the town she can see some clear mornings from the gallery rail, no one learns what was spared them, because that is the shape the price has always taken: paid in full, and paid in silence, by someone they will never think to thank.

Maren keeps the light. Years go by measured out in wick-trims and storm seasons and the particular ache of standing watches alone, and she finds she does not mind it the way she thought she would — there is a kind of peace in a duty this exact, this necessary. Some nights a chart arrives by the mail boat, corrected in a tidy, familiar hand, no letter with it, no name signed, nothing but the depths marked true where they once were wrong. She keeps those too, filed behind the ledger, and reads them on the worst nights like a kind of company. The coast sleeps because Maren does not, not fully, not ever again — but she tends the flame, and the flame holds, and that, she has come to understand, is the whole of what she owed and what she keeps.
