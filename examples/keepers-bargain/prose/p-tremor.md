The sea pulls back from the stilts before dawn, farther than Maren has ever seen it go, baring black rock and the ribs of a wreck no one has charted. The lamp gutters blue at the top of its wick, just for a moment, the flame bending sideways as though something has drawn a breath in the room with her. Below the shoals, unseen, the thing that has slept there through three keepers' lives turns over, and this time it does not settle back.

She knows the feeling in her chest for what it is. The bargain, coming due.

At low slack she takes the gallery glass to the bar, and the wreck gives up its name without her asking. Clinker-built, coast-rigged, the stem her father shaped the summer before he left — her mother's boat, lying where the sea put it, kept the way the ledger keeps its lines. So that is the truth of it, settled at last and colder than the not-knowing: her mother capped the lamp one night and ran for the coast, and the water took its toll at the bar and handed back the hull.

Maren goes down to the ledger and reads the blank line right for the first time. Not an invitation. A debt carried forward, one keeper's leaving already counted into the sum, and the ink waiting to see whether it gets a name or another wreck.

Elias's ship rides at anchor past the shoals, provisioned and ready, sails already loosed to dry in the grey morning air. He finds her on the gallery as the tide keeps falling, and there is no question in his face this time, only the offer itself, plain as a hand held out. The ship sails at dawn. He asks her to take the seat beside him.

Behind them the lamp burns low and blue, and the water goes on pulling back from the stilts, patient, waiting to see what she decides to leave behind.
