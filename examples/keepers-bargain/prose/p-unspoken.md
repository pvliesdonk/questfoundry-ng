Elias packs the way he does everything, precisely and while talking, cases buckled and instruments nested in their felt, and Maren stands in his doorway with the truth sitting behind her teeth where it has sat for days. He is going home believing the coast is only water. She could still tell him. The tide has not turned yet; there is time for the whole story between now and the dawn sailing, and she can feel the shape of every word of it.

She says none of them.

On the chart table he leaves her a parting gift, wrapped against the salt and tied with survey twine: a fair copy of his soundings, corrected the way she corrected them for him — bad instruments, worse weather. Her own lie, drawn careful and beautiful in a good man's ink, depths where she told him depths ought to be. He is proud of it. He shakes her hand.

She walks the gallery alone after, the copy under her arm, the ship's lanterns burning small and certain past the shoals. Whatever she decides at dawn, she will decide it the way she has decided everything since the page burned — alone, with the lie standing at her shoulder, keeping its counsel, keeping hers.

The offer stands. The tide is going out.
