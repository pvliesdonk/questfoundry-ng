At dawn Maren caps the lamp herself, her hand steady on the housing though nothing else in her is. The flame goes out under her palm with no more ceremony than a held breath released, and behind her, for the first time in three generations, the Stilt Light goes dark. She does not look back at it from the ladder. She has practiced not looking back at things, these last days, and finds the practice serves her now.

She steps aboard, and the ship takes her, and the water beneath the hull feels wider than any water she has stood over in her life.

The sea does not settle behind them. It does the opposite — a restlessness under the wake that is not the ship's doing, something that follows the way a storm follows heat, patient, drawing closer by matching their speed rather than closing it. The shoals empty of birds within the week; word comes later, from other boats, other coasts, of nets pulled up strange and empty, of depths that will not hold still on any chart. Maren does not ask after these things directly. She does not have to. She knows the shape of what she let loose better than anyone left ashore.

She is free. The oilskin coat goes into a trunk and stays there; her hands, in time, forget some of their old exactness with rope and wick. But she keeps no lamp now and still wakes at the hour of the trimming, every night, without a clock to tell her — sits up in whatever bed she is borrowing, the old rhythm moving in her like a tide that has nowhere left to pull against, and waits out the minutes until the feeling passes and she can lie back down. Free, she has learned, is not the same as unburdened. It is only a different weight, worn on the inside now, where no one else can see it and no ledger will ever record it paid.
