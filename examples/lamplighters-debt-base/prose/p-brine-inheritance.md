The oilskin coat is too big for her. It always was. Tamsin walks the canal ring in her mother's shape, sleeves rolled twice, hem dragging at her ankles like something reluctant to follow.

Dusk comes off the water grey and low. The town has gathered at the bridges the way they gather for a burial, in the quiet that is not respect so much as waiting. They watch her hands. Brine-chapped, steady enough. She holds the striker her mother held, the one worn smooth by two hundred years of thumbs that were not all Veil thumbs, and she thinks of that lineage the way she thinks of deep water. Best not to look down into it.

Lamp one. The wick catches and the flame comes up green, not the honest yellow of tallow but a sick sea-colour, the light of things that live where light does not go. It does not warm her. It leans, faintly, toward the black canal, as if listening the way she has been told to listen.

She says the words her mother said. She does not know if she believes them. Mercy, the creed calls this. A keeping. She lights the second lamp and the third, and the ring wakes behind her, a chain of green mouths breathing over still water, and the townspeople follow her along the stone with their eyes and none of them come close enough to touch her coat.

Junia walked this route until the night she did not come back. The logbook says nothing of that night because the logbook was sealed before it. Tamsin lights lamp after lamp and feels the oath settle into her like cold into iron, slow, permanent, a promise made to something that never told her its name.

The ring bends toward the far quay. There, past the last living flame, the black column stands unlit. Lamp Forty-Two. The corroded iron is green already without any fire in it, salt-furred, patient. She has been told not to light it. She has not been told why. As she nears, the striker heavy in her fist, she hears it. Under the lap of the tide, under her own held breath, a knock. Low. Deliberate. From inside the column, or under it, or under everything.

It knocks again. It has been knocking, she understands, for a long time before her. It is only that hers are the first ears sworn to hear it.

Her mother's map is folded in the coat against her ribs, wet-inked, half a thing. The town waits behind her for her to be finished, or to begin. The knock comes a third time and does not ask. It only says: here. Here. Here.

The tide-line lies below the lamp, black and receding. The stair inside the tower climbs into salt-dark. She stands between them with the green light on her wet-rope hair, and chooses which way to go down.
