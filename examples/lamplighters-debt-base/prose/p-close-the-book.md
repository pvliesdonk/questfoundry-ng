The pen is in her hand. She has taken it up. Her fingers close on the cold barrel the way her mother's fingers must have closed, at this seam of the ring, with the fog in her lungs and the knock coming even through the stone. One stroke. The line waits under her hand, wet and reaching, wanting only to be finished.

Tamsin sets the pen down.

She lays it flat across the swollen page, unused, its nib dry as the day it was cut. The last stroke stays where her mother left it, trailing off into water it never quite named. She does not follow it. She will not draw the thing to its end, will not give the wet line the hand it waits for.

Halvard makes a small sound, low in the throat, patient still.

"They paid us to be quiet," Tamsin says. The words come slow, each one set down like a stone in still water. "Two hundred years of quiet. I used to think that was the shame of it."

The knock comes up through the iron. She lets it.

"But quiet is a mercy too. It is a kind you can give." She thinks of her mother's hands on this leather, lifting the pen and setting it down and lifting it again, the choosing of it, night after night. Whatever her mother reached for at the end, whatever she wanted to know or wanted spared, the not-asking was hers to keep. Tamsin will keep it the same way. Not because she is forbidden. Because refusing is the last tenderness left to offer a woman who cannot be offered anything else.

She closes the book. The covers meet with the soft wet sound of a thing sealing over, algae and old leather folding the map back into the dark where it waited. She works the clasp the way Junia worked it, the same turn of the thumb, the same pressure, the salt of her own skin pressed into the salt in the grain until the two are one salt.

"You will not lay the hand," Halvard says. It is not a question. The milky eye gives back nothing.

"No."

The knock keeps its rhythm below her feet, slow, even, owed and unanswered. Let it knock. Some doors are kept shut as an act of love. She holds the sealed book against her ribs where the warmth was, and there is no warmth now, only weight, and the weight is bearable in a way the answer never would have been.

Still, her thumb finds the clasp again. Not to open the map. To open the years before it, the plain dry years where the ink has long since set and cannot listen. There is other counting in this book. She has felt it under her hands and never read it whole.
