The first wick is the hardest, because there is no rhythm yet in her hands, only the fear and the cold iron. She kneels. She draws the charred crook of the wick up out of the tallow and trims it clean, and the green tongue steadies, and she breathes. One. She has learned to breathe by the count.

The second comes easier. She feeds it what it asks and no more, watching the flame drink, watching it settle to that low steady green her mother taught her to read like weather. She does not hurry. Hurry is how a lamp guttters. Hurry is how a light drops out of the ring and out of the count, and the count is the only prayer she trusts.

Oren keeps her his silence and his coil. She is grateful for both. She does not tell him so.

Third. Fourth. Her knuckles crack when she folds them around the trimming iron, the salt gone white in the seams of her skin, her mother's coat heavy and stinking of oil at her shoulders. The labor is the same at each column and the same is what makes it hold. Trim. Feed. Watch it take. Move on. She thinks of two hundred years of women going down this ring in the dark, doing this exact small thing, and the thought does not frighten her the way it should. It steadies her the way the flame steadies. She is one of a long line of hands. The work outlasts the hand.

The black column hums up through her boots the whole way round. She feels it before the others, always, a low patience in the stone that keeps its own slow measure against her breathing. She lets it keep. She trims the lamp beside it and does not look and does not answer, and the not-looking is a kind of tenderness she has no other word for.

By the last of them her arms are stiff to the shoulder and the sky is going the grey that means the tide of morning. The flames stand green the whole ring round. Not one dark. She has fed them all against the pressing dark and the pressing dark has not won a single wick from her, not tonight.

She kneels at the last column longer than the work requires. The iron cools in her grip. Beneath her, through her, the knock keeps its measure, owed and unanswered and waiting, and she lets it wait, because the waiting is hers to keep now too. This is the keeping. Not the map, not the answer, not the mercy the creed would have her offer. Only this. The trimming. The count. The fire she will guard until it takes her the way it took her mother, or until she is old.

She counts them once more. The count comes out whole.
