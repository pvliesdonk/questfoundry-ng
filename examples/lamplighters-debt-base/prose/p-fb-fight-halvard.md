At the tower's foot Sister Halvard is waiting, or not waiting. It is hard to tell with her. She stands where the stone goes green at the base, one hand flat against it as though feeling for a pulse in the mortar, her milky eye turned up toward the lamps and her good one closed. The habit smells of lamp-oil even here, even under the salt. It always does. Tamsin has come to know that smell the way she knows the count, a thing that arrives before the person and stays after.

Halvard's lips are moving.

Tamsin does not slow, but she does not hurry past either. She hears the shape of it more than the words, a low tide of syllables worn smooth by two hundred years of other mouths. It is not a prayer she was taught. It is not a prayer that wants teaching. There is no name in it she could set down and no god she could point to, only the cadence, the going-under and coming-up of it, patient as the knock and pitched to the same slow measure. She understands, passing, that the two are answering each other. That they have always answered each other. That this is what the tending sounds like when it forgets there is anyone left to hear.

The old woman does not open her eyes. But her voice bends. It makes a small room in itself, a held breath, and Tamsin steps into it without deciding to, and for the length of one line the prayer carries them both.

Then it closes again and goes on without her.

Nothing is said. Nothing needs saying. Halvard knows what Tamsin carries down the ring in her cramped hands, and Tamsin knows what Halvard keeps here at the foot with her palm to the cold stone, and the knowing is enough, is more than words would be. They are not friends. They are not kin. They are two women who have each agreed, on separate nights and for reasons neither will confess, to stay and listen when it would be kinder to themselves to go.

Tamsin passes on into the grey. Behind her the murmur keeps its measure, threading itself through the hum that rises from the black column, and she does not look back to see whether the nun's lips have stopped or whether they ever truly will. She has her own keeping now. She lets the old woman have hers. The tide comes on without asking, and the two of them stand in its path like lamps.
