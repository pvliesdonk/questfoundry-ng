The door does not want to stay open. The wind off the ring leans against it, cold and full of salt, and Tamsin sets her shoulder to the wood and holds it while Oren finds his coat.

He finds it the way a man finds his hands in a dark room. His fingers pass over the wrong sleeve, then the right one, and he pulls it on with the wire still slung wrong across his back so the coil swings against his hip. She does not help him. She has already done the helping. The rest is his to carry down the step.

The cold comes through the gap between them and takes the tallow-light and thins it. She feels the boards under her feet. She feels the other thing under the boards, faint, patient, the slow knock coming up through the stone and the iron into the soles of her, felt before it could ever be heard. She does not tell him she feels it now. She lets him keep the little he can still carry.

He stops at the threshold with the wind pulling his coat open again. For a moment he looks at her, and there is a question in his mouth that he does not ask, and she is grateful. Locals never ask it. He learned that late, and he learned it from her, tonight.

"There's no shame in it," she says. It is a lie shaped like a mercy. "You were brought here to lay copper. You laid it. The rest was never yours."

"It's yours, then." Not a question either. He has caught the trick of it from her.

"It's the route's." She holds the door. Her arm aches with holding. "The route is mine."

He nods. He does not touch the frame on the way out, the way he touched it coming in. He has stopped touching the island's things with one finger. He has decided not to commit the whole hand, and the door is the last of it, and he steps past without laying so much as a knuckle to the wood.

The wind takes him. His boots find the step, then the road, and the salt closes over the sound of him the way water closes over anything the ring is done with. She stands in the open door and counts, without meaning to, the way she counts the lamps. One breath. Two. She lets him go on the third.

Below her the knock keeps its own count, older than hers, older than his copper. She holds the cold a moment longer than she needs to. Then she lets the door swing to the wind, and watches the dark take the last pale shape of him toward the mainland road.
