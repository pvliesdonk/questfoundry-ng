The house keeps his shape a moment after him. A cold spot near the door where his heat stood. The wire he left, coiled wrong, its cut end pointing at her like something asked and unanswered.

Tamsin stands with her hand still on the latch. Her mother's oilskin hangs too long on her, the way it always has, and she pulls it closer against the draft that finds the boards no matter how she caulks them. Through the window the road runs pale toward the mainland, and she watches for him without going to the glass. She does not need to see. She has learned the shape of a man leaving.

She told him true. That is the thing she will carry. She built the warning slow, word by word, watching the color go out of his face the way tallow-light goes out of a wick, and she chose each word for how it would land, and every word was true, and that is the cruelty of it. A lie would have been mercy. The truth was the knife she used because it was sharper.

His boots quicken somewhere out there, and the sound of them is already thinning, already becoming wind. He will make the causeway before the tide turns. He will not come back. No one will send another, not now, not with him carrying the story of the drowned woman and the lit lamp to the mainland where they will call it island foolishness and let the wiring stall, and the flames will not be snuffed, and she will keep the count alone as her mother kept it alone, and the greenish fire will burn on above the water that has been patient with her family for two hundred years.

She has spared him the knocking. She has spared him the ladder and the three days and the water giving him back. She tells herself this in her mother's reciting voice, the count-voice, the one that does not tremble, and it holds, mostly, the way the caulk holds, mostly.

Beneath the boards the slow thing settles. Not louder. Never louder. Only nearer to certain, the way a held breath is nearer to the exhale. It has what it wanted. It has her by herself in the dark with no one left to hear it but the one who cannot leave.

She lets go of the latch. Her palm has kept the shape of the iron, ridged and red. She looks at it a while. Then she takes up her matches, because the lamps do not light themselves, and the road is still out there, and she has not yet watched him gone.
