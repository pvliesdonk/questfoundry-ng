She turns her back on the knock. It is the only mercy she has to give, and she is not sure it is one.

The tower door is swollen with salt and gives grudgingly. Inside, the air is older than the air outside, still and cold and tasting of iron. The green light does not follow her in. She climbs into the dark by feel.

The first stair takes her weight and she finds it already waiting for her foot. There is a hollow worn into the stone, a smooth dish where the tread should be flat, and her boot settles into it as though the step had been cut to fit her. It was not cut for her. It was cut by feet that came before, and the feet before those, thumb-worn stone the way the striker is thumb-worn, generations pressing the same passage down into the rock a hair at a time.

She climbs. Each step gives her its groove. Her mother stood here. Her mother's mother, whom Tamsin never met, only heard named in the low voice the town keeps for the drowned. Junia went up this stair carrying the same striker, the same oath, the same not-knowing, and set her feet exactly here, in exactly these worn places, because there was nowhere else on the stone to set them.

The coat drags behind her. The hem catches on a lip of stone and she frees it without stopping. Her breath sounds too loud in the shaft. Below her, faint through the iron and the water and the whole weight of the tower, the knock keeps its patience.

She understands, climbing, that the stair is not hers. She is only its newest tread. It was here before the Veils and it will be here after, and every keeper who ever mounted it thought the same small thought she thinks now, that her feet were the first, that this once might be different. The grooves say otherwise. The grooves are the true logbook. Two hundred years of the same choice pressed into the same stone, and none of them ever wrote down why.

The map lies against her ribs, wet-inked, unfinished. At the top of the stair there is a room, and in the room there is a chest, and in the chest her mother's logbook waits sealed against the very thing Tamsin has been sworn to. She does not have to break it. She could climb back down. She could set her feet in the same grooves going the other way and let the seal keep its silence one more life.

But she is here, and her hand is already reaching, and the stone underfoot has been shaped to bring her exactly this far.
