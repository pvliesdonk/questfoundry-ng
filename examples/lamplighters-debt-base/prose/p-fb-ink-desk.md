She sets the pen down on the desk and watches the last line her mother drew.

The ink beads. She has seen ink dry a hundred ways, and this is none of them. It gathers along the stroke in small dark drops, tight and round, each one holding its own curve of lamplight the way a droplet of water holds the window. She reaches for the blotting-cloth and presses it flat against the page and counts her own breath, one, two, three, and lifts it. The cloth comes away clean. The line is as wet as before.

She presses again. Harder. She leans the weight of her arm into it, the whole cold length of her mother's oilskin sleeve, and holds until her elbow trembles, and when she takes the cloth away the drops have found each other and drawn themselves back into the perfect wet stroke, patient, unhurried, exactly as her mother left it.

The page will not let the matter rest.

She thinks of brine on a windowsill, how it dries to salt but stays somehow damp in the grain, how the sea leaves a little of itself behind in everything it touches and will not be wiped out. This is that. The ink is not ink. Or the ink is ink and something has entered it, the way the cold enters the glass, the way the knock enters the floor of her chest and stays.

She tips the logbook toward the flame. The heat should take it. The green light lies along the wet line and warms the leather and the drops do not shrink, do not skin over, do not so much as tremble. They hold their small dark faith. She thinks, absurdly, of a mouth kept a little open. Of a candle a mother leaves burning in a window for a child who is out on the water and has not yet come home.

She closes the book.

Under her ribs the pull is there again, the one without hurry, and she understands that the ink will bead like this tonight and the night after and long past her own dying if she lets it, that the line will keep its wet edge for whoever comes next, that this is not decay but appetite, held very still, held very kind.

She stands. The desk goes dark behind her. Beyond the window the ring waits in the fog, and the dark lamp waits at the far end of it, and Halvard waits under the dark lamp with her hands folded and her mercy ready. Tamsin takes up the light. The unblotted line goes with her, sealed inside the swollen leather, still saying the one wet word it has been saying for two hundred years.
