She looks up from the page.

The window holds the ring the way a puddle holds the sky, badly and whole. Each lamp has come loose from its iron in the fog. They bleed into one another, green into green, until the whole circle is a single smeared band of light laid around the dark like a ring around a finger that is no longer there. She cannot count them. She has never once, in all the nights of the route, failed to count them. Tonight the fog has taken the arithmetic and left only the shape, and the shape is the same shape as the ink, and she does not want it to be.

She lowers her eyes to the line.

It lies where her mother left it. Open. The last stroke reaches out and stops, and the black of it gleams, still wet, still patient, catching the small green breath of the flame at her back and giving nothing of it away. The window offers her the finished thing, the ring already closed in light and water. The page offers her the choosing of it. Between the two her thumb hovers, not touching, the brine-chap of it cracked white across the knuckle, her mother's coat heavy on her shoulders and smelling of tar and cold.

Beneath the tower the knock comes up through the stone. Slow. Even. It does not hurry her. It has not hurried anyone in two hundred years. It only keeps its count where she has lost hers, patient as the ink, patient as the water lying flat between the lamps, and she understands with a plain dread she cannot pray away that the knock and the wet line and the closing ring are one thing wearing three faces, and that all three are waiting on her hand.

She closes the logbook over the open stroke. She does not seal it. She only holds it shut against her ribs where the warmth of the wrong page presses through the oilskin like a small held fever, and she stands, and the flame gutters and steadies at her back, kind and useless, holding it off or holding it near.

The knock does not stop when she moves. It goes with her.

Out there, past the smeared ring, the black lamp waits with its green corrosion and its hollow throat, and Halvard waits beside it in the fog, and the water lies very still. Tamsin puts her hand to the tower door. The iron is cold and it hums, faintly, the way the column hums, the way the whole night hums now that she has stopped counting and started listening. She goes out to meet what her mother left half-said.
