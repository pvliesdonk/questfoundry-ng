She takes the logbook out from under her coat before she can decide to. It has ridden against her ribs all evening, swollen and cool, the leather gone soft as something drowned. She opens it there in the black column's shadow, where Oren's hard light spills across the pages and makes them shine like wet stone.

The old entries are her mother's hand, plain and dutiful. Lamp Four low, trimmed. Lamp Eleven fouled with weed, cleared. The dates march down the pages the way the ring marches around the water, faithful, unremarkable, the record of a woman doing a small thing well for as long as she was given. Tamsin turns past them. Her thumb leaves a cold smear where the algae has soaked through.

Then the writing changes.

It does not stop. That would be a mercy. It goes on, but the letters loosen, the way ice loosens at the edge of a thaw, and the words her mother set down begin to shiver into marks that are not words. Loops that fold back on themselves. Strokes that reach and reach and do not land. She tilts the page toward Oren's glow and the ink seems to move under the light, to slide a hair's breadth and settle, as if it has only just now finished drying, as if it is drying still.

The knock comes up through the iron at her back. Here. Here.

She knows this hand. She grew up watching it fill grocery lists and mend-notes and the tight small numbers of a life kept honest. This is the same hand, and it is trying to say something it no longer has the shape for. The marks lean all one way, toward the last page, toward the lower corner where the strokes gather and thin and become a line, a single trailing line that leaves the words behind entirely and starts, instead, to draw.

A curve. Then another beneath it, wider. The beginning of something vast turning under the canals, sketched in ink that will not dry because it was never finished, because the hand that drew it went into the water before the shape could close.

Tamsin's pulse is loud in her own ears. The oilskin is cold and the flame behind her holds its small green breath and Oren has gone quiet, watching her face rather than the page, and she understands that whatever her mother was reaching for is still here, still open, still waiting in the last wet stroke for a hand to carry it on.

She lowers the light to the corner where the line trails off.
