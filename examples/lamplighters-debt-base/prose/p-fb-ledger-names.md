So she reads the names.

They are written small, tucked into the margin beside each brown sum, a hand that shrank as it wrote them. Not Mowe. Mowe is the receiving. These are the others, the ones the coin passed through, the ones who carried it across the water or looked the other way while it went. Tamsin knows them. That is the cold of it. She knows every one.

Halvard, who blesses the flames each dusk and calls the ring a keeping of souls. Coom, who mends nets by the sluice gate and gave Tamsin bread the week her mother drowned. Sedge. Ferrow. The widow Brakes, who keeps the tallow shop and weighs Tamsin's oil each morning with her thumb heavy on the scale, and smiles, and calls her Junia's girl in a voice soft as rendered fat. Their names sit here in the ledger the way they sit in the lane, familiar, worn, unremarkable. Nodding names. Names that touch their caps to her when she passes with the ladder over her shoulder.

She had thought the silence was hers. Hers and her mother's and her grandmother's, a Veil weight, a thing carried down the route alone in the dark. It is not. It is the whole town's. Every name here is a mouth that stayed shut, and the mouths still open in daylight to greet her, still shape kind ordinary words over the black water, and never once the word that matters.

She sits very still. The pages smell of algae under her hands. Somewhere the map's last stroke stays wet, unset, patient. Below the floor the thing turns toward what it is owed, and above the floor, in the dry warm rooms and the tallow shop and the chapel with its green light, the town has agreed for two hundred years not to hear it turning. They have agreed together. Gently. Over bread. With their thumbs on the scale.

Tamsin thinks of tomorrow's route, the lane at dusk, the caps lifting. She will pass Coom at his nets. She will weigh her oil under the widow's smile. She will know now what she did not know an hour ago, and none of them will know that she knows, and this too is a kind of silence, already asked of her, already tallied.

The names do not accuse. They only wait, brown and small and terribly familiar, for her to decide what a knowing like this is for.

She closes the book on her thumb, keeping the place. There is someone in this town who ought to be told. She is only not sure yet whether it is one of them, or the last one who does not yet know.
