She looks at him a long moment, and the wanting in his face is a terrible thing to see. He has held this out to her like water to a parched mouth. Freedom. An ending. The line broken at last, and no one after her made to climb the ladder in the dark.

His eyes wait on her. Rheumy, ringed, wet with the sleeplessness of years. She sees the boy he must have been before the paper found him, and the old man he is now, worn thin over the same fear, and she understands what he is truly asking. Not about the lamps. Not about the money. He wants to know if she will forgive him. He has spent his whole life paying a family to keep a silence, and he has told himself all that while that the paying was mercy, and now at the end of it he needs her to say so. He needs to hear it from the mouth of the girl he has bought.

He does not say the word. He would not dare. But it is there in the slack of the chain his fingers have let go, in the lean of his body toward her, in the way his breath catches when she does not answer.

Tamsin holds the ledger against her chest. The oilskin coat is heavy on her shoulders, her mother's coat, salt-stiff at the collar where her mother's neck once wore it thin. She thinks of her mother taking his coin and lighting the lamps and never once asking why. Strength, he had called it. And then he had unmade the word himself.

She could speak it. It would cost her nothing to speak it. It would give him the thing he has wanted longer than she has been alive, and it would settle over both of them like a hand laid gentle on a fevered brow, and it would be a lie, and it would be a kindness, and she is no longer certain there is any difference between the two.

Below them, faint through the boards, the water keeps its slow work. She keeps her count. She keeps his secret, or she does not. The old man watches her mouth and waits to learn what he is.

The verdict is hers. It has been hers since she crossed the seams into the light. She lets the silence hold him a moment longer, because he has held her family in a silence like it for two hundred years, and it seems only just that he should feel the weight of one.

Then she decides.
