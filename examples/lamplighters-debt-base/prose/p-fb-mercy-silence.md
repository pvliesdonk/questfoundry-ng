The silence goes on. She lets it. She has learned that too, that a silence held long enough becomes a room of its own, and that the two of them are sitting in it now, waiting to see which of them will spend first.

Mowe does not spend. He has said his piece and given it to her and now he only breathes, that wet unsleeping breath, the watch-chain hanging like a thing already drowned. His eyes go somewhere past her shoulder. He is very old in this quiet. She can see the boy he was and the corpse he will be, both of them held for a moment in the same stooped frame, and neither of them able to look at the floor.

Because the floor is where the third of them sits.

It comes then, the knock. Far under the boards, under the wet foundations, under the black patient water that does its work whether anyone lights a lamp above it or not. One knuckle of sound. Then nothing. Then, after a length of time she does not measure because measuring it would mean admitting she is counting, another.

Mowe does not startle. He has heard it all his life. That is the cruelest part of him, that he flinches from the paper and not from this, that he has made his peace with the knocking by paying it never to be spoken of. The bribe was never for the town. The bribe was for his own mouth, to keep it shut, to keep the asking from being heard.

Tamsin does not move. She holds the ledger against her chest like something that might keep her warm and knows it cannot. The knock is not loud. It is only certain. It has the rhythm of a thing that has all the time there is and knows the exact shape of the wait, that has been waiting since before her mother, before Mowe's father, before the flames were ever greened and lit and named a mercy.

She listens to it against her will and finds it is not asking her to answer. It is only letting her know that it can hear her listening, and that the listening is the same as an answer, that it has always been the same, that her mother's silence and Mowe's silence and now her own were never refusals but a slow long yes given one held breath at a time.

The knock comes once more. She lets the silence take it. She lets the silence take all of it, the ledger and the count and the old man's throat offered up in the dark, and she does not spend, and neither does the water, and the quiet closes over the three of them like a lid set gently down.
