He climbs the last stairs backward, hauling the crate up by its rope handles, and he does not look at her until he is already talking. When he does look, he only grins wider and sets the crate down on the cold floor between them like an offering he is proud of.

"You must be one of the lamp folk," he says. "Good. You can tell me if I'm about to electrocute anything sacred."

Tamsin does not tell him anything. She watches his hands.

He unpacks the way a boy empties his pockets, quick and certain, everything laid out where he can see it. Coils of copper wire, bright and unweathered, catching his hard white light and throwing it back. Glass tubes nested in straw. Ceramic spools. A meter with a needle behind a little window. He sets each piece down on the stone in a row, spacing them, and the arrangement is careful the way a thing is careful without meaning to be. Relics on an altar he does not know he is building. The copper burns on his forearms shine in the wire-light, old lessons his skin has already learned.

"See, the trouble with your old green flames," he says, holding a tube up to the light, turning it, "is they're kind and they're useless. Pretty. Slow. My ring will put a steady light on every column by the vote, and nothing to tend, and nothing to fear in the dark. Progress." He says the word plainly, like a man naming a tool. He believes it the way she once believed the tide came in twice and went out twice and asked nothing of anyone.

Below the copper and the glass, through the iron, the knock keeps her mother's hours. Here. Here. Here. He is kneeling right above it. His confidence is so complete it is almost a kind of innocence, and Tamsin feels the shape of it and cannot bring herself to break it. Not yet. He lays down the last spool and squares the row with his thumb.

"Charming, all of it," he says again, softer, to the instruments and not to her, and the dark takes the word and keeps it.

She holds the closed book against the oilskin. The wet ink is still cold under her thumb. He does not hear the thing that keeps time under his knees, and she is the only one in the tower who does, and that makes her, for this one held breath, the keeper of whether he ever learns to.

Salt sits on the stone between the copper pieces. She should walk the ring before the vote. She should see it once more the way it is.
