She does not go down at once. She waits on the step and lets his light climb toward her, white and unbraided, throwing shadows the wrong way. It makes the tower look like a place she has never worked. It makes the green stains on the iron look like something spilled.

By the time she comes out into the lower gallery he has an audience. Word of an outsider travels the harbor faster than any tide. Three of them stand at the tower mouth in the last of the daylight, Mrs. Odell and the Carrow brothers, come with nets they are not mending, watching Oren Quist unspool his wire across two hundred years of soot.

Tamsin knows the way they stand. She reads the room before she is in it, the old habit, her mother's habit. She keeps to the dark of the doorway and listens.

They do not lower their voices much. That is the cruelty of it, that they do not think it worth the trouble.

That coat, Mrs. Odell says. Wrong cut for weather. Mainland weight. He will be soaked to the bone by the second bell and think the sea did it to spite him.

Hear how he says the lamp numbers, one of the Carrows murmurs. Forty-Two. Says it flat. No water in the word. They lengthen it here, let the vowel sit like standing brine. He clips it. He says it like a man reading a list of things that belong to him.

Oren goes on working, cheerful, unhearing, calling up some small kindness about the soldering. He does not know he is being catalogued. He does not know that a coat and a way of saying a number are the whole of the verdict, that here you are either born to the listening or you are weather passing through.

Tamsin looks at his easy back and the copper burns on his forearms and the white lamp swinging where the green flame should stand, and she understands that they are right and that being right is a small mean thing. He is not one of them. He never will be. He can wire every lamp on the ring and still not hear the knock she carries in her teeth.

And beneath all of them, patient, keeping her mother's hours, the black lamp goes on marking time, indifferent to which vowels a man uses when it comes for him.

She steps out of the doorway. The townsfolk quiet. Oren turns, grinning, glad of any face.

The daylight is nearly gone. Before the vote, before the flames are snuffed, she means to walk the ring one more dusk and learn which lamps still burn green.
