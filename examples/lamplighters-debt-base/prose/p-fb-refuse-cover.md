The door does not want her yet. She knows why. There is one thing more, and it is not a kindness.

The cloths are where they have always been, folded on the low iron shelf inside the tower mouth, gone stiff and grey with the years of salt that have worked into them. Her mother folded them. The creases are her mother's creases. Tamsin lifts them and they crack a little at the fold, the way old sailcloth cracks, and the smell that comes up out of them is tallow and brine and the faint green reek of the flame they were made to tend.

The lamp burns behind its glass beside the door, the last of the ring, the one nearest the tower. Its light is the old light, that low drowned green that has stood over the town's sleep for two hundred years and called it safe. It is meant to burn. It is meant to be fed. It is meant to be seen from the water and from whatever waits in the water, a small kept promise that the vigil holds and the mercy will come.

She drags the first cloth across the glass.

The green goes soft. It goes to a smear, then to a stain, then to the color of a thumb pressed too long on a bruise. She lays the second over it and the third, and with each the light gives more of itself up, until what is left is a dull ache of a glow that no eye on the black water could find, that no patient thing could take for a promise kept.

Her hands do the work. Her hands know it. She has trimmed and fed this glass a thousand nights and now she smothers it with the same care she gave the feeding, and the care is the worst of it, because it is real. She is gentle with the cloths. She is gentle the way you are gentle laying a sheet over a face. This is the tenderness the creed taught her and she is turning it inside out and it fits her hands just the same.

The light dies down to that bruise and holds there, refusing to go all the way out. She lets it. Out is not what she is choosing. She is choosing only that it not be seen, that the mercy the creed demanded of every keeper before her hangs unlit and unoffered above the still water for the first time in two hundred years.

Behind her the knock keeps on, owed, patient, unanswered. She does not look back at it. She stands with her stiff grey hands full of her mother's folds and the glow going dark under them, and she leaves the mercy smothered, and she does not know what a thing does when the light it waited for stops coming.
