The door is plain. She had not noticed how plain. Grey wood swollen with a hundred wet winters, a latch of old iron gone the same corroded green as the column at her back, a threshold worn into a shallow bowl by boots that came before hers. Her mother's boots. Her mother's mother's. Tamsin fixes her eyes on it and holds them there.

The knock comes up through the stone and into the soles of her feet, and she lets it. She does not fight the feel of it. Fighting is a kind of listening too. She only keeps her gaze on the latch and counts, not lamps now, not the ring, but the plain steps between her body and the door. Four. Perhaps five. A count she chose herself. The first count that was ever hers.

Behind her the water holds its stillness. She can feel the pull of it the way one feels a held breath in a room, the wanting of the surface to be looked at once more, the small reasonable voice that says a keeper always checks the water before she goes, that says her mother would have checked, that says there is no harm in a single glance. She knows that voice. She has trimmed by its light her whole life. It is the sweetest bar in the cage.

She does not turn her head.

The trimming iron is warm now where her fist has held it. She loosens her grip and lets the blood come back into her fingers. Her mother's oilskin hangs heavy on her, salt-stiff at the collar, cut for a taller woman, and she wears it out the door the way she would wear it anywhere, into the ordinary dark of a town that will sleep tonight not knowing what was refused on its behalf.

The latch is cold. She sets her palm to it. The hum that lives in all the old iron of this place lives here too, faint, patient, threaded up from the black column through floor and wall and into her skin. She feels it under her hand and she does not lift her hand away.

Whatever waits below has waited two hundred years to be answered. Let it wait unanswered. Let the debt stand. She lifts the latch. The door gives with a low wet complaint, and beyond it the night air comes in clean and full, tasting of rain instead of the well, and she steps up out of the worn bowl of the threshold and does not look back at the still black water where the knock goes on keeping its measure for no one at all.
