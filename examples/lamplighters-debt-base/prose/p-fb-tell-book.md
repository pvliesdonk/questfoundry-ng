The ledger lies open across his knees and neither of them moves to close it.

Tamsin watches the wet line at the end of her mother's map hold the lamplight the way a low tide holds the last of the sky. It has not dried. It will not. She has known this the length of a season and said it to no one, and now it is said without her saying it, laid bare across the knees of a mainland boy with copper burns on his arms and no birthright in this silence at all.

He keeps his one finger where it touched the ink. That is his way. He greets a thing he does not understand with a single fingertip first, testing whether it will bite, before he lets his whole hand commit. She has seen him do it to the junction boxes and to the greenish flames and to the cold iron rungs of the ladder down. Now he does it to the last word her mother ever wrote and could not finish.

Below the boards the knock keeps its measure. Slow. Patient. Owed something she has never let herself name aloud. It comes up through the stone and the iron and into the soles of her boots, and she has counted it the way she counts the lamps of the ring, every night, without fail, because to lose the count is worse than the dark.

He lifts his eyes from the page. He does not ask her to take it back. He does not ask for the ladder or the door or the last boat to the mainland before the tide turns. He looks at her the way a man looks at a thing he has decided to carry, and the deciding is done, and it is in his face plain as the burns on his arms.

She reaches across the open ledger. She does not do it gently. She takes his hand off the wet ink and folds it into her own brine-chapped fingers, and his palm is warm and hers is cold, and the ledger breathes its algae between them, and the knock comes up through the floor into their two joined hands as if it too has been listening for this, for the moment there would be two, for the count to change and hold.

His grip tightens. He is staying. She feels it settle in him the way the swallowed word settled in her, down and down, past sending back, past kindness, past the clean mercy she had half-wanted to grant him. She holds his hand and does not tell him it will be all right, because that is a comfort-word for people who do not stand over the water, and she will not puncture the hush with it.

She holds on. The tide keeps knocking. They wait together now, and the waiting has two heartbeats in it where before it had one.
