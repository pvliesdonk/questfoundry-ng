She takes him out to the ring.

The night is low and salt-heavy over the canal, the water black as the iron that rings it, and the old lamps burn their greenish flames down the curve of the wall, one after another, the count her mother taught her running under her breath the way it always does. Thirty-nine. Forty. Forty-one. She does not say the last number aloud.

Lamp Forty-Two stands unlit where it has always stood. Black column gone green with corrosion, salt crusted into every seam, a shape that gives back no light and asks for none. Oren follows her across the wet stone with the coil still on his shoulder, though there is nothing here to wire, nothing here that will ever take a wire. She stops him a pace short of it. She does not touch the column herself. She has learned better than that.

She lays his hand against the iron instead.

He flinches at the cold and then goes still. She watches his face by the green light of the ring, watches the practical set of it come apart, because he can feel it now, the faint hum that lives in the metal, and under the hum the knock. Slow. Patient. Coming up from somewhere below the waterline into the column and out through the iron into his palm, a measure kept for two hundred years without hurry and without pause, waiting for a thing it has never once been told it will not receive.

She says nothing. There is nothing to add. She brought him to the ledger and to the wet ink and to the paid mouths, and now she has brought him here, and the lamp is doing what no page could do, what her steady voice could not do. It is telling him itself.

His single finger presses flat, then his whole hand, the way she has seen him take hold of anything he does not understand, giving himself over to it by degrees. The knock comes again through the meeting of skin and iron. She feels the moment it reaches the deep part of him and settles there, down where the swallowed word settled in her, where it will keep him company for the rest of whatever he is going to have.

She reaches for his other hand in the dark. His fingers are cold from the column and hers are cold from the coat that was her mother's, and they close on each other anyway, the two of them standing over the thing that knocks, sharing the weight of the hearing. The flames burn on down the ring. The count goes unfinished. She does not let go.
