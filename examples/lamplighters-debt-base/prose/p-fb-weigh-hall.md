The hall has been emptied for the vote. The benches are pushed back against the walls, and the long table stands bare but for a water-ring left drying where someone set down a cup and forgot it. The green light does not reach in here. Only the grey of afternoon through the high windows, thin as skimmed milk, laid flat across the boards.

Tamsin steps onto the floor and the floor answers.

Her boots are her mother's boots, too wide, and they come down hard in the hush, one and then the other, and the sound goes out ahead of her and comes back off the far wall a half-beat late, so that she seems to walk in company with someone slower. She does not slow to match it. She counts the steps instead, the way she counts the lamps, because a thing counted is a thing held, and she needs to hold something now.

Eleven boards to the table. Twelve.

The book is warm where her thumb has kept the place. She feels the ledger's weight and the ladder's weight at once, the same weight, the weight of a thing that must be carried because it has been handed down and cannot be set on the ground without waking what sleeps under the ground.

Mowe stands at the table's end. He is a tall man gone stooped, folded over his own height like a reed the tide has bent, and he does not look up at first. He looks at the watch-chain running between his two fingers, back and forth, silver catching the milk-grey light. Back. And forth. He has worried it so long the links have gone smooth. She wonders if he knows he does it. She thinks he does not. She thinks it is the one honest sound the man makes, that small metal telling.

The cold of the room sits on her skin like a wet cloth. Somewhere far below the boards the canals go about their patient work, and she will not think of them, and thinking of not thinking of them is the same as listening.

She stops an arm's length from him. Close enough to see the pulse in his throat, the loose skin of an old man who has kept a small brown promise every year of his life and called it kindness. He receives. She has learned the word for what he is today.

He lifts his head. The chain stills between his fingers.

"You've been to the counting-house," he says, gently, as he says everything. "I thought you might."
