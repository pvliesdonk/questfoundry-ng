The hall stretches long before her, floored in old iron plate that keeps the cold of the canal in it all year. Her boots find the seams. She counts them without meaning to, the way she counts the lamps, and the counting steadies her the way it always does, and she lets it carry her toward the far bench where the light is thin.

Mowe has not seen her yet. He sits bent over the ledger, and she thought she knew what she would find there. She thought she would find the man who receives. The tall shape that touches its cap to no one, the silver chain worried smooth between two fingers, the gentle voice that has paid her family to keep its mouth shut for two hundred years. She has carried that shape up the lane in her head, heavy as the ladder.

But it is his hands she sees first.

They are not still. They rest on the brown book and they will not settle, a small tremor running through the knuckles into the paper, so the page trembles under them like water under a lifted oar. He is not reading. He is holding on. The chain hangs slack from his waistcoat and he has forgotten to worry it, and that is the thing she notices, that the habit has left him. His thumb presses the ledger flat as if the numbers might rise off it and speak.

She stops where the light does not quite reach her.

All her life she has weighed him wrong. She has taken his silence for a strong thing, a closed door with a man behind it who knew what he was shutting out. Now she sees the door is only a man, old and stooped and afraid of the paper in his own hands, a man who calls his fear mercy because he cannot bear the other word for it. The debt does not hold him up. It holds him the way her mother's coat holds her, too large, worn because there is nothing else.

He is not the power in this room. He never was. Whatever leans on the town, whatever listens under the floor, it does not need him strong. It only needs him quiet, and quiet is what fear buys cheapest.

The knowing sits cold in her. She had come here to stand before a wall. She finds instead a hand that shakes, and she understands that a shaking hand will give her anything she asks, and that this is worse.

She lets her breath go slow. The iron holds its cold. She crosses the last of the seams into the thin light, and Mowe lifts his face.
