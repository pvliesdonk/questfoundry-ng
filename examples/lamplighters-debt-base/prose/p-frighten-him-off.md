She opens the door before he can knock again, and the cold comes in with him, and his easy grin is already there, thinning the way it thins these nights, as if the salt in the air were slowly working it loose.

He has the wire coiled over his shoulder. He touches the frame of the door with one finger before he steps in, the way he touches all the things this island hands him. She marks it. She marks everything now.

"Late," he says. "I know. I wanted the run to forty-two done before the vote. If the council's snuffing the flames by morning, I'd rather it be my copper carrying the light."

She does not tell him the flames are older than his copper. She does not tell him what the copper will be laid against. Instead she lets the quiet sit until he feels the weight of it settle on the boards between them.

"You've been hearing it," she says. Not a question. She keeps her voice level, a lamplighter's voice, the voice her mother used for reciting the count. "The knock. In the column. You put your hand flat on lamp forty-two and it comes up through your palm before you hear it."

His grin goes. The copper burns on his forearms stand out pale in the tallow-light. "You feel it too, then. I thought it was the pumps. I told myself it was the pumps."

"It isn't the pumps."

She watches him weigh that. She could stop here. She does not. She feeds him the next thing, slow, the way you pay out rope over a cleat.

"My mother wired nothing and she heard it every night for eleven years. Then one night she went down the ladder to answer it, and the water gave her back three days later, past the mouth of the ring, her lamp still lit in her hand. That is the route you're finishing, Oren. That is what the copper is for."

The wire slides off his shoulder. He does not touch it with one finger. He does not touch it at all.

"You should have said." His voice is small. "Somebody should have said."

"I'm saying now."

He is already moving, already gathering the coil back up by the wrong end, already at the door with the salt wind pulling at his coat. She holds it open for him. It is the kindest thing she has done in years, and it feels like a hand pressed over a mouth. He goes down the step into the dark and does not look back, and she listens to his boots quicken toward the mainland road until there is only the wind and the boards and the slow patient thing beneath them, waiting for her to be exactly this alone.

She closes the door. She is.
