The dark lamp stands where it always stands, at the far seam of the ring, its iron gone green as old bruises. Halvard is already there. The fog has made her a shape before it makes her a woman, ash-grey, hands folded, the milky eye catching Tamsin's light and giving nothing back. The smell of lamp-oil comes off her like breath.

"You've counted wrong tonight," Halvard says. She does not say it unkindly. "That's how it begins. The counting stops. Then the listening starts."

Tamsin holds the logbook against her ribs. The wet page ferments its small warmth through the oilskin.

"We were taught it was a defense." Her voice comes out thin. "The flames. Kept lit so the thing below stays down."

Halvard's mouth moves toward something that is not a smile. "That is the child's version. The one they give you so you'll light the lamps and ask no more." She lays her palm against the column, flat, the way one lays a hand on a sleeping brow. "The flames are not a wall. They are a candle in a window. We keep them for the drowned thing the way you keep a lamp for a child out late on the water. It is not our enemy, Tamsin. It is owed."

Beneath them the knock comes up through the stone. Slow. Even. Halvard closes her eye at the sound of it, the way another woman might close her eyes at a familiar step on the stair.

"It has knocked two hundred years," she says. "Patient as your mother's ink. It does not want the ring broken. It wants to be answered. That is all it has ever wanted. A hand laid where mine is laid. A mercy given back."

The knock finds its rhythm and the rhythm is the map's rhythm, the same slow reaching stroke that trails off wet under the leather at Tamsin's ribs, and she feels it fit itself against the shape of the thing her mother left half-said, and the fit is exact, and it is unbearable.

Her mother had held this book to her chest at this same lamp. Her mother had lifted the pen and set it down and lifted it again. The question is here in the iron, in the wet line, in the fog that took the count. To draw the last stroke is to know what circles below. And to know it is a thing that cannot be given back, cannot be blotted, cannot be dried out of the grain.

Halvard steps aside from the column and folds her hands and waits, ready, tender.

"You have the pen," she says. "You have always had the pen."
