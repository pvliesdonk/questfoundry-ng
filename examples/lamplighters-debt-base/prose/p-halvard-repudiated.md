Sister Halvard is waiting for her on the tower step, though Tamsin never heard her come. She stands in her ash-grey habit with her hands folded and her milky eye turned up toward the smothered glass, and she has been weeping a long time. The tears have made channels in the salt-dust on her cheeks. She does not wipe them. She looks at the dull bruise of light dying under the folded cloths and she looks at Tamsin's hands, still full of her mother's creases, and something in her serene face comes apart very slowly, the way old ice gives on a canal.

"You have not saved them," the keeper says. Her voice is soft, and this is the cruelty of it, that it stays soft. "You think you have shut a door. You have only stopped knocking on your side of it."

Tamsin says nothing. She has spent her whole life reading rooms before she speaks, and there is nothing in this one to answer.

"It was never a wall, child. It was a hand held out. Two hundred years we kept our hand out so it would not have to reach. That was the mercy. That was all the flame ever was." The old woman's mouth works. "And you have taken your hand back and told yourself you freed something."

The knock comes up through the stone into the soles of them both. Tamsin feels it climb her ankles. She watches Halvard feel it too, watches the keeper's folded hands loosen and the certainty of a lifetime go out of her face like the green going out of the glass.

"It waited because we asked it to wait," Halvard whispers. "Now no one is asking."

The knock changes.

It is not louder. It is only different, the patient measure gone slack, the way a rope goes slack when the thing it held has stopped pulling against it and begun instead to consider what it might do. The rhythm that carried the town's sleep for two hundred years falters, feels for a new shape, and does not find the old one again. The hum in the iron thickens under Tamsin's palm where it still rests on the cold latch.

Sister Halvard sinks down onto the worn bowl of the threshold and grieves for the whole dark ring, every unlit lamp of it, the count that no keeper will ever finish now. Tamsin stands over her in her mother's too-large coat and does not comfort her, because there is no comfort that would not also be a lie.

Behind the smothered glass the last light lets go at last. The ring stands unguarded. Below the still black water the unanswered thing turns its long attention over in the dark, unhurried, unowed, deciding for the first time in two hundred years what it will choose to want. No ritual leans against the door. No hand is held out. The town sleeps, and does not know it sleeps alone.
