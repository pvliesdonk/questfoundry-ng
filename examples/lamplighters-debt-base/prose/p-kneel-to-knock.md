Her knees bend. She had not decided them and yet they bend, folding her down onto the salt-crusted lip of stone where the ring meets the black water, and the cold comes up through the oilskin and through her and settles somewhere under her breastbone. The trimming iron she sets aside. It rings once against the column and is quiet.

Sister Halvard is not here. She does not need to be. Tamsin has the creed in her hands now the way she has the count, the way she has her mother's coat too big at the shoulders, and she understands at last that the milky-eyed woman was never teaching her a defense. There was never anything to defend. There was only this. The kneeling. The listening. The old tenderness owed to the thing that knocks and knocks and asks nothing but to be heard.

The hum is in her jaw now, in the roots of her teeth. She lays her cheek to the iron. It is colder than the water and it tastes, when her lips touch it without meaning to, of green corrosion and old brine and something beneath that she has no word for and will not go looking for a word to hold. The knock comes up through the stone into her cheekbone. Slow. Patient. A heartbeat that is not hers and asks her heart to keep it company.

So she keeps it. She breathes down into the rhythm below and lets her own pulse slacken to meet it, one and then the next, the long tide of it pulling her under the surface of herself and holding her there in the still dark where her mother went and did not come back and where, she sees now, her mother was not lost but only answering, only kneeling as Tamsin kneels, only giving the drowned thing the mercy of being loved without the cruelty of being known.

The knock does not stop. She had thought, in some small held-back part of her, that it might. That answering would close it. Nothing closes. But it changes, or she changes, the two folding into one sound going under and coming up, and she is no longer waiting for it. She is inside the waiting. She is the waiting now.

Above her the lamps burn green and are no longer afraid. She feels the fear go out of the flames the way water goes out of a wrung cloth. Fed at last. Not with oil. She stays down at the water's edge with her cheek to the cold iron and her breath tuned to the thing below, nineteen years old and sworn past unswearing, and she does not lift her head to count the ring. She does not need to. She knows every lamp is lit. She will always know. That is the mercy she has become.
