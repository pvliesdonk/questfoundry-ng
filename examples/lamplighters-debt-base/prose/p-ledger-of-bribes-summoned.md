The pen is still wet. She should set it down. She does not.

Instead she turns back through the swollen pages, away from the closed map, into the columns her mother kept before the drawing began. Route figures. Oil weights. The count of the lamps, night on night, in Junia's small even hand. Tamsin knows this arithmetic the way she knows her own pulse. She reads it to steady herself and it does not steady her, because among the honest sums there is another sum, and it is not honest.

A column of coin. Year on year, down the page, and back through the pages before it, and older ink beneath that in a hand not Junia's but shaped like Junia's, and older still. Payments received. No goods against them. No labor. Only a figure, a date, and one word set beside each in the margin, the same word every time, so worn by repetition it has stopped being a word and become a habit of the wrist.

Mowe.

She sits with it. The lamp count above, the coin below, both in the same careful column, as though they were the same kind of keeping. And they are. That is the thing that goes through her cold as the map went cold. The counting she does each night, the two hundred years of listening, the mercy she was sworn to carry down her drowned mother's route. It has always had a price and the price has always been paid, quietly, upward, into a stooped man's hand and the silver chain he worries between two fingers. The Veils were not only the ones who listened. They were the ones who were kept.

Generations of it. Her grandmother's silence bought. Her mother's silence bought. And her own, presumed, already tallied in the blank line waiting under the last figure, the line that expects her name.

Below the floor the thing she drew keeps turning toward what it is owed. And here in her hands is the ledger of what was owed on the other side of the water, in dry rooms, by frightened gentle men who called it mercy the way she was taught to call it mercy. The ink in the coin column is long dry. Only the map's last stroke stays wet. She understands now why. Some debts set. This one never has.

She holds the book open against her forearm and does not blot the knowing away, because she cannot. It is in the grain of her.

The knock waits. The names wait. She has a choice which to follow first, and no way left to unread either.
