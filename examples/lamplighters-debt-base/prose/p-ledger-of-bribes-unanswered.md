The map lies half-drawn where she left it, the last stroke trailing off into water it never quite named. Tamsin does not follow it. She turns back through the swollen pages instead, backward toward the plain years, where the ink has long since set and cannot listen.

The logbook falls open near its middle, and there her mother's hand keeps a different kind of watch. Not lamps counted. Coin counted. A column of it, ruled neat down the algae-soft page, each entry a date and a sum and a single word beside it. *Received.* The same word, over and over, in ink gone brown as old kelp.

Tamsin reads the topmost sum. Then the one below. Then the year, and it is not her mother's year, nor her grandmother's. The dates run back and back, one clean line at a time, past the reach of any hand that ever held this pen. Two hundred years of *received*. The payments arrive on no feast day she knows, no rent-quarter, nothing the tide would recognize. They simply come, generation laid on generation like silt.

And the name. She finds it in the margin, worried thin by the same fingers each time, as though the writer had touched it and thought better of writing it whole. *Mowe.* Eldric Mowe, and his father before the letter of his father, back to a Mowe who must have been young when the flames were first lit green. She thinks of his silver watch-chain, the two fingers turning it, the gentle voice that calls the snuffing of the lamps a kindness. He has been paying her family to keep still since before the word for still was learned.

Still about what. That is the ledger's cruelty. It records the price and never the thing purchased. Column upon column of silence bought, and not one line to say what the silence was wrapped around. Only that it was worth this. Worth two centuries of quiet coin passed hand to hand across the black water, worth the keeping when everything else was let rot.

Her thumb rests on the last dry entry. Below it the pages go wet, go toward the map, go toward the question she has already refused once tonight. The salt on her own skin answers the salt in the leather. Somewhere under the canals the count of lamps waits to be resumed, and she has not resumed it.

She could trace the columns down, year on year, and learn only how much. Or she could read the names in the margins, and learn who was afraid.
