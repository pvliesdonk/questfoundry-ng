She lowers the light to the corner where the line trails off, and holds it there.

The curve wants to go somewhere. She can feel it the way she felt the knock, a pull without hurry. One arc bends and a second answers it, wider, and if she lets her eye follow the way the ink means to be followed, the two curves are not two curves at all. They are the near edge of one great turning. The strokes her mother left are only the part that surfaced. Below them, unwritten, the shape goes on. It closes. It would close into something the size of the whole ring of lamps, the whole black circle of the canals, the town laid over it like a lid over a barrel that is not empty.

She takes her thumb from the page. She does not want to be touching it when she understands.

Nights pass and she comes back to it. This is the thing she cannot make sit still in her: the ink does not set. She has kept the logbook dry against her ribs, held it near the green flame, laid it open in the cold air, and the last line stays wet. Not smeared. Not spreading. Wet the way the glass is warm, wrongly, faithfully, a fever held under the skin of the paper. She touches it on the fourth night and her fingertip comes away dark and she thinks, it is still drying, and then she thinks the truer thing beneath it.

It is not drying. It is waiting.

The map is not a thing that died half-made. Her mother did not fail to finish it. Her mother stopped, or was stopped, at the exact stroke where the hand must change, and left the line open the way a door is left open, the way a place is set at a table. The wet ink is not the wound. It is the invitation. Her mother meant the pen to be picked up. She meant it for a hand that knew the warmth and the knock and could bear to keep drawing anyway, into the water, toward the shape that leans against the wall of the world and asks to be heard through it.

Tamsin holds the logbook and does not draw. Not yet. The green flame keeps its small breath at her back, kind and useless, holding the thing back or holding it close.

Beyond the window the ring blurs. The lamps run together in the fog until they are one long smear of green, and the black water between them lies very still, and somewhere under it the shape her mother started keeps its patience, waiting for the line to close.
