"I have," Tamsin says. Nothing else. She has learned that from him, how a small sentence can be a closed hand.

He nods as though she has confirmed a weather. His eyes come up to hers, rheumy, pale, ringed with the wet redness of a man who does not sleep. She looks into them and finds no wall there, no door, only the old fear looking back, and behind it something older than his fear, the thing his fear has always been about. He is afraid of the paper. He is more afraid of what the paper keeps quiet.

"Your mother never asked me why," he says. "Not once in all the years. She took the money and she lit the lamps and she kept the count. I used to think that was strength." The chain hangs slack. He does not reach for it. "It was only that she knew. Same as I know. There are things you do not ask because the asking is heard."

He leans closer, and his voice drops to the softest thing in the room, softer than the metal telling that has left him. "Let them die, Tamsin. The old flames. Let the new light come and end it. No more counting. No more climbing that ladder in the dark to feed a mercy nobody can see the shape of. You are nineteen. You have carried a debt that was never yours, and I have paid one that was never mine, and if the lamps go out by morning we are both of us free of it. That is all I want. To hand you nothing. To be the one who broke the line."

He believes it. That is the trap in it. He offers her freedom the way a man offers his throat to whatever will take the least from him, and calls it kindness because kindness costs less than courage.

Tamsin thinks of the water going about its patient work below the boards. She thinks of Halvard's serene milky eye, her certainty that the flames are a tenderness owed, a warmth kept burning for a thing that only wants to be kept company. She thinks of the count that steadies her, that she has never once let fall.

Two tendernesses, and she cannot tell which of them is the cage. Mowe would snuff the ring to end his dread and call it setting her loose, and Halvard would keep it lit forever and call the same chain love, and between the two of them the thing beneath the canal keeps its slow, patient rhythm and does not care which mercy she chooses, only that she is listening, and she is, she cannot stop, she has been listening since she crossed the seams into the light.

She holds the ledger. She holds the count. She does not answer him yet.
