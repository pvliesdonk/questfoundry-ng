Morning comes and does not change anything. This is the thing no one tells the keeper. The light shifts from grey to a colder grey, the tide stands high against the ring, and the lamps burn on greenish in the failing dark as though the sun were only weather passing over, not the end of the vigil. There is no end of the vigil. She understands that now the way she understands her own count. The debt was never hers to close. It was only hers to carry a little further down the ring than her mother carried it, and then to hand on, cramped and salt-white, to whatever hand comes after.

Mowe finds her at the tower foot. She hears the watch-chain first, the small worrying of silver between two fingers, before she hears him. He has come to tell her the vote has failed. The mainland man will not lay his wire without his own man to lay it, and that man has not come, and the council will not carry the cost alone. He says it gently. He mistakes gentleness for the thing he owes her. The lamps will stay, he says. The old arrangement will stay. He does not say the word arrangement as though it cost him anything, and she watches him not-say it and knows he has been not-saying it her whole life and her mother's whole life before that.

She could open her hand and let the town see what has been paid into it across two hundred years. She feels the shape of the choice sitting under her tongue. She swallows it. Not for him. For the count. A town that knew would want the flames snuffed and the knock answered and the matter closed, and there is no closing, only the trimming and the feeding and the low green light held against the dark. Let them keep their sleep. Let them keep their lamps lit and never ask by what those lamps are lit, or against what.

Mowe leaves relieved, the way a man leaves who has mistaken another's silence for absolution. She lets him have it. It is the cheapest thing she owns.

That night the ring burns green and afraid. She feels the fear in the flames the way her mother taught her to read weather in them, the greenish light guttering low and frightened at each column, drinking what she feeds it and wanting more, wanting the one thing she has not decided whether to give. The black lamp hums up through her boots. The knock keeps its patient measure, owed and unanswered, folding itself into the murmur at the tower foot until the two are one sound going under and coming up.

She stands at the last column with the trimming iron cooling in her grip. Beneath her the water is very still. The knock waits. It has waited two hundred years and it can wait the length of one more breath, and she draws that breath, and holds it, and does not yet know which way her knees will bend.
