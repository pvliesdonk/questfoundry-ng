The road takes the last of him and does not give it back. Tamsin stands where the door left her, and the house is quieter now than it was with him in it, a different quiet, the kind that has stopped waiting for another voice.

She does not go to the glass. She takes up her matches instead. The oilskin drags at her shoulders as she pulls the hood, her mother's weight and not her own, and she steps out into the salt with the count already starting behind her teeth.

One. Two. The lamps burn their greenish burn along the ring, each one where she left it, each one hers now and only hers. She walks the stone and counts, and the count holds her the way a hand holds a rail in the dark. Forty. Forty-one.

She does not say the next number.

The dark lamp waits at its place in the ring, black iron gone green with the sea, salt-crusted, cold. Unlit. It has never taken her flame and it does not want one. What it wants comes up through the stone into the soles of her boots before it reaches her ears, patient, slow, older than her count and older than any copper Oren laid, and she feels it climb her the way cold climbs a wall.

The knock keeps its own reckoning. She stands close and lets it find her, because there is no longer anyone to stand between.

On the mainland the council will worry their papers and their votes. Eldric will worry the chain between two fingers and call it a delay, a setback, a matter of another electrician come spring. He will not believe her. None of them will. The wiring stalls where Oren dropped it, cut end pointing at the water, and the story that goes to the mainland is island foolishness, a drowned woman, a lamp that would not stay dark. They will laugh, or they will look away, which is the same laughing done kindly.

She is the only one who has heard it. She understands that now, standing at the black column with her matches unstruck in her fist. Her mother heard it and kept the count and told no one who would believe. Now the hearing is hers, whole and undivided, a thing passed hand to hand across two hundred years and set at last in her keeping.

The knock comes again. Nearer to certain. Never louder.

Tamsin lays her palm flat against the cold iron and holds it there, and the hum runs up her arm and into her chest and finds her pulse and matches it, slow to slow, and she does not pull away. Below her the water is patient. It has learned patience from her family. It can wait a little longer, and so, it seems, can she.

She is alone with it. She lets that be true. She counts her own breathing until even that falls into step.
