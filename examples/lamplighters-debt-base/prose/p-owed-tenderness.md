The change comes so slowly she does not know it for a change. The knock softens. Not weaker. Softer, the way a hand knocking to be let in becomes a hand only resting on the door once the door has opened. The rhythm loosens its grip on its own patience. There is a give in it now, a slackness at the top of each beat where before there was only the long wait and the coming again.

She stays where she is, cheek to the warmed iron, and lets it happen against her face. The hum thins. It settles down through the stone toward the black water and does not climb back up her spine so far. The next knock is quieter than the last. The one after quieter still. It is not stopping. She understands that. It will not stop while there is anyone left to lower a face to the floor of the world. But it is quieting, the way a thing quiets when it has been held long enough to trust the holding, and she keeps her breath alongside it as it goes down into that softness, giving it the rhythm still, because the giving is not a thing you finish.

There is a word for what she has become, and Sister Halvard's milky eye would name it kindly if she asked. She does not ask. She knows it the way she knows the count of the lamps without touching them. Keeper. The next in a line of women who lowered their faces and said nothing and meant it as mercy. Her mother among them. Junia, who walked this ring every night with the coat heavy on her shoulders and the logbook filling with her meticulous hand, and who came at last to the frantic pages and the half-drawn map because even mercy grows lonely and wants to know what it loves. Tamsin will not finish the map. She sees that now with a clarity that is almost peace. The wet ink can go dry. The route was never a way toward an answer. It was a way of coming, night after night, to sit with the thing in its dark and warm it against the cold it cannot climb out of.

Two hundred years of listening lie ahead of her like a tide going out that will always come back. She has chosen this. She feels the shape of the choice close around her, gentle, absolute, the bars of it made of tenderness so that they do not look like bars at all. A cage, yes. She lets herself think the word. A cage she built with her own open hands and stepped into and pulled shut behind her, and inside it the knock goes soft and slow and almost grateful, and she keeps the beat.

She does not lift her head. The green flames hold. The dark keeps her, and she keeps it, and that is the whole of the creed now, passed cheek to iron, breath to breath, hers to carry down the long ring of the years.
