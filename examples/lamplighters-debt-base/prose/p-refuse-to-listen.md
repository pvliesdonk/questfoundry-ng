The breath goes out of her. Her knees do not bend.

She turns from the water. That is all it is, at the start. A body turning, boots grinding salt on the stone, the trimming iron cold in her fist and the black column at her back with its slow patient measure going on without her. She turns and she keeps turning until the still water is behind her and the door of the tower is ahead, and the knock comes up through her soles the way it always comes, and she lets it come, and she does not answer.

"No," she says. Not loud. The word is small and it is enough. It falls into the murmur at the tower foot and does not sink. "I have listened all my life. My mother listened all hers. She listened until she went down into the listening and did not come up."

The knock keeps its rhythm. It has no ears. She knows that. She speaks anyway, because the words are not for the thing below. They are for the shape of the cage she has stood inside since she was old enough to hold the count, the ring of green light drawn tight around a town's sleep, each lamp a bar, each night a turning of the key. Trim and feed and hold the vigil and never ask, and hand it on cramped and salt-white to the hand that comes after. That is the mercy the creed calls mercy. She sees it plain now. It is only a leash worn long enough to be mistaken for a life.

"I will not spend mine at the edge of the water," she says. "I will not learn its measure by heart. I will not go down after you."

Halvard is not here to hear it. That is a small grief and she lets it pass. The old woman would have called this the breaking of faith, and it is. Let it be. Let the thing below decide what a two-hundred-year wait is owed when the last keeper walks away from the column and does not kneel. She does not know what she has loosed. She is honest enough with herself to hold that in her open hand. Perhaps the black lamp has been waiting only for someone to stop feeding it. Perhaps for someone to stop.

The knock does not quicken. It does not slow. It goes on behind her, owed and unanswered, and she has decided at last that it can go on owed.

She fixes her eyes on the door. She draws a fuller breath than the water ever let her draw, and she does not look back at the still black surface where her mother's question is still sinking, and she takes the first step toward the door on legs that have finally learned which way to bend.
