Word of the mainland's anger comes to her the way weather comes, from far off and without a face. The wiring stopped where Oren left it. The copper lies uncut against the stone, its end turned toward the water like something that changed its mind. No one will finish it before spring, if then, and the council across the strait mutters over its papers and its unpaid contract, and the muttering does not reach the island as words. It reaches as rumor. As silence with an edge on it. She hears they are angry the way she hears a tide two coves over, a sound that is not quite a sound.

Let them be angry. The anger is a mainland thing, and the mainland has never had to keep the count.

She stands at the black column in the grey before dawn with her palm still warm from its cold, and she knows what she knows and can prove none of it. She has asked. In the days since Oren went she has asked, quietly, the way her mother taught her to ask, reading the room before the words. The netmender looked at the floor. The woman at the tallow-house said the sea makes many noises against old iron, and it was true, and it was not the truth, and both of them understood that. No voice in town will stand beside her and say it heard the knock. They have grandmothers who heard it. They keep the not-hearing the way her family kept the count, a thing passed down and never spoken.

So she is alone in the hearing. She has made her peace with that word, or the beginning of a peace. The story that crosses to the mainland will be island foolishness, a drowned woman's daughter frightened of a dead lamp, and they will laugh or they will look away, and she has stopped needing them to do otherwise.

The knock comes up through her boots. Patient. Owed. Older than the argument stalled on the far shore, older than the copper, older than her count. It does not care that the electrician is gone. It does not care that no one believes her. It has waited two hundred years and it can wait through one more delay, one more setback, one more electrician come spring, and she understands with a cold that is not entirely fear that it is not waiting for them at all.

Morning is coming the way the tide comes, without asking. The council has voted the flames dead by daylight. She still has her matches in her fist, and the ring still burns green behind her, and the count sits unfinished behind her teeth. She turns from the dark lamp toward the living ones. Whatever she does now, she does with her own two hands, and no one to say she was right.
