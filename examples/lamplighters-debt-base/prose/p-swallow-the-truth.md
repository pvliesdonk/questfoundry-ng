She does not speak the word he wants. She swallows it instead.

It goes down like brine, like the taste of a hand pressed too long over a mouth. She feels it settle where all the other unspoken things have settled in her, the count she keeps and the questions she does not ask and the name she will not give the thing beneath the boards. One more silence, laid atop two hundred years of them. It fits. That is the terrible part. It fits so well she can hardly feel the seam.

Mowe reads it in her stillness. His breath lets go by degrees, the way a rope pays out over a cleat, and his fingers find the watch-chain again and begin their old worrying. He is forgiven, or near enough to it that he will tell himself the rest. She lets him have that. It costs her the same as speaking would have, and it leaves her mouth clean of the lie, and she is not sure the cleanness is worth anything at all.

The bribe stays where it has always been. Buried in the ledger against her chest, in the coin her mother took and the coin his father paid, in the long quiet chain of paid mouths reaching back past lamplight into the dark. The town will not learn what it never thought to ask. Below, the water keeps its slow work, and the knock does not come again, because it has already been answered. She has answered it the way her mother did. With her keeping. With her count. With the coat heavy on her shoulders and the seam pulling closed.

Mowe rises. He is smaller now, unburdened, an old man going home to sleep at last. She watches him fold himself out the door and does not rise to see him off.

Then she is alone with it. The room, the ledger, the boards, the patient thing beneath them. She thinks of the ladder waiting in the dark and the lamps waiting to be counted and the map her mother left half-drawn, its wet ink stopping where the asking would have begun. She will not follow it. She has just chosen not to. The silence is hers to carry now, and it will want a witness, or it will want no one at all.

There is a knock at the outer door. This one is knuckles, plain and human. Oren, come with his wire and his questions, come too near a thing he does not know he stands over. She holds still and hears him shift his weight on the step. She has kept one secret tonight already. She feels how easily the keeping comes now, and it frightens her more than the water does.
