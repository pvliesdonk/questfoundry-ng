She opens the door before the count can talk her out of it.

Oren stands with the coil of wire looped over his shoulder and that grin already thinning at the edges, the one she has watched wear down night after night on the ring. Copper burns mark his forearms like the tally of some other reckoning. He asks her something about the junction at Forty-Two, plain and practical, and she does not answer. She takes his sleeve instead. She leads him inside.

She brings the ledger down from against her chest and lays it open across his knees where he sits. The leather is swollen, the pages breathing algae into the small room. She shows him the count her mother kept and the coin folded in the back and the long chain of paid mouths, generation to generation, the town's silence bought and buried. She tells him the name of the man who pays. She tells him whose father paid before him. Her voice does not shake and that is worse than shaking would be.

Then she turns the last page. The map. The strokes that trail off mid-line where her mother's hand stopped, and the ink at the end of it still wet, catching the lamplight, never setting, never spreading, a thing that will not dry no matter the heat she holds it near. She lets him touch it. His finger comes away damp. She watches him understand that this is not water, that no water stays like this, and she watches the understanding go into him the way the swallowed word went into her, down and down to where it will settle and never leave.

Below the boards the knock keeps its slow patient measure, up through stone and iron, into the soles of their feet. She does not have to point to it. He hears it now that he is listening, and once heard it cannot be unheard.

"I could have sent you off," she says. "Frightened you back to the mainland. It would have been the kinder thing." She looks at him and does not soften it. "I am not going to. You wired the ring. You are standing over it same as me. So you will know what you stand over."

He is quiet a long while, the ledger open on his knees, the wet ink shining between them like something that has just been spoken. He does not ask to leave. She had thought he might, and part of her had wanted it, the mercy of one clean witness sent home whole.

He stays. He carries it now. Whatever comes up the ladder or up through the iron, it will find the two of them waiting, and the waiting is shared, and she cannot tell if that is rescue or only company on the way down.
