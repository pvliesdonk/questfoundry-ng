There is a way to end this. She feels it as a weight in the hand, the way she feels the ladder before she lifts it. She could carry the book down the lane at dusk when the caps lift. She could set it open on the widow's scale, on the sluice gate where Coom mends his nets, on the chapel step under the green light where Halvard blesses the flames. She could name the sum aloud. Two hundred years of promises, brown and small, and every one kept. The town has agreed together, gently, over bread. She could ask them to hear the agreement said in daylight in a voice that does not touch its cap to anyone.

And then. She sits with the and then. The names do not accuse; they wait. If she says the sum, she says the debt, and if she says the debt she must say what it pays, and the word for that is the word her mother shrank from, the word that leans the way a hand leans when it is afraid of the paper. To tell the town is to open every shut mouth at once. She does not know what comes through them when they open. She knows only that the water beneath the floor is listening for exactly that.

So not the town. Not yet. Perhaps not ever.

There is one other. He came from the mainland with copper burns on his arms and an easy grin, and he has been losing the grin a little each night, and he has not said why. She thinks she knows why. He has heard the lamps speak. He has heard the knocking under the black water and he has not run, and that is the thing that stops her breath now, worse than the ledger, worse than the caps in the lane. Oren Quist chose to be here. No one paid him to swallow it. No one taught him the count.

She could tell him everything. Share the weight with someone who is not tallied in the margin, who owes the ring nothing, who might stand in the dark beside her and hold the other end of the ladder. Or she could frighten him off before the wiring goes deeper, drive him back across the water clean, spare him the knowing, and keep the dark ring for herself the way her mother kept it, alone.

Telling is tenderness. Telling is a trap. She has learned that today, tracing the coin. Every kindness in this town is a thing paid so it will not have to be thought about.

The hall is empty. Somewhere ahead Mowe waits with his watch-chain worrying between two fingers, the man who receives, the man who calls his cowardice mercy. She closes the book on her thumb and rises.
