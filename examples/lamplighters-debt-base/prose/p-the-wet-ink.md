The chest is waterlogged past locking, and the seal breaks like something already tired of keeping. The logbook is inside, swollen leather the color of a drowned thing, and it opens itself to the last page as though it has been holding that page ready.

Routes. Column after column in her mother's cramped hand, the hours and the lamps and the tides, the ledger of two hundred nights that all look alike. Then the writing stops being writing. Junia's line begins to draw instead. A curve where a word should be. Another curve under it, wider, and another, and the curves are not decoration. They are a shape. They circle. They coil under the town the way the kelp coils against the wall, learning a habit, and the strokes go around and around beneath the marks that mean canals until they are drawing something too large for the page to hold, and the last stroke trails off mid-line as though the hand that made it was called away.

Tamsin sets her thumb to that final mark.

The ink is wet.

Not damp. Not the sea's damp that lives in every page here. Wet, freshly wet, black and cold and giving under her thumb, though her mother's hand has been in the water eleven months. She lifts her thumb. A smear of it comes with her, a small black tongue of it on the pad of her finger, and she does not wipe it on the coat.

Below her, through the iron and the water and the whole patient weight of the tower, the knock finds her again. Here. Here. Here. Slow. Timed. She knows the timing now the way she knows the comb. It keeps the hours her mother kept on the route, the same three beats at the same turn of the tide, as if the dark lamp has been marking Junia's rounds all this while, waiting for the tread that fits its step. The map in her lap and the knock beneath her feet are the same drawing made twice, one in ink and one in sound, and both of them are still being drawn.

Then the tower door groans below.

Light comes up the shaft that is not green. White light, hard and steady and wrong, swinging on a wire. A voice climbs with it, cheerful, out of breath, entirely unafraid.

"Anyone up top? Lamp Forty-Two's my last on the ring." A laugh, easy. "Whoever soldered these old columns did it to last. Charming, all of it. Bit of a graveyard down here, though."

Oren Quist. The mainland's electrician, come to wire the ring, come to make the flames obsolete by morning. He does not hear the knock. He is standing on the stair below her with his crates and his cheer, treating the dark like a room that only wants a switch, and beneath his boots the black lamp goes on keeping her mother's hours.

Tamsin closes the book on the wet ink and does not answer him yet.
