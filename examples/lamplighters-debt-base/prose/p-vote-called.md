The council house has already voted in its heart before it lifts a hand. Tamsin knows this the way she knows weather. Eldric Mowe stands at the head of the long table, his silver chain sliding between two fingers, and he speaks of morning as though morning were a kindness he had arranged personally. By the vote, the ring will be electric. By dawn, no child will fear the dark of the canals. He calls it the end of a debt. He says the word debt gently, the way he says everything, and no one in the room asks whose debt, or to whom, or what has been paid across two hundred winters to keep the water quiet. They only nod. They are so tired of being afraid.

She leaves before the count. She does not need to hear it.

Outside, the last light bleeds off the canals and the ring comes into its own. She walks it the way her mother walked it, lamp to lamp, and the green flames gutter low in their old iron. They have always been a poor light. Kind and useless, he called them, and he was not wrong. The flame at Lamp Nineteen shudders and nearly gives up. She cups her hand near the glass and it steadies, the way a thing steadies when it is watched. She does this at each one. It is the only prayer she has ever been taught.

The town wants its fear gone. She feels the wanting rise off the shuttered houses like heat off stone. They cannot say what the fire holds back. They have never had to. They only know that the green light has stood between them and something, all their lives, and that a stranger has come with a whiter light and a promise, and that promises are easier to love than vigils.

She rounds toward the black column and stops.

Oren is there, kneeling in his own hard glow, cable spooled across his knees. He is not grinning now. He has gone still in the particular way of a man who has heard a sound he cannot place and is waiting for it to come again. The knock comes up through the iron. Here. Here. Then something under the knock, a shape pressing at the shell of it, almost a word, patient as tide.

He looks up at her. All his easy certainty has curdled to something younger.

"Do you hear that," he says. It is not a question he wants answered.

The wet ink is cold against her thumb through the oilskin. The flame at her back holds its small green breath. She is the only one in the ring who has heard it all her life, and now she is not the only one, and the not-being-alone is its own kind of grief.
