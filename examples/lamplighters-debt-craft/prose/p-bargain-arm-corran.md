You watch his hands.

He has taken the tremor out of his pocket now, not meaning to, and set both palms open a little at his sides the way a man does when he wants to seem to be holding nothing. They are steady. Ink at the cuffs, thin at the knuckles, the skin gone soft the way hands go when the work they do is signing. Forty years of a pen. Forty years of a number carried in a ledger and paid out each spring, and none of it once touched the water or the wick or the cold iron in front of you.

The knock comes up again through your boots. Once. Then again, unhurried, the way it has always come.

You know these hands. Not from him. You know them from the story that lives in the story he is telling, the picture he has just given you without meaning to. She stood just there. He offered her a kindness and meant it the same. So there was a night his hand went out toward your mother's and hers went into it, and the coin passed, and the asking stopped, and he walked back down the narrowed path believing he had spared her. You can see it. His hands would have been steadier then. Younger. The pen not yet worn to this softness. But the gesture is the same one, the palm turned up, the offer laid flat where a frightened person can take it without seeming to reach.

You hold your mother's tools. The wick-hook keeps the shape of a grip that was not yours and never will be, and it fits your hand wrong, and you would not trade the wrongness of it for anything he can name in a year or a lifetime of years. His hands are empty and open and calm. Hers were full of iron and grief and the char at the seam she could not explain, and she carried them into the water rather than into his kind palm again.

You were going to be quiet. You had it in you to be quiet. You feel that possibility go out of you now the way the green went out down the line, one lamp at a time, until there is nothing left to speak into but the dark and the water and this soft-spoken man who thinks stillness is the same as mercy.

His hands stay very still.

You make yours still to match, and you look up, and you find his eyes, and you tell him no.
