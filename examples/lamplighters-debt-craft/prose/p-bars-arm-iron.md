You walk back along the row, and this time you touch it.

The first strut is cold under your palm. You knew it would be. You have brushed these columns a thousand times without meaning anything by it, the way you brush the wall of your own house. Now you lay your hand flat against the iron and hold it there, and the cold comes up into your fingers slow and even, the way water comes up.

Old paint blisters under your skin. You feel the seams where the housing was riveted shut, the pitting the salt has left, the ridge where a hundred hands before yours have worn the metal smooth at the height a hand naturally falls. You fit your grip to that worn place without deciding to. Her grip fell there. Her mother's grip fell there. The iron remembers the shape of them the way the wick-hook remembers hers.

You move to the next strut and touch that one too. Then the next. You are counting them, you realize, the way the thing below counts, one and then a stillness, one and then a stillness. The struts stand at their measured distance down the black water, evenly set, never moved, patient as posts sunk to hold something at bay. The green burns low between your hands.

You had always felt the warmth. That was the thing. You had walked this route and felt the small heat of each flame at your cheek and thought the iron kept it for you. But the iron is not warm. The iron has never been warm. It stands cold in the wet air and holds the fire up and holds the dark down, and the two hundred years of tending never once thawed it, because it was not made to be kind to the hand. It was made to keep its place.

You take your palm away. The cold stays in your fingers a moment, then goes. On the stone at your feet the light lies in its long divided lines, and you stand between two struts with the wet coming through the soles of her boots, and you do not call this the safe part of the path anymore.

Below the deepest bend, the water knocks once. You keep your hand at your side and let the stillness come after it, and you count it with the thing that counts.

Ahead of you the row runs on to where it goes dark. Lamp forty-two stands at the end of it, unlit above the hollow stair. You have been walking toward it your whole life without knowing. You turn your face that way now.
