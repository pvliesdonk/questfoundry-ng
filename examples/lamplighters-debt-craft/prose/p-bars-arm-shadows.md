You turn back to it. The forty-second lamp stands where it has always stood, cold iron blistered with old paint, its glass empty and stained the green of long water. It is the one silent thing on a road of hissing flames. The others breathe. This one holds its breath, and you have come to stand in the middle of that holding.

The lit lamps behind you throw their cages down. You watch the shadows fall. Each housing wears its ironwork, the little bars that keep the wind off the wick, and the flame pushes that pattern out across the wet stone until the whole path is written in stripes of light and stripes of dark. You have walked in this writing your whole life. You never read it.

You read it now. The light lies in long divided lines along the canal, and the divisions are even, and the dark between them is even, and the two go on together down the bend and out of sight. Bars and the spaces between bars. The stone holds the print of them the way water holds the print of a dropped stone, a moment, then smooth again as the flame steadies.

Your boots stand in one of the pale bands. You shift, and the stripe crosses your mother's coat, up the too-long sleeve, across the striker warm in your palm. For a breath the light divides you too. Then it settles.

The knock comes from below. Once. It does not double. The water goes still after it, and you stand in the stillness and let it be what it is, a thing keeping count, a thing that has counted longer than any hand that ever climbed the hollow stair.

You look up the column to the empty housing. No flame has stood in it for two hundred years. No shadow falls from it. In the long banded light of all the others, lamp forty-two casts nothing at all, and that nothing lies across the stone at your feet, a single unbarred stretch of wet dark where the pattern breaks.

You stand at the edge of it. The green burns behind you, and the bars lie down the path the way they always have, and the one place they do not reach is here, under the lamp that no one lit, above the deepest bend, where the water is keeping its count and waiting for the next hand on the stair.
