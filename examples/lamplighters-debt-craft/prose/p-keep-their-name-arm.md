The boards narrow as they go down, slick with the green light and the water's breath. Sable comes with you. She does not ask to be led and she does not fall behind. At the deepest bend the stone crooks like a wrist, and there the canal goes black and holds its own, and you stop, and she stops beside you.

The ledger is still in your pocket. You both know where it is, the way she knows where the key is without touching it. Neither of you looks at it. It rests against your ribs like a stone you agreed not to throw. You could name what it holds. You could say her family's name and the sum and the years aloud into the dark, and she could answer, and then the thing between you would have edges. You do not. She does not. The not-saying settles into the damp between you, level and quiet, the way water finds its own line in a lock.

You set the tools down on the ledge. The wick-hook, worn to your mother's grip. The striker no one else was ever let to hold. Sable's hand comes to rest near them, on the cold stone, close enough that you feel the small warmth of it. She does not reach. She looks at your mother's hook the way she looked at the flames, as though she is learning at last what a thing was for. Her fingers stay open on the stone.

You think she might speak. There is a breath in her that could become a word, the forbidden one, the one that would make the whole account plain and let her set it down at your feet. You watch it rise in her the way the water is rising, slow, patient, filling the silence to its brim. It reaches her mouth and stops there. She swallows it. She keeps it. It is the last kindness left to give and she gives it by giving nothing, and you take that too.

So the two of you stand over the tools and the dark, allied and unabsolved, holding a silence that is both the mercy and the cost of the mercy. Below the bend the black water moves against the stone, once, then holds. You feel the weight of the buried thing press up through your boots. You let it stay buried. You put your hand near hers, not on it, and you wait for what you have to do next to become the only thing left to do.
