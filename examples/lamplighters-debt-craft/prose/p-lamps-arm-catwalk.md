You back away from the dark column and take to the catwalk instead, the narrow iron gantry your mother strung her whole route beneath. It runs high above the water, rail worn smooth by hands that were not yours, and from up here the forty-one flames lie below you like a chart of themselves. You count them again. It is a thing you do now. You count them the way she must have, checking the seam of light for a dropped stitch.

One through ten cluster tight at the town's heart, close-set where the houses lean together over the canal. Then they thin. Eleven and twelve keep their distance across the fish-market water. You mark each green point with your eye and move your lips over the number, quiet, the tally settling in you like ballast. The catwalk hums faintly underfoot. Old bolts, cold air, the give of metal that has held longer than it was ever meant to.

Below, the black water takes the flames and does not give them back. Each green tongue floats a twin of itself down into the dark, and the twin goes deeper than the flame is tall, further than light has any business reaching. You lean on the rail and watch the doubled fires sink. Thirty. Thirty-one. You have to slow yourself. It would be easy to lose the count out here where the wind pulls at the coat and the oilskin flaps like it wants to be off you, back down into the water it came up from.

Forty. Forty-one. You reach the far end of the catwalk and there is no forty-two to add. There is only the gap where the last light should hang, a rung of darkness in the strung green, and past it the deepest bend, folding the canal back on itself so the water thickens and goes still. From up here you can see how the route was built to end there. Everything leads to the cold column. Every flame behind you burns as though it is turned toward that one dark place, forty-one small watchers keeping their patient faces to the unwatched thing.

You hold the rail. You do not count the dark lamp. You could not, and she knew you could not, and she left you the gantry anyway so you would stand here and see the shape of it whole. The knock comes again, up through the iron, up through your palms. Soft. Once. Then the stairs down to the towpath are just there, at the end of the walk, dropping toward the water's thickest fold.
