You go along the towpath toward them, and the crews do not look up. They are past looking. Their work is the work of men who have been told the difficult thing is settled and now only wait to be let home. Cables loop from their hands into neat coils, wound and stacked where the old flames used to stand. You pass the first cradle and it is still warm to no touch of yours, the iron holding the last of a fire that burned before you were born and will not burn again.

Where lamp forty was, a man kneels with a length of copper across his knees. He is threading it into the housing your mother scoured every ninth night, into the socket she kept clean of salt with a rag she wore soft. He does not know that. He works around the char at the seam and does not ask what made it. You step past him and the coil brushes your ankle, and you feel the difference under the coat, the cable heavier than rope, patient in its own way, a thing meant to carry current a long time without rest.

The next cradle stands empty already, its glass lifted out and laid in a crate with straw between the panes so they will not knock together. You stop. The green has gone from that one glass and left it plain, ordinary, a jar. Someone will keep it on a sill. Someone will not know.

The knock comes up through your boots, unhurried, and you keep it under your breath as you walk, once, the pause, once more. You carry the oil against your chest. You carry her tools in the deep pocket where she carried them, the wick-hook worn to the shape of her grip, the striker she never let anyone else touch. The crews wind their copper. The water works its seam. Between the two you thread yourself north, along the guttering line, toward the one column they have left standing because it was never theirs to put out.

The dark ahead is the dark of forty-two. It has been waiting the whole two hundred years, and it waited for her, and now the towpath narrows to a single width of stone and there is no way past the crews except forward, into it, with what you hold.
