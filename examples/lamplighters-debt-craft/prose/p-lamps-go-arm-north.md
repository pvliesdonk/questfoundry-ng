You go north along the towpath, past the crews, and you count the lamps the way she taught you, low under your breath, the numbers a kind of prayer you no longer believe in.

The crews have moved ahead of you. You can see the coiled cables catch the last of the green and give it back cold. Thirty-eight. The flame gutters as they reach it, a small hiss, a smaller silence, and then the water beside it goes flat and black where the doubled fire had been. Thirty-seven. You pass the drowned brightness a stretch at a time. Each lamp is warm still when you come level with it, the iron holding the memory of a fire it no longer has, and you put your hand out and touch one that has just gone, and it is like touching the flank of an animal that has only now stopped breathing.

Her oil is heavy against your hip. The tin she carried, dented on the one side where it rode her thousand crossings. You have not needed it. There is nothing left up here to feed.

Thirty-six. The men do not look at you. They work the way people work at a thing they have been told is kindness, quick and eyes down, and their lamps go out under their hands one after another as though the lamps themselves had asked for it. Thirty-five. The dark runs together behind you now, the seam between one drowned stretch and the next closing over. You keep the count because it is hers. Because when there is no more green there will be no more numbers, and then there will be nothing of her out here at all but the coat and the tin and the word she left in a book.

The knock does not follow you north. It stays where it was, patient, at the fold. But the quieter the lamps go the further it carries, and you understand that you have been hearing it all along in every crossing she made, hearing it and not knowing, the way a child hears rain in the walls and calls it the house settling.

Thirty-four. Thirty-three. You walk into the thinning green with her oil and her count and the map folded against your heart, and ahead the towpath bends toward the deepest water, toward the one column they will never reach, standing dark over the knocking as it has stood two hundred years. You slow. You do not need the tally anymore to know where you are going. Your feet have known since the stair.

You take up her tools and turn toward lamp forty-two.
