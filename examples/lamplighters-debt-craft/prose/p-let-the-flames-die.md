The tools stay where you set them. You do not pick them up. The forty-second lamp stands over you the way it has always stood, cold iron blistered green, its glass empty and stained, and beside its number your mother wrote a single word you have carried down these hollowed stairs your whole life. You do not touch the striker. You do not break the seal. You leave the wick unlit and you turn your back on the dark column, and this is the choice, made plain by making nothing happen at all.

Upstream the men from the council have their new work. You hear it more than see it, the clean click of a switch thrown, the far hum of a wire waking, and one by one the green flames along the ring begin to give up. They do not gutter the way a wind takes them. They thin. They lose their color first, going pale, going grey, going the way your mother went, down and quiet and without a sound to mark the moment. By the low turn of the morning there is not one left. The town wakes to a light it can trust, even and white and answerable to a dial. Corran will sleep tonight. His ledger closes on its own now. Forty years of signatures fold shut with nothing left to buy.

You stand in the last of it. The route is done. Two hundred years of watched flames end here, in your salt-chapped hands, in your refusal to reach. Your mother wrote that you must never leave a flame you have not watched settle. You have watched them all settle now. You have watched the last of them settle into nothing.

At the deepest bend the water keeps its own count. It does not knock. The knock was the patient thing, the single sound and the stillness after, a creature marking time behind a wall it trusted to hold. That sound is gone. In its place the black water moves, long and unhurried, against stone that no longer answers with light. You feel it come up through your boots, the way you have always felt it, only now there is nothing above it. The lamps were bars. You understand that standing here. They were bars, and the door is open, and you were the one who opened it by keeping your hands to yourself.

You wait for the boots on the boards, the green light bending under a door, the name you called half up the stairs. None of it comes. Below the bend the water finds a new line, and holds, and moves again, learning the shape of a dark that has no keeper. You leave the tools where they lie. There is nothing left to watch them for.
