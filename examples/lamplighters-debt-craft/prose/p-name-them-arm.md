The green light is going out of the square. It goes slowly, the way it always goes, lamp by lamp along the far walls, and it lays its last color across the two of you standing where the crowd made room. Sable does not move away from you. She could. There is nothing holding her here now but the looking, and the looking is the whole of what she has left.

Her name is loose in the air. The Ondreys. You said it plain and now it belongs to everyone, passed hand to hand across the settled faces, worn already like a coin that has been in too many pockets. It was hers before this. A door, a warm room, a house key on a chain she has worried thin against her collar since she was a girl. Now it is theirs to hold, and they hold it the way you hold a thing you have found out about, turning it to see the underside.

She stands unmended among the mended. That was always the tell of her, the clothes that never needed sewing in a town that sewed everything twice. You used to think it meant she had been spared. You stand close enough now to see it was never sparing. It was the mark of the money, sewn into the cut of her sleeves, and she has worn it her whole life not knowing it could be read.

Her hand finds the key. She works it. The chain is worn where her fingers have gone a thousand times, and it goes there now, in front of all of them, because she does not know what else to do with her hands. You watch the small motion and you do not stop it. You do not take her wrist. You have your mother's coat too large on your shoulders and your mother's hook cold in your grip, and you know what it is to hold a thing that was left to you and never asked for.

The last green flame on the near wall dips and holds. In its low light her face is turned toward you, open the way a wound is open, waiting for the thing you have not decided whether to give. You have set the ledger down. You have set the years down. What stands between you now has no book to close it in. It is only the two of you, and the name, and the dark coming on, and the choice of what you do with the hook still warm from your hand.
