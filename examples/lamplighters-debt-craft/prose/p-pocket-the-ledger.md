You find her at the top of the boards, where the town's light has not yet decided what kind of evening it will be. Sable stands apart from the others, the way she has learned to stand. The key rests on its chain against her collar. Her fingers are not on it yet, but they know where it is.

You fold the ledger into the deep pocket of your mother's coat. The leather settles against your ribs. You do not raise your voice to the square. You do not carry it up any steps to be read aloud. You let the town keep its supper and its lamplight and its two hundred years of not knowing, and you step close to Sable instead, close enough that what passes between you stays between you.

You tell her plainly. The money her house was built on. The lamp that stays dark. The silence her family was paid to keep, generation over generation, longer than either of you has been alive. You give her the answer she was forbidden. You do not soften it and you do not sharpen it. You set it down between you the way you would set down a thing too heavy to hold at arm's length.

Her hand goes to the key. She works it against her collar, thin from the years of doing so, and for a while she says nothing. She is not looking at you. She is looking at the row of green flames as if seeing them for the first time, or seeing at last what they were always for.

When she speaks, her name is still her own. No one in the square will take it from her tonight. You handed her ruin and then closed your fist around it before it could fall, and she knows this, and the knowing sits between you now, unpaid and unsettled. She could have hated you for the answer. She chooses otherwise. She lets go of the key and lets it hang.

"Then I stand with you," she says. Not because you cornered her. Because she has looked at the dark lamp and decided.

It is a gift and it is a wound, both. You accept it as both. Something is settled between you and something never will be, and you leave the never-will-be where it lies, because some things must be carried unwrung.

Below the row the water keeps its count. One knock, then stillness. Sable hears it too. You see the moment she hears it, the small turn of her head toward the deepest bend, and you understand she is ready to go down and look at what her family was paid never to name.

"Come, then," you say, and you start down toward the water.
