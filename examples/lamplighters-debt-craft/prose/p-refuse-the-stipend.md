The word lands and does not echo. There is nothing out here to catch it. Only the dark iron and the water and Corran, who does not move for a long moment, whose open hands stay open as if they might still be given something to hold.

Then the hands close. Slowly. They come back to his sides, and one goes toward the pocket again, and stops, because there is no coin now to press and no tremor he can afford to hide. He has nothing to do with them. That is the thing you watch. A man who has spent forty years with a task for his hands, a pen, a number, a palm turned up in the last of the green, and now the task is refused and his hands do not know where to go.

"You don't understand what you're asking to keep," he says. His voice is soft as ever. It is the softness you distrust most.

You do not answer him with words. You take the logbook from inside the coat. It comes out swollen and cold, the leather gone green at the corners, and you hold it up so the last of the light down the line can find it. You do not have to say whose hand wrote in it. He knows the hand. He signed beside it for forty springs.

"Every year you paid her to stop," you say. "You called it mercy. Say it now, with the book open. Say it was mercy."

He does not say it. He looks at the book and the water knocks up through both your boots, once, then again, and you understand that his silence was never the same as hers. Hers was full. His was only a lid.

You open the logbook to the page that will not dry. Her hand runs the deepest bend of the canal in wet ink, and around the dark of it she has set them down one at a time, small marks like struck matches, and you count without meaning to. Forty-two. They ring the thing below the way lamps ring a square. And you stand there with the char-smelling page tilted to the light and you cannot tell, you cannot yet tell, whether the flames were laid down to keep faith with what waits under the water or to keep it from ever rising to the air.

The smudge sits at the deepest bend where her hand pressed too long. The margins are thick with her cramped notes, warnings folded into instructions, her voice made small to fit the paper. Corran is still saying your name. You have stopped hearing it. You lean closer to the page.
