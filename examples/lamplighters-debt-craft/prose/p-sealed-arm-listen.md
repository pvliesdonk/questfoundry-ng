You do not move. The page lies open in your lap and it has weight now, more than paper should carry, as if the wet ink pooled somewhere behind the words and settled there and cannot be lifted out. Your hands stay where they are. The coat is too large for you and you have grown used to swimming in it, but you feel small inside it now, folded into the smell of her, into the salt she never washed out. The tin sits open at your knee. The wicks are counted. The ledger is grey against the green leather where you laid them side by side, and you leave them like that, touching, because you cannot yet decide what touching means.

Below you the water speaks. It is not loud. It never was. It works its way along the underside of the town the way it always has, muttering at the stone, testing the joints, patient in the dark under the deepest bend where the forty-second lamp stands cold and gives it nothing to see by. You listen the way she taught you to listen, before you knew what you were being taught. There is the ordinary lap of it. There is the drip somewhere further off that has dripped your whole life. And under those, when you hold still enough, when even your breath goes quiet, there is the other thing. Not a voice. A knock. Soft. Once, then a long nothing, then once again, from below the floor she walked, from below the route that is yours now.

You keep the page flat with your thumb where she left the place for it. The circle she drew runs on under everything, past the margin, past where a hand should be able to reach. You have read it to the end and the end does not close. You sit with that. You do not name what you feel, because naming it would make it smaller than it is, and it is not small.

The wick-hook rests against your hip, worn to the curve of her grip. You have not lit anything tonight. You are not sure, anymore, that the lighting was ever the point. Somewhere below, the knock comes again, and waits, as if it too is listening, as if it has learned to hold its breath the way you are holding yours. And then, from a place that is not below at all, from the ordinary world of doors and hinges and people who sleep dry, there comes a sound you were not braced for. Someone is knocking above the water now. Someone is at your door.
