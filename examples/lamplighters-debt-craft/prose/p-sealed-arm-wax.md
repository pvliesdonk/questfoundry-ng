The seal on the ledger is a different age from the paper it closes. The grey is council-new. The wax is not. Someone lifted this seal from an older document and pressed it here to make the thing look older than the silence it records, and the wax remembers being warm two centuries ago and has been cold ever since.

You touch it before you decide to. Your thumb goes to it the way it goes to the wick-hook in the dark, knowing the shape before the light does. The wax is brittle under the pad of your finger. It has gone the green of the logbook's stains, the green of the flame you carry up the canal each dusk, the green that the town has agreed not to name. In the lamplight it holds a little of the lamp. You press, and a flake comes away and stays on your skin, dry as a moth's wing, and you understand that this seal has outlived every hand that ever pressed a fresh one over it.

The impress in the wax is a ring. Not a crest. A plain closed circle with a line drawn through the width of it, the way you would draw a canal crossed by a bridge, or a mouth crossed by a finger. You have seen the mark your mother scored into the base of lamp forty-two with her wick-hook, the mark she never explained and never let you copy. It is this one. The seal and the scoring are the same hand reaching across two hundred years to sign the same instruction twice, once in wax and once in iron, and both times the instruction is: closed.

You rub your thumb clean against the oilskin. The green does not come off entirely. It never does. Below the canals the water knocks, softer now, patient, the sound of something that has waited so long it has learned the shape of waiting. Your mother's coat holds you at the shoulders like a hand that will not let go. On the stairs, then, a weight. A knuckle finding the door. Someone has come while you were reading, and stands now on the other side of the wood, and waits for you to answer.
