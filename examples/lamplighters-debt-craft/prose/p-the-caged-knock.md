You climb the hollow stair to where the seal sits in the iron, and you crouch, and you set the wick-hook down across your knees where you can reach it.

The knock comes up through the stone. You feel it now in your teeth before you hear it. One sound, patient, and then the water goes still, and the stillness is worse. It was never worse before. You knelt at other lamps and heard the flames breathe and thought this was a route to be walked with your head bowed, the way your mother bowed hers, the way you bowed yours at her burying. You gave the dark below its reverence because she had. You did not ask what the reverence was for.

You know now. You touched the struts and counted them and felt them stand like posts driven to hold a shape at bay, and the knock is not asking to be honored. The knock is testing the seam. It has been testing it for two hundred years, one patient sound at a time, keeping count of the hands, and every green flame down the row is a bar, and behind the bars a thing goes hungry in the wet dark and waits for the counting to end.

The seal has loosened under your fingers. Marren wrote one word beside this number and the ink is faded now but the word held. Don't. You could set your palm flat and never break it. You could climb down and let the vote pass and let every flame die by morning, and the iron would go cold along the whole canal, and whatever the flames have held would come up the loosened seam into the black water that no longer only knocks.

Or you fit the striker to the wick, the striker no one else was ever let touch, and you light lamp forty-two, and you take her place at the bars, and the counting goes on another life. Yours.

There is no third stair. She climbed these steps a thousand times. The one time she did not come back up, the water had her name in it, and you called it from halfway down the boards the way you called it as a child, and no boots answered, and no green light bent under any door. You understand it as a warning now. You understand all of it as a warning now, laid down in worn iron and one held word for a hand exactly your size.

The green burns low behind you. The seal is warm from your grip. Below the bend the water keeps its count, and it is your turn to answer it, and the answer will end in your hands either way.
