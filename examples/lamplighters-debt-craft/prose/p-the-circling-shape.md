The stair holds you a moment before you take it. You open the logbook one last time under lamp forty-two, though you told yourself you were done, and the wet page lies there as it always lies, the ink refusing to set, the map drawn in your mother's steady hand. The shape is on it still. It circles beneath the flames, beneath the row of small green lights that mark the canal from the first lamp to this cold last one, and it never rises, and it never breaks the surface, and nowhere on the page does it stop to be fed. It only goes around. Two hundred years of lamplighters watched their flames settle and wrote their small warnings, and every night the shape kept to its slow round below them, kept counting the way the water counts.

You cannot decide what your hands are holding. Whether the lamps were lit to honor a thing owed light and grief, or lit to hold it under, a starving circle kept starving on purpose. The two readings sit side by side in you and will not settle, the way she taught you a flame must, and you close the cover on both of them because the room below will not wait.

You go down. The stair gives you to the town the way it gave your mother to it, worn hollow in the middle by boots that came before yours, and the green light bends away behind you and does not follow. The council has already begun. You hear them before you see them, cordial, tired, the low agreeable murmur of people who want a thing switched off by morning and are near enough to it now to be gentle.

In the oilskin's inner pocket the ledger sits against your ribs. You know whose names run down its columns. You know the one name that would break the room open if you read it aloud, and you know the cost, because that name belongs to the family of the only person who stood on your step tonight and asked what your mother found. Speak the ledger and the vote could turn. Speak it and you ruin the one face in this room that might have turned toward yours. You keep your hand on the wick-hook, worn to a grip that is not yours, and you step down into the light of the vote with the wet page still circling behind your eyes.
