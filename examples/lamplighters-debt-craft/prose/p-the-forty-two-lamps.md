The coat is too big. You knew it would be. Your mother's shoulders were wider than yours, and the oilskin hangs on you like something borrowed from the drowned, which it is. You keep the sleeves rolled twice so your hands stay free for the wick.

The first lamp stands at the mouth of Herring Lane. You climb, you strike the striker, and the green catches and holds. It is a soft sound, that catching, a breath let out under water. You wait to be sure of it, the way she must have waited, the way the logbook says to wait in her small even hand: *do not leave a flame you have not watched settle.* You watch. It settles.

Second lamp, third, fourth. The canals are quiet at this hour. The water lies flat and dark between the houses, and the green light does not so much shine on it as sink into it, going down and down until you cannot say how deep the light reaches. You speak to the lamps as you tend them. Not aloud, exactly. You tell the sixth one it is stubborn. You tell the eleventh you are new at this and it must be patient. Somewhere your mother crouched at each of these in turn, always writing, and the striker still remembers the shape of her grip better than it knows yours.

You lose count and find it again. Thirty. Thirty-one. The flames trail behind you along the water like a seam being sewn, stitch by patient stitch, holding the town together at its wet edges. By forty you are tired in the arms and warm in a way you distrust, because pride in this feels like something she would have warned you against.

Forty-one burns above the fish market. That is all of them but one.

The last stands over the deepest bend, where the canal folds back on itself and the black water gathers thickest. Iron column, old paint blistered to scale, the glass housing empty and stained a green that no flame put there. You have your striker in your hand. You have oil enough. There is nothing to stop you but the two hundred years it has stood cold, and the note in her logbook beside its number, which is not an instruction but a single word: *don't.*

So you don't. You stand below it while the other forty-one hiss faintly behind you, and this one gives back nothing, no light, no heat. Only, if you hold very still, from somewhere down past the paint and the empty glass and the water, a sound. Soft. Once. Then again. Like a knuckle on a door too far away to reach.
