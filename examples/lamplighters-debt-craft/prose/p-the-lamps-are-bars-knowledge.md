You have read the map to its end, and now you cannot look at the lamps the way she did.

You hold the striker in your palm. It is warm from your grip and cold at the edges, the way her hand was the last time you touched it. For your whole life these flames meant care. You climbed the stairs behind your mother and watched her feed each wick, and the green light bent under every door, and you thought that was tenderness. You thought the town lit its dead a path.

You were wrong about the shape of it.

Look at them now. Look at the way they stand in a line down the canal, each one a bar of light laid across the black. Not candles. Not vigil. A fence. Two hundred years of hands going up and down these stairs, and every hand thought it was worship, and every hand was turning a key.

Something is under the water. The map does not draw a monument. It draws a circling, a thing that has gone round and round beneath the bends until the stone forgot it was ever free. The knock is not a summons. It is a body against a wall. It is the sound of counting, the sound of a thing that has learned patience because patience was all that was left to it.

You think of the word in the logbook beside lamp forty-two. Don't. You had read it as fear. Now you read it as apology. Whoever wrote it knew what the flames were for, and kept feeding them anyway, because the alternative was worse than a locked door.

The hunger down there is old. Two centuries without light, without the smallest mercy, held starving in the dark so the town could sleep above it and call the keeping a kindness. Your mother went up and down these stairs. Your mother fed the fence. And when she drowned, she drowned close to the deepest bend, and you did not let yourself wonder why.

The green light lies along the wet stone in stripes. You stand inside them. They fall across your boots and your mother's too-large coat and the hook worn to the shape of a grip that is no longer here.

A jailer is only a tender who has told herself a better story. You are not sure, now, which one you are, or which one she was.

Below you, the water knocks once. Then keeps its count.
