The shelf you have not opened opens now. There is the tin she kept her spare wicks in, and under the wicks, folded flat so it would lie without a crease, a ledger not her own. The cover is council grey. You know the grey before you know the hand. It is the paper Corran writes his notices on, the paper he holds out with both palms turned up when he wants a frightened person to take a thing and call it a gift.

You open it and the years fall out in a column. A date. A sum. A signature. The signature is his, forty springs of it, the tremor growing into the letters the deeper you read, so that the early Corran wrote a firm clean C and the late one leaves it shaking. Beside each sum a second name signs for having been paid. The name is Ondrey. Sable's name is on the last lines, the child's letters of it, then the woman's. She was paid before she could read what the money was for. The money bought her house and her key and her good coat, and it bought, line by line by line, that no Ondrey would ever ask why lamp forty-two stays dark.

You have met a girl who was purchased and did not know the currency. You set the ledger down beside the logbook. Grey against green-stained leather. Corran's hand against your mother's. Both of them writing in the dark to keep the same silence, one paying for it, one watching it settle.

The logbook is still closed over your finger. You have read the page as far as it will be read gently. The wet part waits below, the part where the two hands agreed and you did not want to see where. You could pry no further. You could sit here between the tin and the shelf and let the last line stay unread, and know only what a ledger can teach, which is the price and not the reason.

But your thumb is already in the place she left for it. The ink there never dries. Under your finger the bend runs on past where she stopped, and the shape of it is not a line anymore. It circles. It comes around beneath the canals and comes around again, vast and unfinished, drawn by a hand that ran out of margin before it ran out of the thing it was drawing. You take your finger out. You let the page fall open the rest of the way. The wet dark of it is a shape too large for the paper, and you read it, and the water below knocks once, and holds its breath with you.
