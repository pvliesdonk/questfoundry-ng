You do not go into the vote. Not yet. You turn back to the lamp instead, and the striker is already in your hand, the one no one else was ever allowed to touch, and you thumb it once and the green comes up the way it always comes up, low and patient and the color of things kept under water.

You open the logbook one more time. The wet page waits. The shape circles. You hold the leather to the flame.

It does not want to catch. Waterlogged things never do. The edge browns and curls and the green stains go darker before they go at all, and you watch the map take the fire the slow way, the canal of small lights swallowed lamp by lamp, the first, the middle, the cold last one under your hand. The shape blackens where it circles. The ink that would never set curls up into the heat and is gone, and it does not rise, and it does not break the surface, and now it never will on any page a living hand can read.

The margins go last. The browner writing that leaned the wrong way, the older hand that answered her instead of instructing her, curls in on itself and answers nothing. Her warnings fold into the flame the way they were always folded into her instructions. You cannot ask her which reading was true. You will never be able to ask her again. The stair once gave you her boots on the boards and the green light bending under the door, and you called her name up it and waited, and this is the last thing she left that could still speak, and you are holding it to the fire so it will stop.

The town is safe from it now. No lamplighter after you inherits the map. Two centuries of small warnings end in your salt-chapped hands and the ash falls into the lamp's iron dish and settles, the way she taught you a flame must settle before you leave it.

You have watched it settle. You can go down now.

But the answer is gone with her, and the choice she died holding falls to you alone, and you do not know anymore whether the lamps are grief or bars, whether the thing below is owed the light or held under by it. You burned the page that might have told you. You will decide blind, the way she must have decided once, with nothing to go on but the grip worn into the wick-hook and the ring of green lights waiting on the water.

You look again at the ring of lamps.
