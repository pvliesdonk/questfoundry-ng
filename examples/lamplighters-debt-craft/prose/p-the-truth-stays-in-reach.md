The coin is gone from the air between you. You did not take it. You feel the absence of it the way you feel the seal still warm under your thumb, a thing not done that stays possible, a thing not sold that stays yours. Nothing has closed over. No hand shut around the day. The word your mother wrote is still legible on the iron. The seam is still a seam. Below the bend the water keeps its patient count, one knock and then the stillness, as if it too is waiting to see what your hands will do now that no one has bought them back.

You stay crouched in the hollow the boots wore. You let the questions sit where they are, uncarried off by any stipend, unpinched into any peg-hung coat. They are heavy in the way the wick-hook was never heavy, and you find you can hold them. That is the strange thing. You can hold them and still breathe.

You look down the row from where you kneel. All your life the green light bent under the door and meant she was home, and you called her name up the stair and got boots on the boards and thought the lamps were how she loved the town. You thought each flame was a hand cupped over something cold to keep it warm. You understand now what the flames are. They are not care. Every lamp she lit was a turning of the same key, and the route is not a vigil kept out of tenderness but a wall built out of it, and behind the wall is a thing that has been kept from its dark two hundred years and has learned to knock softly and wait.

She knew this. She knelt here and knew it and went up the boards and hung the coat and let you sleep. The map told her and she read it to the end and she carried the reading down under the water with her rather than set it in your hands. You have the reading now. You cannot give it back. It will be under everything you touch after this, under the striker, under the seal, under whatever you say to whoever comes up the stair next.

The green burns low. The seal is warm. You rise from the hollow with the wick-hook across your arm and the knowing settled into you like cold water into cloth, taking its own shape, refusing to be wrung out. The town is up there in its light, and one of them has been paid to keep this quiet longer than anyone living has drawn breath. You know her name. You know it should not stay a kept thing. What you do not yet know is whether the truth goes to one pair of hands first, or to all of them at once.
