You come up the stair into the cold and the knock finds you again, softer now, or maybe you have only stopped bracing for it. Below the dark lamp the black water works at the stone. Not the slap of a wake or the wind's chop. Something patient, from beneath, testing the same seam over and over the way a tongue goes back to a broken tooth. Once. A pause you could set your breath to. Once more.

The council keeps its lights burning in the hall above the towpath, and you can hear them through the shuttered glass, the low even talk of people who have decided already and are only saying it aloud to make it real. Corran's voice among them, gentle, apologetic, the voice of a man laying a coat over something so no one has to look. They are voting on the cables. On the morning. On the end of it. He has wanted this forty years and tonight it comes to him quiet as a signature.

The vote carries. You know before the words that it carries, because the town has always wanted the past to switch off clean, and now there is a switch. By morning the ring goes to current. By morning the green is done.

Then the first light dies.

It is one of the northern lamps, out past the bend, and you feel it before you see the crews reach it, feel the tally lurch in you as it drops from the count. Forty. A crew moves to the next with their cables coiled like they are gathering rope off a drowned thing. Thirty-nine. The green goes out of the water a stretch at a time, the doubled fires that sank so deep sinking now into nothing, and the dark that was held in forty-two places begins to run together along the canal.

You stand with the oil you carried and the ledger's numbers still cold in your hands, the figures that laid by, year on year, for a flame she wrote *don't* beside. One by one the watchers close their eyes. The knock does not stop for them. If anything it comes clearer now, up through the stone and into your boots, into the coat that is not yours, as each snuffed lamp leaves it more room to be heard.

At the end there is only the forty-second, unlit these two hundred years, dark as it has always been, the one they cannot put out because it was never lit. It stands over the thickest fold of water and keeps its empty green glass turned down at the knocking. The town thinks it has won something. You look at the last dark column and understand it is the only one still burning, in the only way it ever has.

The crews are between you and the water, their cables strung across the towpath. North lies the guttering line of the dying lamps. You will have to choose your way past.
