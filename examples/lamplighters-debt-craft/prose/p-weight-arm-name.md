You do not answer her. Not yet. You turn from the door, from the fitted coat and the worried key, and you look at the lamp your mother watched settle a thousand nights before this one.

You say her name. Not to Sable. Into the dark past the lamp, into the low room and the black water beneath it and the bend where the light does not go.

"Marren."

It comes out smaller than you meant. The oilskin coat hangs heavy on your shoulders, hers, cut for a wider back, and the wick-hook sits warm in your fist where her grip wore the iron soft. You say the name the way you used to say it up the stairs when you were small and could not sleep, half a question, waiting for the answer that always came, the boots on the boards, the green light bending under the door.

Nothing comes now. The room holds still. The logbook lies open under the lamp, her hand running down the page in ink that never fully dried, the map circling toward the place it will not close. Never leave a flame you have not watched settle. You learned to read her that way, in corrections, in the words she pressed hard enough to score the paper beneath. She spoke to you in margins. She does not speak now.

You wait. You give her the silence you would give a lamp, the long patience she taught you, the watching that outlasts the wanting. You listen for the boots. For the striker no one else could touch, ringing once against its tin. For any small proof that the woman who went down learning this is somewhere on the other side of the line, waiting the way the water waits, ready to carry you back up as far as the bend.

Below you the water knocks. Once. That is the only answer the dark will give, and it is not hers.

So you close the logbook. You do it slowly, the wet page last, your thumb lifting from the circle that does not meet itself, and the cover settles over all of it, the map and the drownings and the two hundred years of quiet. You breathe out. Sable is still at the threshold, still listening to you, still asking with her whole stilled face what your mother found.

You have finished reading. There is nothing left up here to hear. Whatever you carry now, you carry it down into the town, into the vote and the lamps and the sealed dark iron, and you take up the hook and turn toward the stair.
