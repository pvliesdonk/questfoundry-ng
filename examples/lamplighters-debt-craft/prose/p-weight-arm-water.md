You do not answer her at first. You go to the window instead, because the answer is not in your mouth but down there, under the deepest bend, and you have always spoken best to the things that cannot speak back.

The canal lies black below the sill. Lamp forty-two stands cold above it, its green glass empty, the one dark column in a row of small hissing flames. You know the exact place. You have known it since you were old enough to be told not to look, and you look now. The water there does not move like the rest. It holds a stillness that is not calm, the way a held breath is not rest.

You wait for it. You have learned the rhythm the way your mother learned it, the way she wrote it small in the margin beside the number and then wrote the single word beneath, the word you have run your thumb across so many times the ink has gone soft. Don't. She did not write why. She drowned before she could write why, or she wrote it in the map instead, the circle that runs on and never closes, and you finished that reading tonight and cannot give it back.

The knock comes.

It is soft. It is one sound, from below the surface, from the far side of a wall of water two centuries thick. It is not the hull-knock of a boat, not the bump of debris on iron. It is patient. It has the patience of a thing that has counted every night the lamp stayed dark and is content to count more. It knocks once and then the water is still again, waiting, the way Sable waited on your step for a line she was raised not to cross.

You keep your hand on the wick-hook. It has worn to the shape of a grip that is not yours. Below you the black bend holds its breath and yours, and you understand that the lamps were never lit to honor what waits there. They were lit to keep it counting. Your mother learned that in the water. You are still standing on the dry side of it.

Sable is behind you in her fitted coat, asking again what she found. You close the logbook over the wet page, over the map, over the word your mother left. The seal lies flat and plain on the green leather. You take the striker no one else was permitted to touch, and you turn back toward the door, and the town, and the choice waiting for both.
