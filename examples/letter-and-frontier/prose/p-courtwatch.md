Elara stood in the stone hall as the soft rustle of robes swelled, the courtiers’ eyes shifting toward the doorway where the messenger had entered. The scarlet wax pressed cool against her thumb, the queen’s emblem a silent weight. Candlelight caught the iron stamp on the parchment, its edge faintly glowing. The court’s advisor, Meros, stepped forward. “Your counsel has been a boon,” he said. He inclined his head, then said, “I thank you, courier.” Elara lowered her head, bowing just enough for the tip of her chin to meet his gaze. The murmur of the lords settled into a low hum, the stone walls drinking the distant drum that rolled outside. A scent of pine resin and rust‑colored dust drifted in, a reminder of the Silver Plain beyond the arches. The knot in her chest tightened; the moral conflict she had carried since the quill touched the wax now pressed against her ribs. She steadied her breath, aware that every glance could weigh the oath she altered against the safety of the realm.
