She turned west, and the trail widened onto the exposed stretch of the Silver Plain. The wind carried a faint murmur that locals called the River Clan, their swift riders who rode the boundary like ghosts. The plain spread beneath a mute sky, cracked earth flayed by rust‑colored dust that rose in thin clouds. Rumors she had heard in the market told her the clan kept watch here, their spears flashing when danger approached.

She felt the satchel’s weight—cracked biscuits and a half‑filled skin—as the air grew colder. The sky darkened, a thin line of gray gathering on the horizon, promising a sudden sandstorm. In the distance, the stone towers of Stonegate Fort rose, its walls cold and its gate left ajar, the iron bars catching the wan light. The fort could shelter her, but its garrison was known for recording every traveler’s step.

A rider in the River Clan’s colors fell into step beside her, his spear tipped with rust, his eyes scanning the horizon. He nodded, his voice low. “We will keep the plain clear,” he said. She raised her hand in acknowledgement, tightened the strap of her cloak, and urged her horse forward, the riders matching her pace as the storm loomed and the fort’s gate waited as an uneasy alternative.
