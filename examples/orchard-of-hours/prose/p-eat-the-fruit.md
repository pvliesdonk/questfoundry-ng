You take the fruit down from its stem. It comes away too easily, warm in your palm as a small breathing thing, split at the seam and honey-heavy, collapsing where your thumb presses. You do not read the tag. You know the weight of this one without lifting it, have known it the length of the office, the single hour you could not carry laid down in loam so that it might belong to no one, least of all to you.

You lift it to your mouth. You bite through the spoil.

The rot gives, sweet and rank, and under the honeyed pith your teeth find the cold stone, and past the stone the hour beats once against your tongue and is swallowed. It goes down whole. It does not stay swallowed. It rises.

The room comes back. Not this room. The other, the one you buried, the low light and the hand you would not take and the face turned toward you waiting for the word you gave away instead of saying. It returns entire, undulled, with all its weight restored to your arms at once, and the weight is more than you were ever meant to bear standing. Your knees find the loam. The office lifts off you like a coat too long worn, and the cold it kept out comes in, and you are only a woman in an orchard at dawn with her hour back and her dead unsaid goodbye pressed whole against the inside of her ribs.

You weep. You have not, in all the long keeping. It is not relief and it is not clean. It is the plain animal grief you sent away, come home to a body that can hold it now, that must.

Dawn finds you there. The mist thins to nothing and the grave-cold light lies flat across the rows, and the trees stand as they have always stood, bearing their low heavy fruit, their tin tags still and dull. No keeper walks among them shielded. The sorrow they carry carries no one now who cannot be touched by it. Whoever comes after, kneeling with a jar and a heaviness they cannot name, will find the orchard fruiting on and no one exiled to bear it for them. Only the ground. Only the trees. Only the hours, kept the way loam keeps a stone, and someone, ordinary and grievable, to plant the next.

You wipe your face with a soil-stained hand and let the fruit fall from the other, its black stone bright with nothing.
