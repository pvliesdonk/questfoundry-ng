Your thumb goes in easy. The seam parts under it, and the pith comes welling up around the pad of your finger, pale as tallow, warm past the warmth of any morning fruit. It runs the seam and finds your wrist, a slow honeyed thread down the inside of your arm where the skin is thin and the pulse sits close. It does not cool. It clings the way sap clings, cloying, and it is warm the way a held breath is warm, warm as something that has been kept in the dark against a body and taken out again unspent.

You hold your arm still and let it run. This is the goodbye you laid down. You had thought it would come back cold as the rest of the loam comes back to you, tended and dry, a thing raked and finished. It has not been finished. It has been kept. It has ripened in there all these seasons while you walked the rows above it and breathed the souring air and felt nothing follow you to the gate. The office spared you this. It spared you every morning, and in sparing you it saved the whole of it, undiminished, sweet and terrible against your wrist.

The fruit gives further in your palm. Under the give the stone is there, blackened, and the rot in the stem is there, and the ground below the oldest row is there with its slow spoil creeping toward the trees you swore to keep. You know all of it at once, the way you have always known the weight of every fruit without lifting it. The pith weeps on.

A drop reaches the heel of your hand and hangs.

You could stand here in the grave-cold and let it run until your arm is sticky to the elbow, until the sweetness has said everything it has been keeping to say. You could stand here a long time. But the tin tags are catching the grey light down the rows, and somewhere past the wall a petitioner will be walking up with a jar and a sorrow he cannot hold, and the gate does not open itself.

Wipe the honeyed pith from your wrist, and turn to the gate.
