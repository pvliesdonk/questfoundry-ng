The knife goes in past the give. The pith parts wet around it, and the sweetness thickens as though the fruit would keep you at the surface, would have you press and stop and let the soft flesh close warm over your thumb. You do not stop. You draw the edge down through the spoil, through the last living beat of the thing, and the two halves of your surrender fall open in your palm.

At the center of it lies the stone.

It is black all through, no seam of paler kernel left, and it takes no light. The morning lays its grey across the ruined flesh and finds nothing there to catch. You lift it free of the pith with two fingers and it comes away clean, shedding the sweetness that had held it, wanting no part of the fruit that grew to guard it. Cold. Colder than dawn over the rows, colder than the tin tags under your thumb, cold the way a word is cold when you have swallowed it and let the years pack the ground down over it. This is what the goodbye became, down in the dark, kept for you all this while. Not the soft thing that still beat under the rind. This. Hard, and small, and closed.

You hold it up between the two ruined halves and the smell of the rot rises, the same souring that has crept through the loam and found the trunk and gone down into the ground you swore to keep. You understand that they are one thing, the goodbye and the spoil, that what you laid down to take the keeping has been curing in the dark all these seasons into this. You turn it once. The stone does not warm. It will not warm in any hand.

Beyond the split fruit and its blackened heart the gate stands open on the grey, and someone is already there, waiting at the edge of the rows with a jar cradled against him. He does not come in. He waits to be let in, or turned away.

Close your fist around the cold stone and turn to the gate.
