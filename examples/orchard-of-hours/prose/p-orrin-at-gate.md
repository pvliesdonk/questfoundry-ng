He is at the wall when you reach it, no farther in than the last stone lets him stand, a young man with his coat buttoned wrong and a jar held against his chest the way you would hold something that might still wake. First light lies grey along the rows behind you. The smell of overripe fruit comes with it, sweet and going off.

"They said the keeper takes it," he says. "The hour. So a body doesn't have to carry it alone." He turns the jar in his hands, once, and laughs, low and wrong, and stops himself. "She has such small hands. My daughter. You wouldn't think a thing that size could hold on the way she holds on."

You know the office he means. You took it the day you laid down a goodbye you could not stand to say, and you have not wept since, nor mourned, nor felt the dead follow you to any gate. A mercy. You have never been able to tell it from a slow walking-out of your own skin.

"Come," you say, and lead him in.

You pass the oldest row without looking at it. The bark there weeps dark, the trunk gone soft, and the rot has found the loam and started along toward the neighbors, into roots that were never its own. You read the tags as you go, thumb to tin, and you feel the spoil under the rows the way you feel each hour's weight without lifting it. It is spreading. It will not wait for you to be ready.

He talks the whole way. Her hands, the way she grips his finger, how she laughs at the wrong moments the way he is laughing now. You weigh what he brings against the ground you would trust it to.

At the planting bed you stop. He sets the jar into your palms.

It is warmer than you thought. It thrums, faint, an hour not yet spent, a small kept life against your soil-stained skin. To open the bed is to lay his last mercy in ground you know is failing. To fold his hands back around it is to send him home with the whole of it, undiminished, his to hold.

He watches your face. The tin tags catch the grey. The jar goes on beating in your hands.
