The mound settles under your palm and gives up its warmth to the morning, that sweet-rot heat rising off the fresh-turned loam, the smell of a thing already going over. You do not take your hand away. The dawn lies quiet on the rows, grey and level, and the tin tags hang unmoving down the length of the failing ground. Somewhere behind you the wall gate has stopped its knocking; Orrin is gone, lighter, carrying nothing but his wrongly buttoned coat.

One grief heavier now, and none of it lodged in you. You feel for it the way you would feel a bruise, pressing to find where it hurts, and find only the even cold the office lays down in place of hurting. His child's hour rests a hand's depth beneath your fingers, folded into a soil you know is spoiling, and the spoil will come for it as it comes for the trunk two rows over, in its own slow hour. You wagered against that. You laid the small kept life in the widening softness anyway, because the other thing, the sending-home, the leaving him to hold it, was a cruelty your hands would not do.

So this is the office, weighed out plain and handled. You take what would undo them and it does not undo you. You keep the ground whole a while longer for whoever comes after, kneeling with a jar and a heaviness they cannot name, and you keep it at the price of standing here untouched among the weeping trees. You know the cost of it entire now, and the worth. Both at once. Neither cancels the other.

The soil sinks a finger's width and holds. You draw your hand back and it comes away dark to the wrist, cold as the bed you closed. Down the row your own tree bears its low heavy fruit, split at the seam, honey-heavy, going soft on the stem. You have not looked at it in a long keeping. You look at it now. Somewhere under your ribs, where a goodbye once lived and lives no longer, the quiet waits to be answered, and the grafting knife lies where you set it in the grass, and the dawn holds still, and the trade you have gone on making asks to be made once more, or unmade.
