You turn the jar and press it back into him. His hands come up out of habit, and you fold his fingers around the glass, warm against warm, the beating hour returned to the chest that carried it in.

"The keeper cannot take it," you say. "Not now. Maybe not again." The words come plain and cold, the way you would tell a man his well has gone bad and the water can no longer be trusted. "The ground here is failing. If I lay her hour down, I cannot promise it stays whole. I cannot promise the rot won't find it. Better you hold the whole of it than lose it here."

He does not understand at first. He looks past you to the rows as though the trees might argue on his behalf, as though somewhere among them stood the mercy he walked all this way to leave. There is none to give. His mouth works. No laugh comes this time. He holds his daughter's coming death against his coat, buttoned wrong, and he holds it now the way you told him he must, alone, undiminished, the small kept life still thrumming under his palms.

"I only wanted her to be light," he says. "After."

"I know," you say, and you do not, and the not-knowing is the office speaking, the thing that has kept the dead from following you to any gate.

He goes back through the wall the way he came, no farther out than the first stone lets him stand, and then past it, into the grey, the jar warm and turning in his hands, his to carry every hour of the way. You watch until the mist takes his shape. You do not follow. You have never been able to follow.

Then you are alone among the souring rows, and the words you said go on standing in the cold where you left them. The ground here is failing. You hear your own voice return it to you, flat, true, spoken aloud at last. It is the one thing the keeper must never let be so. To say it is to unmake the office in the saying. The oldest row weeps its dark sap behind you. The tin tags catch the light and give none back. You stand and listen to what you have said, and it does not soften, and it does not go.
