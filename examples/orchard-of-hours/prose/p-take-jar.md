You kneel. The bed opens under your knife and your hands, loam turned up dark and cold, and you set the jar into it the way you would settle a seed that has already begun to want the light. You know this ground. You have read it under your thumb a hundred mornings, and lately it has answered wrong, a sweetness going over at the edge of it, a soft place widening toward these rows. Still you lay him here. You wager that the orchard can hold one more sorrow before the rot finds it, that his small kept life will keep a while longer than the trunk two rows over, and you cover his mercy with the same earth the blight is walking through. It is a bet against your own hour. You make it anyway.

The soil goes over the jar. The thrum dims and is gone, folded down into the dark.

He does not see the tree that weeps. He is looking at the fresh mound, at your stained hands, and something in his face has come unclenched, the whole of it laid down at last where he could not carry it. "Thank you," he says. "For taking what I couldn't hold." He buttons his coat wrong over the empty place against his chest and turns for the wall, lighter, going out into the grey unburdened, past the blackened bark he never marks.

You stay and watch the ground settle. This is the office, then, weighed out plain: you take what breaks them, and you feel nothing break in you, and you plant it in a soil you know is failing because to refuse is a cruelty you cannot bear either. The dirt sinks a finger's width and holds. You press your palm flat to it. Somewhere under your ribs, where a goodbye once lived, there is only the cold even quiet the office leaves.

You think of her small hands, gripping, the way he told it. You wait to feel the weight of that. Nothing comes. And you understand, kneeling in the spoiling loam with his grief safe and yours long since given over, exactly what this keeping costs and exactly what it spares, and whether that trade is one you can go on making. Your hand smooths the last of the soil level. The tags catch the light down the failing rows.
