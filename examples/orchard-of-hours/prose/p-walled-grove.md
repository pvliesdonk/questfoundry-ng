Dawn comes grave-cold over the Orchard of Hours, and you walk the rows as you have walked them since your braid was black. Each tree bears its single fruit, low and heavy, an hour someone could not carry and chose instead to lay down here. You know the weight of every one without touching it. The tin tags catch the grey light, small and cold under your thumb as you pass. The air is sweet the way orchard air is sweet, and beneath the sweetness, this season, a souring you have learned to breathe without flinching. The office keeps that from you. Grief is a thing you tend, not a thing you hold; you rake it into the loam and it does not follow you home.

At the end of the oldest row you stop. This tree you planted with your own surrender, the hour you gave up to take the keeping, the goodbye you could not stand at the foot of a bed a long time ago. Its fruit hangs split at the seam, blackened, collapsing on itself. Dark sap weeps from the bark below it. The rot has found the trunk and gone down, and you follow it with your eyes into the ground that feeds the neighboring rows, the ones you swore to keep whole. It is spreading. You have known for some days that it was spreading.

You draw the pruning knife, worn to the shape of your grip, and set the edge to the stem. One clean stroke. The fruit comes loose into your palm, warmer than the morning has any right to make it, warm as a small breathing thing. Honey-heavy. It gives against your hand where the skin has opened. Inside the spoil, faint under the sweetness, something still beats. Your own hour, the one you laid down, still living in the ruin of it. The goodbye you never said, kept for you all this while in the dark of the fruit, ready. Your thumb rests at the split seam. The pith is soft there, weeping, ready to be pressed. Deeper, past the give, the blackened stone waits under your knife.

Press your thumb into the weeping pith, or cut deeper to the blackened stone.
