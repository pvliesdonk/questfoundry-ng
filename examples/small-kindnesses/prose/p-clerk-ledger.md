The clerk's finger comes down on the page and begins to move. You watch it more than you watch anything else in the room, this single pale finger tracing the lines of the ledger, each line a name and each name owed. The ink is not quite ink. It catches the lamp and holds it too long, wet-looking where it should be dry, dark in a way that seems to go down rather than lie flat on the paper. Where the finger passes, the letters shiver, as if they know they are being counted.

You lean closer without meaning to. The names mean nothing to you and yet the weight of them presses at your chest, one debt after another, a whole column of people carried on this table the way you have carried other people's parcels through the wet all your life. Someone owed a face. Someone owed a kitchen. Someone owed the sound of their name in a mouth that loved them. The finger goes down and down, unhurried, and the smell of the damp paper rises around you, and somewhere behind you the stallkeepers are folding their night away.

The finger stops.

It lifts, just barely, hovering over a line you cannot read from where you stand, and the page seems to hold its breath under it. The lamp does not flicker. The rain has gone so thin now that you hear the small dry rasp when the corner of the leaf begins to rise, the clerk's other hand at the edge, patient as water. The page holds. It does not turn yet. It waits the way a held note waits, the way the string on your parcel waited under your fingers before you touched it, and you understand that the turning is not a threat so much as a fact, coming whether you are ready or not.

Her name is under that finger, or yours is. You cannot tell which. Both weights sit on the same quiet table and the lamp burns steady over them and the clerk does not care, has never cared, which one you lift. The page tilts. The finger stays where it is a moment longer, giving you the moment as one gives a coin, and then the paper begins, softly, to turn.
