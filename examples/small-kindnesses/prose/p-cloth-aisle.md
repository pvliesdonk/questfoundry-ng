You turn from her because you cannot keep looking, and the aisle of grey silk takes you in.

The bolts hang from their rods in long soft columns, and the rain has been at them. They drink the wet from the air the way cloth does, thread by thread, until each length hangs heavier than it should, sagging at the fold, darkening from the hem up. You pass close. The silk brushes your shoulder and leaves a coolness there, a faint damp weight that does not lift when you step past it. Grey on grey on grey, the color of a sky that has forgotten how to be anything else.

You have walked this aisle a hundred times with your satchel bumping your hip and never once slowed. It is only cloth. It is only the thing they sell before the things that matter, the same way a shop puts its plainest goods at the door. But tonight your hand comes up without your asking and you catch a fold of it between two fingers, and it is heavier than water has any right to make it. As though the silk were holding something back too. As though it also had drunk more than it could carry and was waiting, quietly, to be wrung.

Behind you Emery is still folding paper around small kept things, and the sound of it follows you between the bolts, that dry patient rustle. You do not turn to check whether her jaw has gone any thinner in the light. You keep your eyes on the cloth. You let the grey close over the lantern row until it is only a warmth at your back, a gold you can feel without seeing.

The parcel with your name on it waits somewhere past all this hanging weight. You know exactly where. You have always known where everything waits in this market; that is the whole of your usefulness. The string is tied in the market's exact knot, the one your fingers could undo in the dark.

The rain thins. You feel it thin, the way you feel the silk go heavy, in the skin more than the ear. Ahead the aisle opens. You let the last bolt trail across your knuckles, and then there is nothing between you and the counter where the parcels are set down and left.

You cross to where the parcels wait.
