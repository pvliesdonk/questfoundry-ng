Then the market goes quiet.

Not the quiet of the rain finishing, which you can still hear if you reach for it, the last of the drops finding the tarps at longer and longer intervals. This is a different silence. It comes up from under the boards and fills the aisle to the brim. The murmur that was always here, the haggle and shuffle, the far-off clink of coins passing hands you never see, all of it stops between one breath and the next, and the stopping has a pressure to it. It leans against your ears. It presses the way deep water presses, and you swallow to make the ache go, and the ache does not go.

You stand in the middle of it. The lamp beside you gutters and steadies, gutters and steadies, and the small hiss of its flame is the only thread of sound left, thin as a hair drawn through the dark. Everything else has been collected. Even the quiet feels owed to something, feels counted, feels set down on the far pan of a scale you cannot see.

Your hands are still closed. You have not opened them. There is nothing in them and there is everything in them and you do not trust yourself yet to know the difference by feel. The silence lets you keep them shut. It asks for nothing. That is the worst of it, that it asks for nothing now, that the debt is paid and the ledger closed and the aisle emptied and still you stand here as though waiting to be told you may go.

Somewhere against your coat there is a weight you had forgotten, a small squared bulk, string-tied, patient as everything else in this place is patient. You do not touch it. Not yet. You only feel it there, keeping still, the way she kept still under your grip until she could not.

The silence holds. You hold inside it. And for a moment that has no edges you are the only sound left in the world, the small wet catch of your own breathing, going on.
