The lights go one at a time, and they do not hurry.

The nearest lamp shortens first, the wick pulled down to a coal, and the coal to a memory of a coal, and then the glass is only glass. You watch it happen and it does not feel like closing. It feels like folding, the way you have seen her fold a cloth over something she meant to keep, corner drawn to corner, the crease pressed flat with the flat of a thumb. Around the place where she stood the stalls put themselves away. Not toppled. Not torn. Just done, the way a day is done.

Across the aisle the fruit-seller's string of small bulbs winks out, left to right, unhurried as counting. Then the lantern over the ribbon stall. Then the one that was always crooked on its nail, the one she used to tilt straight when she passed, muttering at it. It goes out crooked. No one will straighten it now.

The dark comes in behind each light like water finding the low place. It is not a sudden dark. It arrives the way a blanket is drawn up over someone already sleeping, settled at the shoulder, tucked without waking them. You stand in the middle of it and let it reach you last. Your own lamp is the final one still burning, guttering low at your elbow, and you do not touch it. You let it choose its own moment.

When it goes, it goes gently too.

The aisle is black then, and quiet, and the rain has counted itself down to almost nothing, a drop, a long wait, a drop. Your hands are still closed. You can feel the shape of the market settling around the empty space, indifferent, already forgetting, the stalls breathing out their little lights and keeping nothing of her at all. You keep all of it instead. The whole warm weight of her, folded corner to corner in the dark of you, pressed flat and put away where it will stay.

You do not move toward the exit. You are not ready to be somewhere she has never been. So you stand where the last light was, in the blanket-dark, holding what you carried out, and you let the rain finish its counting alone.
