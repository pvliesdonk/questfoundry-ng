You watch her hands.

They have gone still on the counter, the cracked cup half-swaddled in its cloth, her fingers stopped mid-fold as if the cloth had turned to water and there was no point closing it around anything. That is the stillness you know. Not rest. Not pause. The stillness of hands that have already been told, that have already set the argument down and found it too heavy to carry a second time. You have seen it in your own hands over a thousand doorsteps, the moment before you turn away empty. She has it now.

The lantern thins along the edge of her wrist. You can nearly see the counter through the back of her hand, through the bones you have watched tie a hundred knots quick and sure, through the small competent grace of her that never once fumbled a string. It is fumbling now. It is not moving at all.

You want the hands to go on packing. You want the cup wrapped and the cloth tucked and the whole ordinary business of closing a stall to keep happening, because as long as it keeps happening she is not going anywhere. But the hands know. They stopped before her face did, before her voice did, the way a body always knows a thing an instant before the mouth agrees to say it.

You set the parcel down on the counter. Softly, so the string does not catch. Your name lies there between you, soft on the wet paper, and you do not look at it. You look at her fingers on the cloth, and you put your own hand near them, not on them, a hand's breadth of cold counter left uncrossed.

Her thumb moves. Just that. It finds the frayed hem of the cloth and worries it once, the smallest habit, the thing hands do when the rest of a person has gone quiet. The rain has come down to single beads now, one and then another off the awning. You count them without meaning to. You are good at counting things you will have to give back.

Stay, you think, and do not say. Let the packing take as long as it takes. Let the last cup stay unwrapped. Her hands are still, and you keep yours near them, and the small distance holds.
