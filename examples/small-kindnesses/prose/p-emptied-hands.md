You look down at your hands.

They hang at the ends of your wrists as if borrowed, brought to you by someone who could not tell you what they were for. You flex the fingers. They answer, but slowly, the way a word answers when you have said it too many times and it has gone strange in the mouth. There is a lightness in them you do not trust. Something has been set down and not picked back up, and the hands do not know how to be hands that are not holding it.

You turn them over. The palms are the same. The old callus at the base of each finger, worn there by strap and knot and the long ferrying of other people's parcels through other people's rain. You know the callus. You do not know the ache above it, the empty curl where a warmth used to sit and be carried.

Once these hands waved back. You cannot find the memory of waving, only the shape the shape has left, a hollow the size of a gesture. You bring them close to your face in the thinning light and they smell of wet rope and cold tin and nothing tender. You cannot recall what tenderness they once knew how to keep.

The rain is gone. The tarps drip in their own time now, unhurried, and the market breathes around you as if nothing has changed, as if a debt were only weather. Against your chest the one warmth left to you presses steady, the parcel with your name tied shut, unclaimed, still yours. Your hands could reach for it. You watch them not reach.

They lie open instead, loose in the lamplight, lighter than they have ever been and less sure of what light is for. A carrier's hands. Made for the weight of others, emptied now even of the weight they never meant to set down. You close them and open them once more, testing, and they close and open, obedient and far away, and you cannot say whose they are.

Beside you the woman is still waiting. Still smiling. Still holding out the warmth of a thanks you have no hollow left to hold.
