You step back from the claiming-table and the world tilts toward glass. There is a pane beside the stall, tall and rain-dark, the last water sliding off it in thin bright threads that carry the lamplight down and let it go. You pass it the way you pass everything, sideways, meaning only to be gone. The reflection catches you before you can.

Someone stands in the glass wearing your coat.

You know it is your coat by the strap worn pale at the shoulder, by the way the hem hangs heavy on the left where the parcel rides against your ribs. You know the coat. You do not know the person inside it. The face there is a face you have carried all your life and it looks back at you now like a room with the furniture taken out, plain, unclaimed, no one waiting in it. There is nothing in the eyes that answers to being watched. Nothing that has ever been called across a market by name.

You lift a hand. The stranger lifts a hand. The gesture is exact and it means nothing, only two bodies confirming they are the same body, and the confirmation lands cold. You had thought a person was mostly this: the weight, the coat, the water on the shoulders. You are learning that a person is also the places another has kept for them, and those have gone out of you now like heat, and the shape left standing in the glass is only shape.

Behind the reflection, blurred, the stall glows. Behind the reflection, blurred, a gentle-faced woman is still turned toward where you were, her mouth still soft around the sounds of you. You could look at her directly. You keep looking at the glass instead, because the glass asks nothing of you, and she does. Against your chest the warmth you did not spend sits waiting, a small tied weight, the one thing tonight that is still yours to hold.

The rain has stopped. The threads on the pane thin to a last few and then to none, and the stranger in your coat stands very still in the clearing light, breathing when you breathe, going nowhere until you go. You lower your hand. So does the glass. There is nothing more the pane will tell you.

You turn back toward the stall, and toward the smile that is waiting to thank a person you can no longer prove yourself to be.
