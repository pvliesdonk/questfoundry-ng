The cloth moves again. She takes up the cup a second time, though the cup is already half-wrapped, and folds the linen over it as if the wrapping were the point and not the thing inside. Her fingers are slow. They know their work even fading, even with the counter showing through the backs of her knuckles like a bruise of light. She does not look at you while she does it. She looks at the cup.

"Don't trade anything," she says.

The words come quiet, laid down the way she lays down the cloth, careful, without weight she means you to feel. The rain is one bead now and then another off the awning, and her voice fits in the spaces between them.

"Some things are worth more kept than saved."

You hold the string. It keeps its cold line across your thumb. You wait for her to say which things, to point at herself or at the brass or at the parcel with your name gone soft on it. She does not. She wraps the cup and sets it in the crate and reaches for the pressed flower, the nameless ribbon, the small worn nothings she has carried through every rain you have known her, and she works as though there were all the time in the world to close a stall no one will open tomorrow.

Kept than saved. You turn it over. She could mean the cup. She could mean the years she has spent handing warmth across this counter and taking nothing back. She could mean herself, telling you to let her go rather than spend the record of her seeing you to hold her here as someone who would no longer know your face. She could mean the two of you, the thing that has no name and never got one, the thing that would not survive being paid out coin by coin to a clerk who does not care what he collects.

You cannot tell. That is the cruelty of it, that she would give you even this last instruction wrapped so you could not see its shape.

Her thumb finds the frayed hem of the crate-cloth and worries it once. The rain thins toward stopping. You have never once kept a thing for yourself in all these years, and now she is asking you to, and you do not know what she has named.

You look at the parcel. You look at the string.
