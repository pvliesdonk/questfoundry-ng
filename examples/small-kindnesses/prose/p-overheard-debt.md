Behind you the two voices go on, soft as folded cloth. You do not turn, but you can place them by the smell of their stalls, the one who sells wax and the one who sells rope, close enough that their words come to you between the last of the rain.

One of them says a name you half know. The other hums, the way people hum when a hard thing has passed them by and landed on someone else. There was a debt, the first one says. Last winter. The kind that eats a person down to nothing. And then, come the thaw, it was gone. Settled. Nobody knew how.

They are not gloating. That would be easier to stand against. Their voices are low and kind and shot through with the particular tenderness of the spared, the ones who watched the water rise to a neighbor's door and stop there. She was lucky, one of them says. You could feel it in her, after. Like she'd been let go of.

You stand very still. The lamp holds its small circle. The parcel sits under it with your name gone soft in the damp, and the ledger lies open beside it with a name that is not yours, and the stallkeepers behind you talk about someone who was let go of and cannot say who did the letting.

That is how it works, then. A debt settled in the dark leaves no hand behind it. Whoever paid, paid so quietly the town keeps only the mercy and forgets the cost. The one who gave is unwound out of the story. No face lights at their name. No kitchen smells of them.

The rain has nearly stopped. You feel the hush arrive like a held breath, the drops gone to a bead and a fall, a bead and a fall. The wax-seller wishes the rope-seller a good night. A shutter comes down. The market is closing around you the way it closes around everything, without malice, without memory.

You look at the two weights on the table. Your loss, tied and waiting. Her loss, priced and coming due. Your fingers are still near the knot, near enough to feel the cold of the wet string without touching it. There is a coin the night is asking, and you have carried enough parcels to know that some are meant to be set down and some to be taken up, and that the difference has never once been easy.
