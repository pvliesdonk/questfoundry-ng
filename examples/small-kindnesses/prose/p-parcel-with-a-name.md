The table is bare but for two things, and one of them is yours.

The parcel sits at the center under the low lamp, brown paper gone soft with the damp, the string tied in the knot you know by heart. Your name is on it in a hand you almost recognize, the letters leaning the way your own letters lean when you are tired. You do not touch it. You stand at the rim and let your hands hang, and for once you do not move forward, because forward would be reaching, and reaching would be admitting the reach.

The clerk is a shape more than a face, brass scale beside it, patient in the way water is patient. It lays a palm near the parcel without lifting it. Inside, it tells you, is a loss of your own. Something you set down so long ago you stopped counting it as gone. The market has kept it for you, addressed and waiting, the way it keeps everything unclaimed, and it will keep it still if you leave it tied. It does not press. It only balances.

Then it turns the ledger toward you and reads out the other account. Emery's.

Her debt, it says, is paid in a single coin: the memories of being known. Every hand that ever cupped her name, every face that lit at seeing her, every kitchen that smelled of her. When the rain stops, she is collected. Not hurt. Not taken anywhere. Only unwound into indifference, the ledger squared, until no one who passes her stall will remember there was a woman there who waved them through the wet like a friend.

The rain is already thinning. You feel it in your skin the way you feel the string under your fingers before you touch it, the drum gone to patter gone to a hush that is almost nothing. Behind you the stallkeepers are murmuring, low, the sound of an ordinary night closing down. You do not turn. You look at the parcel with your name on it and the ledger with her name in it, two weights on the same quiet table, and the lamp burns steady and does not care which one you lift.

Your fingers move toward the knot and stop. There is a coin the night is asking. You have carried enough to know its shape.
