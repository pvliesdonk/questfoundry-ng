The rain comes soft to Vellamere, and where it pools the market unfolds. You have watched this a hundred times and it still catches you: stalls rising between puddles like breath fogging glass, lantern light bleeding gold into the wet cobbles, the aisles laid out in the reflection of a city that isn't quite the city. This is the hour the market keeps. It sells what people set down and never came back for. A first word. A mother's laugh. The weight of a hand that used to rest between your shoulders.

Your satchel hangs heavy at your hip, the strap gone butter-soft under years of your grip. Inside, other people's griefs are wrapped in brown paper and tied in the market's exact knot. You carry them the way you always have, aisle to aisle, buyer to seller, and you take nothing down from the shelves for yourself. That is the arrangement. That has always been the arrangement.

Emery's stall sits where it always sits, third along the lantern row, and she lifts a hand to wave you in from the rain the way she has for years. The gesture is the same. She is not.

Her face holds the lantern warmth still, but the edges of her have gone thin, translucent at the temple, at the line of her jaw, as though someone were slowly holding her up to the light. Her ink-stained fingers are not reaching for your parcels tonight. They are folding paper around small kept things. A cracked cup. A pressed flower. A ribbon whose color you cannot name and neither, you suspect, can she anymore.

"You came in from the wet," she says, and her voice is gentle, teasing, guarded. "Good. I hate to think of you out there drowning for other people's sorrows."

You set the satchel down. You do not know what to do with your hands.

"The market's called in a debt," she says, as if remarking on the weather. She keeps wrapping. "Mine, as it happens. Paid in the only coin I ever had spare." She does not look at you when she says it, and that is how you know the coin is this: being seen. Being known. The soft, unspoken thing between you that neither ever dared name.

Behind her, half tucked under the counter, a parcel waits. Brown paper gone damp. Your name on it, in a hand you almost recognize. The rain is already thinning.

Follow the lantern row deeper in, or slip through the aisle of grey silk.
