Outside the last of the awnings the rain has changed. It is no longer the drum against canvas that has been the whole ceiling of your nights, that muffled patter you learned to walk beneath without hearing. It is closer now. It comes down thin and cold and lands on the back of your hand, on your cheek, on the seam of your collar where the coat gaps, and each drop is its own small fact, arriving one at a time, spent as it touches you. You had forgotten rain could reach skin. You had spent so long being the thing between the wet and someone else's parcel that you stopped counting yourself among the things that could be rained on.

Against your chest the warmth stays. You feel it through the canvas, a small steady heat that does not thin the way the sky is thinning. It is not borrowed. No one wrapped it and no one is waiting at the end of an alley to take it from your hands. It is yours, and the knowing of that sits low and sure in you, heavier than you expected and lighter too, both at once, the way a held thing settles once you stop bracing against it.

The street opens ahead. It is the same street you have crossed a hundred times, the same cobbles slick and shining under a lamp that cannot decide whether to hold. But it looks wider now. The dark past the last stall is not the dark you hurried through with your head down, mapping it by the feel of the walls. It stands awake. It waits without menace, without promise, simply there, larger than one errand, larger than the small folded life you had made of yourself.

You draw a breath and it goes all the way in. Somewhere behind you a stall is closing that will not open again, and you do not turn back to it yet. You hold what is yours against the rain and you feel the night take you in as one of its own, no longer only a strap and a satchel and a pair of hands. The road forward is unlit and unhurried and it belongs to you as much as to anyone. You step into it. You go on holding, and the rain finds your face, and you let it.
