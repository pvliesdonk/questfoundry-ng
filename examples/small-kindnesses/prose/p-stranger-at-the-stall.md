The stall is where it has always been, and Emery is behind it, and she is thinner than the lamplight now, translucent at the edges the way steam goes when it leaves the cup. She is smiling at you. It is the smile she has always kept for you, and it does not know that you are no longer the one it was made for.

You come to the counter. Your hands find the ledge of it and rest there, because your hands have to be somewhere and the counter is what is near. She says a name. It is a bright small sound, warm at the front and worn smooth from use, and it lands on you and slides off, meaning nothing, meaning a stranger who happens to be standing where a friend once stood.

You nod as if you have understood. She takes it for shyness. She always did.

"You came through the wet," she says, and her thumb goes to the frayed hem of the shawl and worries it, back and forth, the way it does when she is glad and does not trust the gladness. "You always come through the wet."

You do not tell her that you cannot remember coming here for her. You do not tell her that the market kept the part of you that knew the way. The debt is settled. She stays. Vellamere keeps her lantern face and her ink-stained fingers and the cracked cup she wraps and rewraps, and it took the small change to buy that back out of you, and the change was every time she ever saw you plainly.

So you stand at her stall a stranger, and you have saved her, and she will never know it was you, because the you it was is gone.

The rain has stopped. The tarps let down their last slow beads. She keeps talking, gentle, teasing at the edges, filling the quiet you leave because you have always left it and she has always filled it. Against your chest the parcel rides, tied shut, still yours, still unopened, and your hands do not reach for it. You keep them on the counter. You keep looking at her.

You will remember this, at least. Not her knowing you. Only her, lit and lasting, teasing the frayed hem, safe on the dry side of a debt she cannot see. It is enough that she is here. You let it be enough. You lift your hand at last, and you wave, and she waves back, two strangers in the clearing light, and neither of you says the word for what this was.
