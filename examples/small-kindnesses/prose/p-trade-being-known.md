The clerk waits at the claiming-table with the brass scale between you, its two pans level in the lamplight. You do not have to say what you are offering. The market knows the shape of a debt before you name it, and it knows what you are holding out to it now, cupped in you where you have never let it be spent. Every time Emery waved you through the rain like something worth waving to. Every time she saved you the dry corner of her stall, the teasing tilt of her mouth, the way she said your name as if it belonged to a person and not a strap. All the small proofs that you had been seen.

You set them on the scale. It is not a gesture your hands make; it is one they stop making, a loosening, a letting the warmth go out of you into the cold indifferent tin. The pan sinks. The other rises. The clerk gives a small nod, courteous, and somewhere a ledger closes with the softness of a book that has always known its ending.

The last of the rain lets go of the tarps. It thins to nothing while you stand there, and the market holds its due, and it is done.

Emery is still here. Her edges have drawn back in from their fading; the lantern-warmth of her face has steadied, whole again, ink-stained fingers worrying the frayed hem of her shawl the way they do when a thing has come too near. She is safe. She is not going anywhere. She turns to you, and her mouth opens on relief, on thanks, on your name.

You wait to feel it land. It does not.

There is a woman before you in a kitchen-smelling shawl, gentle-faced, looking at you as though you are the whole reason the night turned kind. And you have no place to set the look. It goes into you and finds no hollow shaped to hold it. She is a stranger who knows you, and you are a stranger to the knowing, standing in the after-quiet with a warmth against your chest that is still yours and hands that have just paid out everything that ever made them familiar to her eyes.

You open your mouth. Nothing comes. You do not know how to be thanked by someone you cannot remember being seen by. Her lips move around the sounds of you, and you nod, because a nod is a thing hands and heads can do without knowing why, and the emptied pan of the scale gleams beside you, weightless now, spent.
