You do not decide. Your hands decide, the way they have always decided in the dark of a hundred alleys, and the string gives under your thumb in the market's own knot, the knot you could undo blind. It slips. It falls across the counter and lies there, a soft coil, spent.

The paper is damp enough to fold without a sound. You peel it back. You have opened a thousand parcels and never one addressed to you, and now the brown wing of it lifts away and the thing inside settles into your palms with a weight you were not braced for.

You know it at once. Not its name. Its shape. The exact ache of a want you have carried folded so small and so long you had made yourself believe it was not there, that you were only a strap and a satchel and a pair of hands for other people's grief. It is heavier than grief. It is warm the way a held thing is warm, warm the way her cup was warm across the counter through all these rains, and it is yours. You have never held anything that was yours.

Your thumbs move over it. Learning it. This is what you were afraid to want, and here it is, and it does not break you to hold it. It only fills your hands, which have never known what to do with themselves and now, for once, do.

She is still at her work behind you, closing a stall no one will open. The rain has thinned to almost nothing, a last bead loosening from the awning and letting go. You draw the paper closed again, gentle, keeping the warmth in. Not to hide it. To carry it.

You straighten. Something in you has changed weight and found its balance around the change. You are still the courier who knows every alley by feel. You are also, now, someone who wants, and who has kept the thing wanted, and who will walk out into the thinning dark holding it against your chest under the canvas coat where the rain cannot reach.

You go on. You hold the warm thing close, and you go on.
