Elara shook her head, looked at the Great Lens, and refused. She lifted the sea‑glass, its rune‑etched face cold as stone, set it upon the lantern’s crown, felt the weight press into her shoulders, and swore to guard the sleeping sea, and the oath hardened. The flame blazed. The barrier held, the Great Lens pressed hard against her spine, each rune hummed like a chain that bound the tide to stone, and she felt the weight of the sea settled upon her shoulders, and the resolve anchored. Dawn crept. She lowered the Lens into its brass cradle as the cartographer slipped ashore, his ink‑stained coat dripped sea mist, and distant shouts of the Coastguard rose over the tide, their hollow cries rode the wind like bruised gulls that howled. Marek vanished. Whispers of the Coastguard’s suspicion lingered in the howled wind, curled around the stone like cold breath, and Elara felt the irony of her oath weigh heavier than the tide itself, each gust reminded her of the eyes that watched. Night deepened. The wind carried the cartographer’s footsteps toward the cliff’s edge, his boots echoed on basalt as the Coastguard’s watch faded behind him, and the stone tower sighed, its lantern’s oil‑light trembled, while shadows stretched and the sea’s breath pressed.
