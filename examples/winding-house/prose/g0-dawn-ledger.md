Dawn strikes like a soft hammer on brass. The city answers in a deep tremor that climbs the ribs of the Clocktower and runs its minute teeth around the rim of your skull, a tenderness you learned to love because it means another hour kept. Beyond the walls the horizon does not move. It sits like a bruise that chose a shape and held it, the Halt’s quiet pressed flat against color. Bell teeth meet and kiss. You feel the torque in your bones and know whose hands keep the rhythm.

The shop smells of wool and cold tea. Dropped stitches cobble the floor like missed beats, and Mira’s wrists shine with thread bracelets that don’t remember how they were tied as the clerk mouths numbers and inks her losses into the public ledger with a skitter across parchment. She hums to follow the hour and loses it mid-note. You take the chit when he tears it free, the lark stamped into its surface cutting a small crescent into your palm, heat blooming where time is owed and cannot be kept. You say you will return it balanced, and she looks away so pride will not fray further at the edge.

Stairs wind you up in tight turns. Calder’s breath is even and smells of oil, and his voice sets facts like weights on a beam as you enter the tower’s heart where ribs of brass make a hollow chapel and the Sovereign Wind-Key waits on its velvet cradle with chains drooped like prayer. Duty, he says, is what keeps the walls from learning the Halt’s stillness; his patience wears certainty like a coat that never snags. The mainspring’s breath moves the air in slow pulses. The stones shudder, faint as a yawn resisting sleep. On the worktable the time-ledger wakes, pings Mira Threadgill’s deficit, and unfurls an addendum edged in red: authorized kin may transfer apprenticed years to stabilize an endangered worker, to be entered here. Calder’s seal sits already pressed in waiting wax as she sways on the stool, her fingers pricked and trembling, her afternoons slipping like loose bobbins to the floor. You count under your breath and feel the measure catch on every fifth tick, your own years gathered close like a drawstring. The corridor runs dim to the left, and to the right the pigeonwheel waits with its metal chatter held. The hinge of the moment holds.
