The corridor holds its breath. Lantern motes drift like slow ash, a fall of pale gold shorn from the lantern mouths that burn thin and sting the throat, and in their patient float the air takes on weight, as if your steps might score a groove you could read later with your fingertips. You stop and count. The tick arrives late, a reluctant tooth finding a rung after your fourth heartbeat and thinking about the fifth, so you press two fingers to your wrist and take the measure again while the tower’s ribs give a slow, iron sigh. The measure slips.

Heat from the lark-stamped chit prints through your glove, a small crescent of debt bright enough to smart, and you picture Mira’s wrists bright with thread that will not remember its pattern, her hum catching on a burr in the hour and coming away rubbed thin. You could pay. Years knot tight in your chest in the coil you were taught to keep, each loop a morning of clean sight you will need when gears bend truth and men lay weights with steady hands, and you feel how a single turn would loosen it until days unspool without grip. Boots sound far below. Somewhere a ledger wakes and tastes ink, and in the dim there is a curl of cold that could be the Halt’s slow breath or only winter testing the seams where work meets prayer.

The motes keep falling. Between reluctant ticks your heart drums its plain march and the count stretches, and in the drawn space you hear the pigeonwheel’s birds shift once in their hoods and then hold, as if even their letters would wait to learn which way you set your weight. You lift your hand. Ash-light clings to the skin, the crescent brightens, and the corridor narrows to a seam you could press or refuse, the hinge of a door holding under your palm.
