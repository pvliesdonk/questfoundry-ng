Your thumb finds the worn crescent. You brace your feet, feel the butt‑heavy pull settle into your wrist, and wrench the Sovereign Wind‑Key backward until the teeth unmesh with a hard, clean refusal that bites through bone. The suspension chain gives its single low chime, and the hungry coupling yanks free; air pours through the tower like summer blown from a shuttered room, warm and seeded with old polish and dust, and every pendulum in reach seems to breathe wider. The city slackens. The long‑sealed gates rasp, wood on iron, a slow unsealing that scratches your teeth, and the tower’s great lung lets years out in a hush you can taste. Calder staggers against the rail. He reaches, misses, the grit on his thumb leaving a dull smear along the brass as his certainty slips a notch, while Rook’s salt‑cold hand closes round your forearm and keeps your knees from folding under the change.

Through the mica‑thin face you catch the streets. People stand as if struck still, then inhale together, color flushing back into cloth and cheek as if a painter set down water and chose thicker pigment, and you feel it land as a soft weight across your collarbones. There—Threadgill Lane. Mira lifts her head at the seam table, the dented brass thimble kissing the same finger it always does; her stitches gather themselves and pull closed a tiny truth along a hem as the bells slip to a gentler pace that lays room between the notes. You steady yourself with your palm flat on the warm plate. You count in pairs, lose the fifth, find it again a beat late, and the lark‑stamped chit in your apron tugs as if to anchor you while the world slows and opens, the gates breathing their first cold into your skin.
