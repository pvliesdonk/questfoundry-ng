Brass breath and oil thicken. Each draw tastes of filed teeth and lamp-heat, and with the siphon’s throat pinned and true, the tower pulls itself a notch smaller, a cinch that runs from the Sovereign Wind‑Key’s stem into your shoulder where Orun Calder’s palm steadies and claims. Boots below catch the bell’s beat. Their measure multiplies in the streets, shutters pare light, and you hear the register’s dry rain begin again, a harvest of names gathered under the heading of what must be kept. The air presses, sweet with brass, heavy as a kept rule.

Your apron tugs. The lark chit in your pocket leans toward ink and circle, toward a line that stains the page around a woman’s hours, and a thin suction gathers behind your eyes where mornings cost. You do the counting. Her breath, your strength, the banked years sealed in the barrel’s drum, all weighed against streets that will not slow without a price, and the city speaks through the dogs and pins in a patient, metal grammar. You swallow brass.

Pins hold. You slide the collar cap home and feel the gasket bite, a small, obedient pain in your palm echoed by lanterned patrols crossing and recrossing the square as clerks net dissent and write it down as hazard to the common wind. The hatch seam chills your knuckles. You keep the Key braced a breath longer, then set it in its sling, and the mainspring settles into its long, hard hum that threads stair to square and tightens the city by one bright tooth. You stand the watch, beat steady against bone.
