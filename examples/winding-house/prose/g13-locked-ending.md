The square draws tight. Patrols cinch the Clocktower’s base, belts of boots and crossed lamp-cones sewing a seam no hand may pick, and the clerks’ quills raise a dry palisade of names under the narrow title of what will survive. They call you guardian, and the word fits like a strap under the ribs. Sanctioned drifts take Mira’s color again; her hum loses its refrain, the dented brass thimble kissing the same finger while her stitch wanders, and when she looks up it is as if the hour before has been rubbed thin. At the gate, Rook stands in sailcloth maps with one shoulder turned to the lintel. The watch sets poles and closes. He touches the tuning fork to iron and the dead note finds your teeth, then the doors send him back into the road like weather kept out by planed wood.

Households lose hours in quiet waves. You make the rounds with measures in your mouth, and the tower’s hum reads through your bones as you trim governors, snug escapements, take slack from belts until the lamps hold steady against the Halt and afternoons fold with a blink no one recalls owning. A child claps once and waits for the pair. A kettle cools unopened. Mira’s seam wrinkles where it should lie true, thread bracelets stilled as she stares at a hem that will not confess its beginning. Calder sets the ledger in your hands. It drags at your grip like wet wool, its calfskin warm from the lamp and his palm, the pumice grit on his thumb leaving a faint rasp on yours as he steadies the margin. You mouth numbers in pairs, pausing at the fifth, and you sign the tooth that takes, then the one that holds, while the city brightens by a narrow, merciless degree.

Night settles silk-slow. The avenues purr, strong and forgetful, shutters breathing in time as the wall lifts you into air that does not move. Beside you, Calder watches the still horizon, patient as a rule. The ledger sits between you like a brick of gathered weather. You feel its pull along the arm to the shoulder and deeper, a necessary weight you do not hand back. You set your fingers to the parapet, count, and keep the beat steady beside him, while the years go into the drum and do not return.
