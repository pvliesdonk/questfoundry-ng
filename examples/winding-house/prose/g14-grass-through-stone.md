You take up the Key. Its weight rides the bones of your palm like a tamped‑down bell, a familiar drag that steadies the new slack in the air while the tower’s breath goes wide. Orun Calder clings to the inner rail. His mouth works without its former metronome, and the fine grit on his cuffs looks suddenly like debt made visible, fretted into cloth by years he did not pay. Rook Ashfell watches. Her salt‑cold fingers had left a print on your forearm, a pale cuff you carry as you step into the stair’s slow spiral and down toward the unsealing that scrapes along your teeth.

At the foot, the gates have given. Wood sits against iron with a new, aching compromise, and through the mouth they make the Halt’s thin breath threads in, dry and clean as linen kept too long in shade. You step out. The courtyard, once kept tamped and swept by overseers with sand and oil, shows its forgetting in hairline fissures, and in each seam something patient has lifted a jaw. Grass rises. Not lush, not sudden, but a spoked insistence of green teeth testing where plates meet, prying up the tamp, counting by damp and day the places iron once ruled and now releases. You kneel. Between thumb and forefinger you feel a blade bend and spring, a tender click against skin that marks a register larger than any dial you have wound, and the taste of metal eases from your tongue. Behind you, the bells keep their slower talk. Ahead, the road shows its first shy whiskers of time and you reckon the cost in your own tendons, in Mira Threadgill’s steadier breath, in doors that will not close as fast as they once did. You breathe with it. The Key warms in your grasp, and the grass keeps counting at the seam.
