The bell-wind takes the stair. It rolls over the rail and into your chest with the memory of salt drying on a pier rope, of soot feathering a workroom lung, a sweetness that stings the split at your thumb where brass has thinned you to a crescent. It tastes like unlatched doors. It tastes paid for.

Below, the streets lean to it. You hear shingled pitches answer, low and sure, and the slack between bell strikes opens like a seam you can slide a needle through, each pause a measured kindness that comes dear. Cloth breathes, joints unstick. A cartwright rubs a wrist and lets his axle sing a slower note. On Threadgill, Mira lifts her face into the draft and you see the thimble kiss, the quick lay of her hand, the way color returns as if a dyer released it back from pawn; the wind takes the old smoke out of her hair and leaves room behind her eyes that was not there an hour ago. Freedom is sweet. It salts the bruise.

Rook’s grip holds, steady as a keel. You feel the cost in your elbow and in the space where the mainspring’s pull used to sit, a quiet vacancy with weight to it, and in the small tug at your apron chit that reminds you ledger and mercy always share a hinge. Calder steadies himself against a rib of brass and watches the gates grind, teeth meeting teeth, a long chew that opens the city’s mouth to the Halt’s cold breath. Air like riverlight slides under your collar. The great plates hum lower. A single mote hangs and turns once, then twice.

You draw one slow count. You set your boots to the stair, the bell-wind moving at your back like a hand that does not ask again, the world yawning and bright with rust and brine where the doors have given.
