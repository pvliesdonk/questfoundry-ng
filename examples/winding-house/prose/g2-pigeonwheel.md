The stair coughs you into the narrow loft. Air tastes of tin and stale seed, a skin of oil laid thin across the light. A clockwing pigeon erupts from the sill in a scatter of rivets and paper ash. It cuts close. Its metal pinions clatter a cadence too tidy for wildness, each slap a stamped coin paid to motion, each pause a tax. The beats hop your jaw. They find your teeth and roost there.

You steady the pigeonwheel with a palm. It hums. Beneath its rim the whole tower’s breath passes in long, even pulls, a patient tide that wants nothing but continuation, while your own pulse answers in little stutters that don’t quite fall where the tower asks them to, the misalignment a burr you can feel with your tongue. You taste brass. You taste the lark on the chit that cut your palm at dawn and left a crescent’s ache, the ledger’s bite still warm where promise met skin. You think of Mira’s wrists and their pale bracelets, of afternoons that slide, of hours you could loosen from your store and push across the table, clean and counted, or keep hoarded like grain against a winter no one has named aloud. Your grip tightens on the rim until your knuckles pale.

The bird wheels once, quick as a key turned in an unseen lock. It climbs into the slatted light and is gone, but the clatter remains, a faint combing in the bones of your face. You close your mouth to hold it still long enough to hear its measure, and the pressure settles like a penny laid on the tongue.
