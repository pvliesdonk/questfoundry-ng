Wind saws the alley thin. You step into it and feel the count change, a hitch between fourth and fifth that makes you catch your breath and pair it again. Rook is there before the door can bite him. He slips you sideways where the wall keeps its own quiet, coat stitched with pale map-lines that blink and vanish when the gusts fold the cloth. Salt and cold metal lean from him. His voice is the low tone a key makes before it seats. Theft, he names it, not balance. Hours lifted clean from wrists that never offered, ground fine to grease a gear that should be cleaned another way. He touches the tuning fork to the air and it thrums though there is no bell, a straight note in the dead space that makes the alley’s trash hiss. There is a way to meet the Halt, he says, to let the city spin down without going out, to share the pull with what waits past the walls. It will cost. It will not cost like this. You feel the lark-stamped chit heat against your palm, and the neat mercy you weighed becomes a hand in the drawer where the ledger’s bite is kept.

The workshop swallows you whole. It smells of oil made patient by dust. Calder waits with a tray of escapements laid like thin bones, each tooth cut to a temper you could taste if you dared. He names three for your ear and sets the calipers in your grip. Good work, he says, your hand is true. You will tune them to sing as one. You nod. You count in pairs and let the fifth catch in your throat so the burr shows itself, and his approval lands with a clean click that does not open anything. Waste nothing, he adds, apprentices who squander hours wash out. The words settle like lead in a weight-pan. His palm rests once at your nape and lifts, light as a thread that snags and holds. The tray waits. The teeth quiver. The leash tightens across the soft of your wrist where pulse keeps score.
