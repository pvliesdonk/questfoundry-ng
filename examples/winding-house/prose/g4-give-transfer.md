The ledger waits. You wet your thumb in the cold time-ink, iron and dusk on the tongue of it, and touch the line where her name sits. You sign in the shop hand you learned before your wrists could lift a mainspring, one neat curl, then the next, breathing one-two, one-two, catching on five. The numerals jump like teeth taking a ratchet. The seal lowers and bites. The bite climbs your arm and nests behind your ribs where your count keeps house.

Mira’s face fills, a wash of rose under the paper pale, and the drift in her gaze knots itself steady. Her stitches go straight as a plumb line. Your hands shake. The day blurs at the edges as if oil smeared the glass, and a name you meant to speak slides away into the felt somewhere under the bench. She sees it. She hums the loom tune she half-remembers and lays her palm over your knuckles until the tremor settles to a tick. You hold each other in the doorway that hums with the tower’s long pulse, wood and brass agreeing for once, her thread bracelets warm against your wrist while your breath pairs and snags and pairs again. The light outside tilts. Weight shifts to the side where you signed.

A red tab on the ledger’s spine springs up with a dry whisper. Somewhere behind the wall a wire sings and carries word uphill. You feel the street notice. Blue coats turn their heads as you step out, glossy cuffs bright as trapped rain, and their eyes begin to walk the route you take by habit between the shop and the Clocktower, heel marks counted, corners kept. Pencils tap on slates. Above, in the place where the city’s tempo is set, a mind you know like a caliper’s narrow bite sharpens to a point that wants measuring. You square your shoulders and start the walk with the new weight humming under your skin.
