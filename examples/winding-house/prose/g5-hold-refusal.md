You set the quill down. Ink beads at the nib like a held breath while you draw your hand back and keep your wrist from trembling, and in the smell of paper and oil you say, quiet as a ratchet taking, that you need your hours to pry at larger gears. Mira nods. Her mouth keeps the shape of a hum without sound, and her eyes lose a tooth in the train, light slipping one notch lower. The clerk stamps. DECLINED lands in red, a blunt tooth striking the plate, and the lark on your chit warms through cloth as if it could see what you have kept and why.

Thread wavers. By afternoon her needle nicks the air and finds only cloth by rumor, and when she reaches for your name she touches blank canvas, a missed hole in a hem where speech should catch. Whispers chip. At the pump and under shutters you hear them grind fine, your coldness weighed like scrap in a palm, and you file each word without looking up because turning would cost more than you have to spend today. The bell thins. Its tongue swings and the sound comes from a lane away, a watched pot of air that never quite breaks, and the minutes slacken as if a spring were eased a fraction.

You work. Steel kisses stone and lifts clean burrs, your counting held in paired steps, fifth caught, sixth released, attention honed to the tooth-mesh you will need for the climb and the lock that waits where the high gears breathe. You waste nothing. Pivots bathe, plates dry, pinions seat with a sound you feel in bone, and when the hand shakes you pinch the crescent at your thumb and let patience settle like a weight on a cord. Your ledger lies plain. Entries march in small neat teeth, no odd gaps, no bright flags for an official’s eye, a dusk-colored line of days that says only worked, balanced, returned, while your edge stays whetted under the apron’s seam.
